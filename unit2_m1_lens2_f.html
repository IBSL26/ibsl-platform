Interrogative Anchor · Before defining Objectives and Key Results, the leadership team must examine current operating reality in relation to the Success in Practice narrative.

1.1.2 — KISS: Keep · Improve · Start · Stop
4 Reflective Filters
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Understand the four KISS filters and what each asks the organisation to examine
  • Distinguish between a specific KISS reflection and a vague aspiration
  • Apply each filter to the SiP — not to the organisation in general
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.1.2

Directing through the four sub-accordions: Expand KEEP, IMPROVE, START, and STOP one at a time. For each, ask participants to give a live example from their own function — anchored to the SiP. This prevents the exercise from becoming abstract.

The specificity standard: Draw attention to the existing Facilitator Move note in this section — the difference between "improve communication" and "improve cross-functional handover time from 72 to 24 hours." Reinforce this standard before the Application tab exercise begins.

STOP — the hardest filter: Give this extra time. Ask — "What is one thing your function is currently doing that actively contradicts the future we described? What would it take to stop it?" Silence after this question is productive. Let it run.

Suggested time: 8–10 minutes.

The Strategy2Results® process introduces a reflective step designed to answer this question. The SiP statement is passed through a simple but powerful filter called KISS — Keep, Improve, Start, Stop.

Each element asks leaders to examine the organisation in relation to the future state described in the SiP narrative:

◆ KEEP — Protect what is already working▾

Identifies the practices, capabilities, and behaviours that are already moving the organisation toward the SiP and should therefore be protected and reinforced. These are strategic assets that must not be disrupted in the pursuit of change.

◆ IMPROVE — Strengthen what is partially working▾

Highlights areas where the organisation is already moving in the right direction but where performance, coordination, or capability must strengthen to support the SiP more fully. These are often the highest-leverage interventions.

◆ START — Introduce what does not yet exist▾

Identifies new practices, capabilities, or initiatives that do not yet exist but will be necessary for the organisation to operate in the way described in the SiP. These represent genuine strategic gaps.

◆ STOP — Eliminate what contradicts the SiP▾

Surfaces activities, routines, or commitments that consume energy and resources but do not contribute to the future organisation described in the SiP. Stopping is often harder than starting — and more strategically valuable.

Critical principle: Every KISS reflection must be anchored to the Success in Practice statement. Leaders are not evaluating the organisation in general terms — they are evaluating it against the future organisation they have collectively described.

Facilitator Move

When leaders generate KISS items, insist on specificity. "Improve communication" is not a KISS reflection — "Improve cross-functional handover time from 72 hours to 24 hours" is. Push for the operating reality, not the aspiration.

1.1.4 — OKR Anatomy: From Intent to Traction
Structure & Formula
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Distinguish between an Objective and a Key Result
  • Apply the Key Result formula: Verb of Change + Metric + From X to Y + By Deadline
  • Identify the difference between a Key Result (outcome) and a task (activity)
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.1.4

Directing through the OKR anatomy: Walk through the two panels (Objectives and Key Results) side by side. Ask participants: "Think of a goal your team set in the last 12 months. Was it an Objective, a Key Result, or a task disguised as one of these?"

The Key Result Construction Guide: Expand the sub-accordion and walk through all four elements with participants. Then give a live example — ask a participant to state a goal they are working toward, and workshop it through the formula in real time. One concrete example does more than ten theoretical ones.

The critical distinction to land: Draw specific attention to the red box — outcomes versus activities. Ask: "Can you check it off a task list? If yes — it's a task, not a Key Result." This single test is the most useful tool in the room. Reinforce it every time a task-disguised-as-KR appears in the session.

Suggested time: 8–10 minutes.

Objective Key Results (OKRs) translate the identified changes into measurable progress.

Objectives

Define strategic ambition. Must be: Qualitative · Ambitious · Action-Oriented · Inspirational · Memorable. If you cannot wear it on a T-shirt, it is not worth pursuing.

Key Results

Define measurable progress. Formula: Verb of Change + Metric + From X to Y + By Deadline. Example: Increase digital transaction completion rate from 40% to 75% by Q4 2026.

Key Result Construction Guide — 4 Elements▾
Verb of Change

Indicates the direction of improvement. E.g.: Increase, Reduce, Achieve, Improve, Maintain.

Metric

A clear and measurable indicator. E.g.: Customer satisfaction score, cycle time, adoption rate.

From X to Y

Defines the baseline and target. E.g.: From 40% to 75%. Always establish a starting point.

Deadline

Specifies when the result must be achieved. E.g.: By Q4 2026, By end of FY.

Critical Distinction: OKRs define outcomes, not activities. If a statement can be checked off a task list — "launch new platform" or "run customer survey" — it is not a Key Result. Those are activities that support the Key Result, not the result itself. Key Results describe the impact those activities must produce.

The framework is rarely the problem. The problem is that organisations try to measure progress before they have had an honest conversation about where they are actually standing.

Fragmented Efforts

The Activity Trap

People default to measuring busyness because they are not sure how to prove they are achieving real outcomes.

Essentially, the organisation starts measuring the finish line before it has mapped the terrain.

Panoramic Enterprise OKR Prioritisation

Once initial OKRs have been drafted, the leadership team determines which Objectives deserve enterprise focus now. Strategy execution fails when too many priorities are pursued simultaneously.

Each team member reviews proposed Objectives and votes on which represent the most critical strategic movements at this moment.

The Less is More Rule: No more than 4 Enterprise Objectives. No more than 3 Key Results per Objective. This constraint forces explicit trade-offs, ensuring execution energy is concentrated rather than dispersed.

The Prioritisation Matrix: Impact versus Effort

The matrix evaluates initiatives along two dimensions: Impact — the degree of strategic value delivered if achieved — and Effort — the time, resources, coordination, and organisational change required.

The leadership team must pay particular attention to initiatives in Strategic Catalysts, Accelerated Enablers, and Core Strategic Drivers — these represent the highest leverage execution signals.
