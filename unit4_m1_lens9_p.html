ESRG Alignment — The Grounding Question

Enterprise alignment is one of the most decisive success factors in strategy execution. Performance is not sustained by strategy alone — it is sustained by the organisational system: the environment people work in, the systems they rely on, the resources they are given, and the governance structures that guide decisions.

Why ESRG Alignment Drives Sustained Execution

How each domain removes a specific category of friction — and two worked examples.

The ESRG Ignition Point

Enable the Work — Physical & Behavioural

Values that are merely stated do not enable performance. Values that are demonstrable, measured, and enforced create a behavioural environment where conduct is as consequential as results. The Cultural Operating Code translates values into named, actionable norms — moving values from aspiration to operation. Psychological safety completes the enabling condition.

Streamline the Flow

Systems provide pathways through which information, tasks, and decisions move. When clear and reliable, teams can see where work sits, how it progresses, and what action is required. Streamlined systems make execution visible — and visibility is the precondition for accountability.

Resource the Strategy

Teams must possess the skills required, the capacity to sustain effort, and the tools to operate efficiently. Burnout degrades execution quality, accelerates talent exit, and erodes institutional knowledge that is often the organisation's most critical and least recoverable asset.

Govern the Rhythm

Governance provides the structure maintaining the rhythm of decisions, reviews, and accountability. The governance system must apply standards consistently regardless of seniority — leaders exempt from accountability destroy governance credibility faster than any single operational failure.

"When all four conditions operate together, execution no longer depends on leadership attention. The organisation’s operating system reinforces the work itself. That is Grounding."

Worked Example 1 — The IBSL Cultural Operating Code
6 values · 6 norms

IBSL’s values: Intentionality · Integrity · Collaboration · Accountability · Excellence · Human-Centricity

Intentionality
Leading with purpose, clarity, and discipline
AIM
▼
Act with purpose · Interrogate assumptions · Maintain the discipline

Intentionality is not enthusiasm — it is the discipline of knowing exactly why you are doing what you are doing, and holding that line when conditions make it inconvenient.

Reflect: Before your last three major commitments — could you articulate the strategic purpose, the assumption you were making, and the standard you held yourself to?
Integrity
Consistency between words, behaviour, and outcomes
SAY
▼
Say what you mean · Act on what you say · Yield the truth regardless of consequence

Integrity is about consistency between word, behaviour, and outcome — even when telling the truth is uncomfortable. What we say and what we do are the same thing. Always.

Reflect: In your last performance review — did you name what was promised, what was delivered, and what the gap cost — before being asked?
Collaboration
Leveraging diversity of thought and collective intelligence
ONE
▼
Own a shared outcome · Navigate difference with respect · Execute as a unified force

Collaboration is not a meeting. It is the decision to produce something together that no single function could produce alone. The result belongs to the team.

Reflect: In your last cross-functional review — did you present the shared outcome, or only your function’s contribution?
Accountability
Owning results and reinforcing transparency
OAR
▼
Own it · Act on it · Report it

Accountability begins the moment a commitment is made. We do not manage impressions — we report the truth and fix the gap.

Reflect: When did you last surface a gap before being asked — with the cause, the impact, and the recovery action already identified?
Excellence
Upholding rigour, learning, and measurable impact
SET
▼
Set the standard · Earn the evidence · Transform through learning

Excellence is a daily discipline. Not about being the best in the room — about being better than last quarter, and proving it with evidence.

Reflect: At your last review — could you answer: What standard did we set? What evidence do we have? What have we changed as a result?
Human-Centricity
Placing people at the core of leadership and execution
SEE
▼
See the person, not just the role · Enable growth deliberately · Ensure dignity in every interaction

Leadership serves people — not the other way around. We invest in growth. We protect dignity. We never reduce a person to their output.

Reflect: In your last performance conversation — did you open with the person before moving to the result?

Extrapolating · Where

Where Are the ESRG Friction Zones?

Recognising the predictable friction patterns in each domain.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Identify the predictable friction patterns for each ESRG domain
  • Distinguish physical environment friction from behavioural environment friction
  • Recognise friction as organisational intelligence — not failure, but signal
  • Connect specific friction patterns to the type of intervention required
E — Environment Friction
Physical Friction

Teams asked to work in new ways — cross-functional, faster, hybrid — yet workspaces still reflect older patterns. The execution cost is real and largely invisible in any performance report.

Behavioural Friction

When stated values are not translated into observable behaviours and not embedded in performance measurement, they function as decoration. The absence of a Cultural Operating Code means cultural expectations remain implicit — interpreted differently by different people at different seniority levels. That inconsistency is friction.

When psychological safety fails, execution issues surface too late. The organisation loses its most valuable early warning system: the willingness of people to tell the truth about what is actually happening.

✎ Reflection
S — Systems Friction

Workarounds invented to keep work moving become, over time, the de facto system. Data integrity is not a technical problem — it is an execution problem. When leaders spend more time debating the accuracy of information than interpreting what it means, systems are failing execution.

✎ Reflection
R — Resources Friction

The assumption of readiness is itself a source of friction. Burnout is a strategic risk — it degrades execution quality, accelerates talent exit, and erodes institutional knowledge. The absence of a signal that workloads are unsustainable is not evidence that all is well. It is evidence that psychological safety is insufficient. Strategy follows resources: where the money goes is where the organisation’s real priorities reside.

✎ Reflection
G — Governance Friction

Governance structures designed to control risk can begin to slow execution speed as strategy evolves. Selective enforcement — when high performers are exempt from accountability by virtue of results — delegitimises the governance system. A governance system that lacks legitimacy cannot sustain the execution discipline the strategy requires.

Choose Your Value Set

Select one of the three value sets. Work through three values from your chosen set. Choose the three that feel most relevant or most challenging to translate. Present your strongest single norm to the full group.

Build the COC — Three Steps Per Value

Step 1 — Define the behaviour: What would a leader who genuinely lives this value do differently from one who only states it?

Step 2 — Build the acronym: Take the first letter of three action words. Each must be an observable action — a verb the leader performs, not a quality they possess.

Step 3 — Write the norm statement: One sentence specific enough that a new employee on Day 1 would know exactly what behaviour will be recognised and what will be corrected.

The COC Challenge — Present Your Strongest Norm
Test 1 — The New Employee Test

"If a new employee read this norm on their first day, would they know exactly what behaviour will be recognised and what will be corrected?"

Test 2 — The Pressure Test

"Does this norm hold when a senior leader violates it? Or does it only apply to junior staff?"

Test 3 — The Mirror Test

"Would every person in this room be comfortable being assessed against this norm in their own 360-degree feedback?"

"A Cultural Operating Code only becomes real the moment a leader uses it in a difficult conversation. Which norm from today will you use in your next difficult conversation — and when?"

Micro-Climb Summary — ESRG Alignment
Lens 9 synthesis
Awareness — The What

ESRG Alignment examines the four domains that collectively determine whether performance can be sustained. The Cultural Operating Code is the most important output of the Environment domain — values translated into named, acronym-based norms that become the behavioural architecture of the organisation.

Intelligence — The Why

Each domain removes a specific category of friction. The ignition point of Grounding is when all four work together — as a coherent operating system that reinforces the strategy rather than resisting it. At that point execution no longer depends on heroic individual effort.

Extrapolating — The Where

Execution friction is not random. Values misalignment and psychological safety failures in E; workarounds and data fragmentation in S; capability gaps and unsustainable workloads in R; selective accountability and absent leadership in G. These patterns are predictable — and therefore preventable.

Integration Zone — The How

ESRG friction rarely stays within a single function. The Execution Muscle Scan transforms individual observations into collective intelligence. The Enterprise ESRG Alignment Agenda is a leadership instrument for proactive execution architecture — not a complaint register.

Grounding is where strategy execution is sustained. The environment must enable. The systems must streamline. The resources must sustain. The governance must discipline. When all four are working together — the organisation does not just execute its strategy. It becomes capable of executing the next one, and the one after that.

That is not a management competency. That is an organisational superpower.
