Session Overview

The final lens of the S2R® programme. Orientation, facilitation questions, tone guidance, and session structure.

◆ Lens Intent

This lens answers the most important question in organisational performance: not how to achieve results, but how to sustain them. The central argument is precise — sustained performance is not a leadership attitude or a motivational commitment. It is a navigation discipline applied continuously to five force categories that never stop moving.

The architecture of this lens is causal. Awareness introduces Performance Gravity and KISS. Intelligence shows that the three mechanisms of Performance Gravity are each the direct consequence of a specific force category going unnavigated. Extrapolating gives leaders the WHERE (the Alignment Brigade across leadership domains), the HOW TO READ (ABCV as the navigation diagnostic), and the HOW TO RESPOND (KISS as the navigation action). Integration Zone embeds this as a collective quarterly rhythm. Application practises the full chain in real leadership domains.

This lens is industry-neutral and programme-independent. The forces it describes exist in every organisation in every sector every day. The navigation disciplines apply whether or not an organisation has engaged in any formal programme.

◆ Key Facilitation Questions
  • “Name a time your organisation achieved strong performance and then lost it. Looking back — which force had moved that you were not tracking?”
  • “Which of the five force categories is your organisation least systematically monitoring right now — and what would be the consequence of missing its movement for six months?”
  • “When a competitive signal appears — a shift in win rates, a change in customer conversation — what is the first question your leadership team asks? Is it strategic or operational?”
  • “Apply ABCV to the most significant force movement in your environment right now. Which of the four MBT conditions is most under threat?”
  • “Which KISS discipline is your organisation consistently avoiding — and what is the honest reason for that?”
◆ Tone & Watch Points

This lens must feel permanent, not terminal. It is not the conclusion of a journey. It is the opening of a permanent discipline. Every facilitation choice should reinforce: this is how high-performing organisations operate every day, not what they do at the end of a programme.

Watch for: leaders who agree conceptually with all five forces but cannot name one specific force movement currently threatening their strategy. Push to specifics: “Name the force. Name where it is moving. Name what it has invalidated.”

The ABCV connection is the pivot point of this lens. Participants encountered ABCV as an OKR stress-test in Lens 3. Here it is elevated to its full strategic purpose as the navigation diagnostic. Make this elevation explicit: “ABCV was introduced to test the soundness of your strategic commitments. Here we use it to test whether those commitments still hold as forces move.”

The Application exercise runs the full chain. Do not allow it to become a KISS exercise alone. Each scenario names a force, requires an ABCV diagnostic, then activates KISS. Leaders who skip the ABCV step are responding without diagnosing.

Awareness · What

Performance Gravity — and the KISS Navigation Disciplines

Understanding the force that pulls organisations back toward average — and the four precise decisions that counter it.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Understand Performance Gravity as the consequence of unnavigated force movements — not as an internal leadership failure
  • Distinguish the three mechanisms of Performance Gravity and their force origins
  • Articulate the four KISS disciplines as the navigation response to Performance Gravity
  • Apply KISS at the individual and collective level as a permanent operating rhythm
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Awareness

Open with the central reframe: Performance Gravity is not a motivation problem or a leadership character issue. It is the predictable organisational consequence of five external forces moving while the organisation stays still. This reframe matters because it shifts the conversation from blame to navigation. The question is not “why did we drift?” but “which force moved that we stopped tracking?”

When introducing the three mechanisms, note that each one is anchored to a specific force category — this is the bridge to Intelligence. Do not develop the force connections in depth here; Awareness introduces the mechanisms, Intelligence explains the causality.

KISS is fully introduced and defined in this section. It does not need to appear in full depth in any subsequent section. When KISS appears in Extrapolating and Application, it is always as the navigation response — the action, not the definition.

Opening — The Navigation Premise

The forces in your operating environment never stop moving. They moved before your strategy was designed. They are moving now. And they will continue moving regardless of what your performance indicators show, what your strategy says, or what your leadership team has decided.

Sustained performance is not the reward for strong results. It is the outcome of continuous navigation — the deliberate, disciplined practice of reading the forces that shape the environment in which you perform and adjusting your position in response to their movement.

Most organisations treat sustained performance as a stability problem. They assume that if they maintain what they are currently doing, results will continue. The assumption is wrong. The environment in which your current results were earned is already changing. And the gap between your current approach and the current environment will only become visible when the cost of correction is already significant.

Performance Gravity is what happens when forces move and the organisation does not move with them. It is not a failure of effort. It is a failure of navigation. And like physical gravity, it operates continuously — whether or not the organisation is aware of it.

The Three Mechanisms of Performance Gravity
Each produced by a specific force

Performance Gravity operates through three distinct mechanisms. Each mechanism is the organisational consequence of a specific category of external force going unnavigated. Intelligence examines each force category in depth. Here we introduce the mechanisms and their force origins.

Mechanism 1
Momentum Illusion
The enterprise competes with a playbook the market has already moved past
Force that produces this: Competitive + Disruptive
▼

When competitive and disruptive forces shift and the organisation does not track them, the organisation continues running the approach that once produced results — unaware that the landscape in which those results were earned has already changed. Results remain acceptable in the short term because momentum carries forward. The gap between the playbook and the market widens invisibly until it becomes undeniable — at which point the cost of correction is significantly higher than earlier navigation would have required.

Mechanism 2
Complexity Creep
Structural responses to old conditions accumulate permanently as organisational weight
Force that produces this: Macro-environmental
▼

When macro-environmental forces — regulatory frameworks, economic conditions, political dynamics, technological infrastructure — shift over time, organisations build structural responses to each change. Each response is justified at the time of its creation. Collectively, across multiple macro-environmental shifts, they create an organisation that is heavier, slower, and more internally focused than its current environment requires. The processes, governance layers, and approval structures built for yesterday’s macro-environment become permanent fixtures even after the conditions that justified them have changed.

Mechanism 3
Execution Regression
The organisation reverts to its historical operating pattern across all mechanisms simultaneously
Force that produces this: Internal forces
▼

Every organisation has a cultural gravity — a set of norms, habits, assumptions, and identity patterns reinforced over years of operating history. This internal gravity does not pause when a new strategy is set or when strong results are achieved. It continuously pulls the organisation’s behaviour back toward its historical pattern. When internal forces are not actively navigated, Execution Regression sets in: the enterprise reverts to the operating pattern that predates its current strategic direction. Standards quietly return to pre-programme baselines. Decision habits revert. The gap between what the strategy says and what daily behaviour reflects widens until the two are no longer recognisably connected.

The fourth force category — Social and Environmental forces — produces a distinct fourth consequence when unnavigated: Legitimacy Erosion. Stakeholder expectations shift while enterprise behaviour remains anchored to what once earned approval. This is examined in depth in Intelligence.

KISS — The Four Navigation Disciplines
The response to Performance Gravity

KISS provides the four precise navigation decisions every leadership team must make at the close of every performance cycle. These are not aspirational commitments. They are operational decisions — specific, named, and accountable. They apply in every industry, in every quarterly review, permanently.

K
KEEP
What is working and must be actively protected
▼

The discipline of Keep is the conscious decision to identify, name, and protect the practices, routines, enterprise behaviours, and systems that are producing reliable results. Effective practices are vulnerable — they depend on specific leadership attention, team behaviour, and structural support. When that attention is redirected, the practice degrades without announcement. Keep requires leaders to make the implicit explicit: what specifically is producing this result, and what must remain in place to ensure it continues? Keep is the direct antidote to Momentum Illusion.

I
IMPROVE
What has shifted and requires recalibration
▼

The discipline of Improve is the honest recognition that something which was working well is beginning to work less well — and that the gap between current performance and required performance is growing slowly enough to ignore but fast enough to be dangerous. Improve is the discipline of early correction: addressing drift when it is still a signal rather than waiting until it becomes a crisis. Every organisation has practices that were excellent six months ago and are merely adequate today. Improve prevents adequate from becoming insufficient before the gap is visible in results.

S
STOP
What is harming performance and must be eliminated
▼

The discipline of Stop is the most difficult and most neglected of the four. Stop requires leaders to identify and eliminate practices, meetings, processes, reporting requirements, and approval layers consuming organisational energy without producing commensurate value. The resistance to Stop is structural: every practice that should be stopped has a sponsor, a team, and stakeholders whose interests are served by its continuation. Stop is the direct antidote to Complexity Creep — the accumulation of structural responses to conditions that no longer exist.

S
START
What must be introduced to sustain the next level of performance
▼

The discipline of Start is the forward-facing investment that sustained performance requires. Start asks: what is the organisation not yet capable of that the changing force environment will require? It demands that leaders examine emerging strategic requirements, internal capability gaps, and governance evolution needs before the environment makes them urgent. Start is the anticipatory discipline that distinguishes organisations that sustain performance from those that merely extend it temporarily. It is the investment in future fitness that current performance makes possible.

KISS is not a reflection template. It is a navigation discipline. In Extrapolating, it becomes the response mechanism of the navigation chain: Forces detected → ABCV reveals which strategic assumption has been invalidated → KISS determines the specific response.

Intelligence · Why

Why the Five Forces Produce Performance Gravity When Unnavigated

The causal relationship between force movement and organisational performance decline — across industries, in daily execution, permanently.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Understand each of the five force categories as a permanent feature of every organisation’s operating environment
  • Trace the precise causal link between each force category and the mechanism of Performance Gravity it produces
  • Recognise Success Inertia as the total navigation failure — all five forces moving while the organisation stays still
  • Apply the causal understanding to diagnose which force is most active in their organisation right now
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Intelligence

This section establishes the causal architecture that makes this lens distinctive. The argument is not that performance is hard to sustain because leadership is difficult. The argument is that performance is hard to sustain because forces are always moving — and the specific way each force moves produces a specific and predictable consequence when not navigated.

Work through each force slowly. For each one, ask participants to name a specific movement in their operating environment right now — not hypothetically but actually. The force categories are not theoretical. They are present and active in every organisation’s environment at this moment.

The causal table is the centrepiece of this section. After working through all five forces, return to it. Ask: “Which row describes your organisation’s most urgent navigation challenge right now?” The answer determines which part of Extrapolating deserves the most attention in their context.

Success Inertia is not a separate concept — it is what happens when all five rows of the causal table are true simultaneously. It is the total navigation failure. Make this visceral: name organisations that experienced it. The pattern is always the same.

The Five Forces — Causal Analysis

Competitive + Disruptive Forces → Momentum Illusion

In daily execution, competitive and disruptive forces do not announce themselves. They move in the background of every commercial decision, every customer conversation, every quarterly review. A competitor repositions. A new entrant captures a segment the organisation considered low-value. A technology application begins solving the same customer problem in a different way — at lower cost, with greater convenience — at the margins of the market.

None of these movements appear as crises on the day they begin. They appear as gradual shifts: win rates that move slightly, customer acquisition costs that increase incrementally, renewal conversations that feel subtly different. Organisations navigating these forces read those signals in real time. Organisations that are not explain each shift as temporary noise — until the pattern is undeniable.

Daily Execution Signal

Gradual shifts in win rates, customer acquisition costs, and renewal conversations. Competitors responding faster to market changes. Customers referencing alternatives that did not exist in last year’s review.

Navigation Discipline

Continuous competitive monitoring embedded in the operational rhythm. The question in every commercial review is not “how did we perform?” but “what has moved in our competitive and disruptive landscape since we last looked — and does our approach still match the landscape as it is now?”

When unnavigated produces: Momentum Illusion. The organisation is running a playbook that once produced results in a competitive and disruptive landscape that has since moved. The results still appear acceptable. The playbook is already obsolete.

Macro-Environmental Forces → Complexity Creep

Macro-environmental forces — economic cycles, regulatory frameworks, political dynamics, and technological infrastructure — are experienced as the background conditions that constrain or enable every operational decision. These forces are often slow-moving, which makes them easy to discount. But their cumulative effect on the definition of good performance is enormous.

The characteristic danger of macro-environmental forces is accumulation. A regulation tightened three years ago, another two years ago, a third last year — none individually disruptive, together a fundamentally different operating constraint. The organisation has adapted to each change incrementally, building a structural response each time, without stepping back to examine the cumulative weight of all responses together.

Daily Execution Signal

Processes and governance layers that no one can explain the current purpose of. Approval requirements that pre-date three regulatory cycles. Budget structures built for economic conditions that no longer exist. Committees that were formed in response to a specific event and never dissolved.

Navigation Discipline

Quarterly macro-environmental scanning at the leadership level. The question is not “what is happening in the macro environment?” but “which of our current structural responses was built for a macro condition that has now changed — and should therefore be stopped?”

When unnavigated produces: Complexity Creep. Old structural responses to old conditions accumulate permanently as organisational weight. The macro environment has changed. The structures have not.

Social + Environmental Forces → Legitimacy Erosion

Social and environmental forces operate simultaneously across every stakeholder relationship the organisation maintains. Customer expectations about how organisations behave — not just what they produce — evolve continuously. Employee expectations about purpose, fairness, and leadership conduct shift generationally and in response to social events that have nothing to do with the organisation directly. Investor expectations about governance, environmental impact, and social accountability have moved from peripheral to central in most sectors.

The critical feature of these forces is that they operate through perception and legitimacy rather than through operational constraint. An organisation can be operationally excellent and commercially strong while its legitimacy is eroding — because stakeholder expectations have moved and enterprise behaviour has not. The erosion is invisible in operational metrics until it surfaces as recruitment difficulty, customer advocacy decline, regulatory scrutiny, or investor concern.

Daily Execution Signal

Recruitment conversations where candidates ask questions about values and practices that were not asked three years ago. Customers raising expectations in account reviews that feel different from previous cycles. Investor questions about governance and disclosure that are more pointed than historical norms. Regulatory attention before any formal requirement changes.

Navigation Discipline

Continuous stakeholder intelligence that reads emerging expectations — not current demands. Expectation leads formal demand by twelve to eighteen months in most sectors. The Start discipline of KISS is most directly activated here: what must the organisation begin building now in its practices, disclosures, governance, and enterprise behaviour before the expectation becomes a non-negotiable requirement?

When unnavigated produces: Legitimacy Erosion. Enterprise behaviour remains anchored to what once earned stakeholder approval while expectations have moved. The organisation is not behaving badly by its own historical standards. It is simply no longer meeting the current standards of its stakeholder universe.

Internal Forces → Execution Regression

Every organisation has a cultural gravity — a set of norms, habits, assumptions, and enterprise behaviour patterns that have been reinforced over years or decades of operating history. This internal gravity is not neutral. It exerts continuous force on every decision, every meeting, every performance conversation, every escalation. It pulls the organisation toward its historical pattern regardless of what the strategy says or what the leadership team has committed to.

Internal forces are unique among the five because they operate entirely within the organisation’s own boundaries. They cannot be read in a market report or a regulatory update. They must be read in the daily observable behaviour of the enterprise — in how decisions are actually made versus how the governance structure says they should be made, in what actually gets discussed in leadership forums versus what appears on the agenda, in which conversations happen in the room and which happen afterwards.

Daily Execution Signal

The gap between what the strategy says and what daily decisions, meetings, and enterprise behaviours actually reflect. Review conversations that feel operationally busy but strategically vague. Accountability standards applied unevenly. The language of strategic direction disappearing from operational discussions.

Navigation Discipline

Continuous reading of the internal force field through structured diagnostics — the Organisational Health Thermometer, the ACE-IT coaching model, and the KISS reflection applied as permanent executive operating disciplines. The question is not “are we implementing a programme?” but “is the organisation’s daily enterprise behaviour matching the strategic direction, or are internal forces pulling it back toward the historical pattern?”

When unnavigated produces: Execution Regression — and uniquely amplifies all three other mechanisms simultaneously. An organisation whose internal forces are reasserting has no capacity to navigate external forces. It is fighting itself while fighting the environment at the same time.

All Five Unnavigated → Success Inertia

Success Inertia is not a separate force. It is the total navigation failure — the state in which all five force categories have moved and the organisation is still positioned for the environment that existed when its results were strong.

Success Inertia operates through a deeply human mechanism. When an approach produces strong results, the people who designed and executed it develop a natural conviction in its continued validity. The strategy worked. The processes delivered. The enterprise performed. Why would we change what is working?

This conviction is not irrational. But it is dangerous, because the environment that rewarded the approach has already changed. The organisation’s attachment to the approach that succeeded is precisely what prevents it from developing the approach that will succeed next.

Success Inertia is Performance Gravity applied to all five forces simultaneously. Competitive landscape moved — untracked. Macro conditions accumulated weight — unremoved. Stakeholder expectations evolved — unread. Internal behaviour patterns reasserted — unaddressed. And through all of it, the organisation continued to believe that what worked yesterday will sustain tomorrow.

Every organisation that has ever achieved something significant and then lost it followed this pattern. It did not lose through a single catastrophic failure. It lost through the quiet accumulation of five unnavigated force movements, each individually dismissible, collectively devastating.

Extrapolating · Where & How

The Alignment Brigade — ABCV — KISS Navigation Chain

Where force movements appear first across the enterprise, how to read what they mean for strategic position, and how to determine the navigation response.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Map force movements to specific leadership domains using the Alignment Brigade
  • Apply the ABCV diagnostic to determine what each force movement means for strategic position
  • Use the MBT condition to identify the precise moment a navigation response is required
  • Execute the KISS navigation response to the specific ABCV finding
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Extrapolating

This section runs in three connected parts. The Alignment Brigade shows WHERE. ABCV shows HOW TO READ IT. KISS shows HOW TO RESPOND. These three are sequentially dependent — do not allow leaders to jump to KISS without completing the ABCV diagnostic. The quality of the KISS response is entirely dependent on the quality of the ABCV reading.

On the Alignment Brigade: Each row now shows the primary force category alongside the domain. Ask each leader to read their own row and identify which early warning signal is already present. If none are present, ask: “How confident are you that you would see the signal if it appeared?”

On ABCV: Participants encountered this in Lens 3 as the OKR stress-test. Make the elevation explicit: “In Lens 3, ABCV tested whether your strategic commitments were soundly built. Here it tests whether they still hold as forces move. Same instrument, elevated purpose.” For each ABCV card, ask leaders to apply the navigation question to the most significant force movement currently active in their environment.

On the Navigation Chain: The chain is the practical payoff of the entire lens. Walk through it once as a group using a real example from the room before the Application exercise.

Part 2 — ABCV as the Navigation Diagnostic
HOW TO READ what force movement means for strategic position

When the Alignment Brigade detects force movement in any domain, the ABCV framework provides the diagnostic instrument that reveals what that movement means for the organisation’s strategic position. Introduced in this programme as the OKR integrity stress-test, ABCV reaches its full strategic purpose here: four diagnostic questions applied to any detected force movement, each with a Must-Be-True condition that identifies the precise moment a navigation response is required.

For each force movement detected: run the four ABCV navigation questions. When a MBT condition can no longer be confirmed as true — that is the navigation signal. That is when KISS is activated.

Arena
The Customer End-Game
▼
Navigation Question
Has this force shifted the customer end-game we are competing for? Are we still solving the right problem for the right stakeholders in the right way?
Strategic Logic
Strategy starts with a clear definition of the arena — the specific customer need the organisation is competing to serve. Force movements — competitive, disruptive, and social — do not just threaten execution. They fundamentally shift the arena itself. The customer’s problem evolves. The job they need done changes. The context in which they experience your solution is different from the context in which it was designed.
Must-Be-True Condition

What must remain true about the relevance of the customer problem we are solving for our current approach to remain the right approach?

When Not Examined
When Competitive or Disruptive forces move and Arena is not re-examined: the organisation continues serving a problem the customer has either solved another way, deprioritised, or reframed entirely. Revenue can be temporarily maintained through relationship inertia while the Arena has already shifted away.
Boundaries
The Friction Architecture
▼
Navigation Question
Has this force created new operational, regulatory, or cultural constraints on our ability to deliver? Are we operating within boundaries that the current environment no longer imposes — or failing to recognise boundaries that it does?
Strategic Logic
Boundaries are the internal and external constraints within which the organisation must operate. Macro-environmental forces move these boundaries continuously — regulations tighten, economic conditions shift, technology changes what is operationally possible. Internal forces also create boundaries through cultural habits and enterprise behaviour patterns that constrain what people are actually willing to do, regardless of what the strategy says.
Must-Be-True Condition

What must remain true about our operational and regulatory environment for our current delivery model to continue functioning within tolerance?

When Not Examined
When Macro-environmental or Internal forces move and Boundaries are not re-examined: the organisation continues operating within self-imposed constraints that the current environment does not require — or fails to recognise new constraints the environment has introduced. Complexity Creep is the result of boundaries accumulated and never reviewed.
Competition
The True Landscape
▼
Navigation Question
Has this force changed who we are actually competing against — including alternatives the customer now has that did not previously exist?
Strategic Logic
Competition is defined by the customer’s choice set — not by the organisation’s view of its industry peers. When Competitive and Disruptive forces move, the set of alternatives available to the customer changes. New entrants solve the same problem differently. Technology creates self-service alternatives. Adjacent competitors develop capability that makes them viable for previously loyal customers. The organisation that defines competition by reference to historical peers misses the force movements that matter most.
Must-Be-True Condition

What must remain true about our specific advantage relative to every alternative the customer could now choose?

When Not Examined
When Competitive or Disruptive forces move and Competition is not re-examined: the organisation maintains advantage over competitors it knows while losing ground to competitors it is not watching. Win rate shifts are explained by execution rather than by the fundamental change in the competitive landscape.
