Lens 8 — Session Overview

Orientation, facilitation questions, tone guidance, and watch points for the full lens.

◆ Lens Intent

Lens 8 sits at a pivotal moment in the S2R® climb. By this point, the strategy is clear, performance expectations are set, and commitments have been secured. Now the gears begin grinding. This lens names that friction precisely — and gives the leadership team the engineering vocabulary and diagnostic discipline to address it.

The central reframe is powerful and must land clearly: a clog is not a failure. It is a signal. It indicates where the organisation’s current operating system has not yet adapted to the new strategic direction. Leaders who understand this stop asking “Why are we failing?” and start asking “Where is the system not yet designed to carry the strategy?”

The Clog Clinic in Application is the centrepiece — protect the full 45 minutes. It must use real stalled KPIs, not hypotheticals. The quality of the diagnostic conversation is the measure of this lens.

◆ Key Facilitation Questions
  • “Think of a strategy that stalled in your organisation. Was the plan wrong — or was something in the system blocking its flow?”
  • “When you last named a performance gap, did you name the symptom or the root cause? What’s the difference in your context?”
  • “Which of the seven clogs is most predictable in your organisation given your current strategic direction? What does that tell you about where to look first?”
  • “If Cultural Resistance is your diagnosis — is that genuinely a culture problem, or is it a Leadership Avoidance problem that has produced cultural symptoms?”
  • “You’ve selected a lever. What would have to be true for that lever to actually clear the clog — rather than just creating the appearance of movement?”
◆ Tone & Watch Points

Watch for: the relief response. When leaders first encounter the clog map, they often feel relieved — they recognise their organisations immediately. The facilitation risk is that the conversation stays at the level of recognition rather than moving to diagnosis and intervention. Push past identification: “Recognising it is the start. Now name it precisely and select the lever.”

Watch for: Cultural Resistance overuse. It is the most comfortable diagnosis because it externalises the problem. Challenge it every time: “Is this a culture issue, or is it the downstream consequence of Leadership Avoidance?”

Watch for: lever vagueness. “We need to improve communication” is not a lever. A lever is a structural adjustment — a redesigned decision right, a reset KPI, a rebuilt process, a named accountability. Push every group until their intervention is concrete enough to own.

Timing: Awareness and Intelligence can move at pace. Extrapolating benefits from brief functional discussion. Reserve the full 45 minutes for the Clog Clinic — it should not be compressed.

What is a Strategic Clog?
Core concept

At this point, the strategy is clear. Objectives are defined. Performance expectations are in motion. Then the gears begin grinding.

At its simplest, a Strategic Clog is an internal blockage that prevents strategic intent from flowing into real-world execution. It is the widening gap between what we said we would do and what is actually happening. These are not minor inconveniences or growing pains. They are fundamental misalignments where the organisation’s current state — its habits, its structures, its very DNA — works actively against its future goals.

Strategy sets the destination. Flow determines whether you have the power to arrive.

To navigate this phase of the execution climb, leadership must evolve. It is no longer enough to simply design a brilliant strategy. The role of the leadership team is to become Engineers of Execution Flow — developing the diagnostic eyes to detect clogs early, before they compound into stalled momentum or declining performance.

Every clog interferes with one or more elements of ABCV Directional Integrity:

  • Arena — clarity of the problem space and customer end-game
  • Boundaries — structural, operational, and regulatory constraints
  • Competition — how the organisation defines alternatives and threats
  • Value Proposition — how the organisation creates distinct value

When execution is clogged, strategy does not fail visibly. It loses directional integrity — gradually and silently.

System Logic: A clog acts like a pressure distortion point in the system. It redirects effort away from intended outcomes, weakens coherence across functions, and creates misinterpretation of strategic intent.

Three Observable Friction Signals
How clogs first appear

Despite the clarity of the plan, the reality of the work will periodically be characterised by friction. Three patterns signal that a clog is forming.

⏱
Decision Paralysis
Choices that should be instinctive begin taking longer than expected, creating a bottleneck at the top. Leaders hesitate where they should act.
⇄
Kinetic Misalignment
Teams move with intensity but pull in different directions, cancelling each other’s efforts. Energy is high; progress is not.
📊
Output–Outcome Gap
Tasks are completed and boxes are checked, but the needle on actual strategic goals isn’t moving. Activity is high; advancement is not.
◆ Facilitator Prompt

Ask the group: “Which of these three signals is most visible in your organisation right now? Raise your hand for the one you recognise most immediately.” Use the show of hands to calibrate which clog category to explore first in the map below.

The 7-Clog Diagnostic Map
ABCV-aligned · Select each clog to explore

Each clog category represents a distinct type of system misalignment. Select each to examine the structural reality, observable patterns, early warning signals, and strategic impact.

Clog 01
Structural Misfit
Boundaries
▼
Structural Reality
Organisational design (roles, reporting lines, decision rights, governance layers) is misaligned to the current strategy. Structure reflects legacy priorities, not strategic intent.
Observable Patterns
Bottlenecks at functional handoffs; duplication of work; fragmented ownership of outcomes; breakdown between front-office and back-office; conflicting priorities across functions.
Early Warnings: Increasing coordination time; rising dependency on informal workarounds; escalation frequency increases; unclear ownership of outcomes.
Strategic Impact: The organisation operates within the wrong structural logic. Execution becomes disjointed, coordination costs increase, and strategy cannot move coherently across the enterprise system.
Clog 02
Capability Gaps
Arena
▼
Structural Reality
Critical capabilities (skills, systems, tools, capacity, partnerships) required to deliver the strategy are absent, underdeveloped, or unevenly distributed.
Observable Patterns
Projects stall or slow; over-reliance on key individuals; inconsistent delivery quality; repeated outsourcing of core capabilities; inability to scale execution.
Early Warnings: Missed milestones; uneven performance across similar initiatives; burnout of key talent; increasing rework.
Strategic Impact: The organisation cannot fully operate within its chosen strategic space. The defined arena becomes theoretical — execution lacks the capability backbone required to compete and deliver.
Clog 03
Decision Bottlenecks
Boundaries
▼
Structural Reality
Decision rights are unclear, overly centralised, or embedded in outdated hierarchical processes. Authority is not aligned to execution proximity.
Observable Patterns
Slow approvals; repeated escalation loops; “waiting culture”; overloaded senior leadership; avoidance of decision ownership at middle levels.
Early Warnings: Decision cycle times increase; growing backlog of unresolved issues; repeated agenda items in meetings; teams hesitate to act without approval.
Strategic Impact: Decision flow is constrained, weakening execution rhythm. The organisation loses responsiveness and cannot adapt at the speed required by the strategy or environment.
Clog 04
Misaligned Metrics & Incentives
Value Proposition
▼
Structural Reality
KPIs, scorecards, and reward systems reinforce behaviours that conflict with strategic intent. Legacy metrics remain active post-strategy shift.
Observable Patterns
Functional optimisation over enterprise outcomes; gaming of metrics; resistance to collaboration; short-term delivery at the expense of long-term value.
Early Warnings: High KPI achievement with weak strategic progress; conflicting performance narratives across functions; misalignment between reported success and actual outcomes.
Strategic Impact: Effort is redirected toward what is measured rather than what creates value. The organisation produces activity and output, but not differentiated strategic outcomes.
Clog 05
Cultural Resistance
Value Proposition
▼
Structural Reality
Deeply embedded beliefs, norms, and habits contradict the behavioural shifts required by the strategy. Informal culture overrides formal direction.
Observable Patterns
Reversion to legacy behaviours under pressure; passive resistance; low initiative; compliance without commitment; scepticism toward change.
Early Warnings: Low engagement in strategic initiatives; limited experimentation; silence or avoidance in critical conversations; informal narratives contradicting strategy.
Strategic Impact: The organisation cannot consistently deliver its intended value because behaviour does not align with strategic intent. Cultural inertia preserves the past, weakening transformation capability.
Clog 06
Technology / Infrastructure Lag
Competition
▼
Structural Reality
Legacy systems, fragmented platforms, and poorly integrated infrastructure cannot support execution demands. Data architecture is incomplete or delayed.
Observable Patterns
Manual workarounds; spreadsheet dependency; delayed reporting; poor data visibility; system integration failures; limited automation.
Early Warnings: Data inconsistencies; long reporting cycles; operational errors increase at scale; inability to track real-time performance.
Strategic Impact: The organisation loses execution speed and intelligence. It cannot respond, scale, or compete effectively against more technologically enabled alternatives.
Clog 07
Leadership Avoidance
Arena + Boundaries
▼
Structural Reality
Leaders avoid confronting trade-offs, underperformance, structural misalignment, or difficult decisions required for strategy execution.
Observable Patterns
Persistent unresolved issues; repeated discussions without closure; weak accountability; shifting priorities; lack of enforcement of decisions.
Early Warnings: Recurring strategic issues across multiple cycles; low trust in leadership decisions; ambiguity in direction; declining ownership across teams.
Strategic Impact: Strategic clarity erodes as unresolved tensions accumulate. The organisation drifts between priorities, weakening both strategic focus (Arena) and execution discipline (Boundaries).
◆ Diagnostic Principle
  • Do not ask: “What is going wrong?” Ask: “Which ABCV element is being distorted — and by which clog?”
  • If multiple signals point to Boundaries → structural or decision system issue
  • If multiple signals point to Arena → capability or strategic participation issue
  • If multiple signals point to Value Proposition → behavioural or measurement misalignment
  • If multiple signals point to Competition → speed, responsiveness, or system capability gap
Intelligence · Why

Why Clogs Are Predictable Features, Not Random Failures

Understanding why execution friction is the inevitable consequence of system misalignment — and introducing the 7 Strategic Unclogging Levers as the leadership toolkit for restoring execution flow.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Explain why clogs are not accidents but predictable consequences of strategy stretching the existing operating system
  • Shift from reactive troubleshooting to proactive execution architecture as the primary leadership mindset
  • Apply the three disciplines of execution engineering: early identification, accurate classification, targeted intervention
  • Match each of the 7 Unclogging Levers to its strategic role and the clog types it is designed to address
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Intelligence

The ignition point of this section is the mindset shift: from “Why is the strategy failing?” to “Where is the system not yet designed to carry the strategy?” This is not semantics. It is a fundamentally different diagnostic posture that moves leadership from blame to engineering.

Spend time on the pressure point examples — they make the abstract concrete. A strategy requiring cross-functional coordination will expose structural misfits. A strategy requiring decentralised execution will expose decision bottlenecks. These are not surprises — they are predictable based on the design gap between what the strategy demands and what the system can currently provide.

For the 7 levers: make clear that levers are structural adjustments, not motivational initiatives. Rarely does a single lever clear a clog alone. The diagnostic question is always: which lever combination addresses the root cause?

Clogs Are Design Signals, Not Operational Failures
The core argument

When leaders define strategy, they design a future operating direction. When the organisation attempts to execute that strategy, it must do so through today’s operating system — its structure, decision rights, culture, metrics, capabilities, and technology. If any element of this system is misaligned with the new strategic direction, friction will appear. The friction is not accidental. It is the natural consequence of system misalignment.

This is why execution breakdowns rarely occur because the strategy itself is incorrect. They occur because the organisation’s structure, capabilities, behaviours, or systems have not yet adapted to the strategy. The clog is therefore not the failure. The clog is the signal that the system requires adjustment.

Clogs emerge precisely where the strategy stretches the existing system beyond its current design capacity. A strategy requiring rapid cross-functional coordination will expose structural misfits. A strategy dependent on data-driven decisions will reveal capability gaps. A strategy requiring decentralised execution will expose decision bottlenecks. These pressures are not random — they are predictable.

Experienced leadership teams recognise this. Rather than interpreting clogs as operational problems, they treat them as engineering signals indicating where the organisation must evolve. This transforms the role of the executive team: not only defining strategic direction, but ensuring the organisation’s operating system can carry that direction without excessive friction.

Detect Signals Before They Compound

Leaders must detect the signals that indicate friction is forming. Bottlenecks, repeated coordination failures, declining execution speed, or growing ambiguity in decision rights are all early indicators that a clog is emerging.

The strategic value of early identification is significant. A clog detected when it is a friction signal requires a targeted structural adjustment. The same clog discovered six months later, after it has compounded into a systemic execution failure, requires a crisis response. The cost — in time, capital, and leadership credibility — is vastly different.

This discipline requires leaders to build signal literacy: the ability to read organisational patterns not just as operational noise but as early indicators of structural misalignment.

Name the Clog Precisely Before Intervening

Each clog belongs to a specific category of organisational friction. Structural misfit, capability gaps, decision bottlenecks, misaligned incentives, cultural resistance, technological constraints, or leadership avoidance each require fundamentally different forms of intervention.

The most common diagnostic failure is applying the wrong lever to a correctly identified symptom. A leader who observes “slow execution” and responds by adding resources is applying a capability lever to what may be a decision bottleneck. The additional resources do not accelerate execution because the constraint is not capacity — it is approval latency.

Accurate classification requires resisting the impulse to solve before diagnosing. The question is not “What should we do?” It is “What type of friction is this, and what does it tell us about the system?”

Pull the Right Lever with Precision

Once the clog is identified and classified, it must be addressed through the appropriate strategic lever. Clogs rarely clear themselves through trying harder or working more hours. They require a deliberate adjustment to the organisation’s structural, behavioural, and operational settings.

Targeted intervention means matching the lever to the root cause — not to the symptom. It also means accepting that rarely does a single clog require just one fix. A Capability Gap may require both Upgrade (systems) and Reinforce (skills). A Leadership Avoidance clog may require both Clarify (decision rights) and Rebuild Trust (credibility restoration).

The goal is to develop a leadership rhythm that moves quickly and precisely: Identify the Clog → Select the Lever → Targeted Intervention.

The 7 Strategic Unclogging Levers
Select each lever to explore

Think of these seven levers as the leadership toolkit for execution architecture. They do not replace the strategy; they restore the conditions that allow the strategy to move through the organisation at the speed and quality intended. Each lever targets a specific dimension of the system.

Lever 01
Standardise
Ensure Consistency
▼

Harmonises processes and service levels across teams so everyone plays by the same rules.

Variability is a silent killer of speed. By establishing a shared “way we work,” you reduce duplication and ensure every unit pulls in the same direction. Best applied to: Structural Misfit, Misaligned Metrics & Incentives, Technology / Infrastructure Lag.
Lever 02
Streamline
Increase Speed
▼

Simplifies workflows and removes the red tape layers that add no value to execution.

Leaner processes mean faster approvals and a team that can pivot as quickly as the market demands. Best applied to: Decision Bottlenecks, Structural Misfit.
Lever 03
Clarify
Eliminate Confusion
▼

Defines exactly who owns what, where the handoffs are, and who has the final say.

Ambiguity breeds hesitation. When people know their roles and decision rights, accountability returns and work stops getting stuck in limbo. Best applied to: Decision Bottlenecks, Structural Misfit, Leadership Avoidance.
Lever 04
Upgrade
Remove Bottlenecks
▼

Fixes underperforming technology and infrastructure that holds execution back.

You cannot run a modern strategy on yesterday’s tools. Upgrading removes the physical and digital friction that makes scaling feel impossible. Best applied to: Technology / Infrastructure Lag, Capability Gaps.
Lever 05
Reinforce
Strengthen Behaviour
▼

Equips the team with the skills, coaching, and confidence they need to actually deliver.

Strategy requires new behaviours. Reinforcement ensures your people are not just willing to change, but actually capable of doing so. Best applied to: Capability Gaps, Cultural Resistance.
Lever 06
Realign
Focus on Results
▼

Adjusts metrics and rewards so they actually match current strategic priorities.

People do what they are measured on. When you fix the incentives, the team’s energy naturally shifts from protecting their patch to creating enterprise value. Best applied to: Misaligned Metrics & Incentives.
Lever 07
Rebuild Trust
Restore Credibility
▼

Heals relationships and introduces the transparency needed for people to commit again.

Trust is the social grease of execution. Without it, the system seizes up. Rebuilding it restores the psychological safety required for high-stakes work. Best applied to: Cultural Resistance, Leadership Avoidance.
◆ Lever Selection Principle

Identifying a clog is only half the work. The real discipline is lever selection. Rarely does a clog require just one fix. If the clog is a Capability Gap, the leaders may need to Upgrade the systems while simultaneously Reinforcing the team’s skills. If the clog is Leadership Avoidance, there will likely be a need to Clarify decision rights and Rebuild Trust to move forward. Always ask: “Which lever combination addresses the root cause — not just the visible symptom?”

The interpretation guidance is critical: a capability gap located in People & Capability is a skills issue; the same gap located in Systems & Execution may be an operating model issue. The 7 COMPASS High-Flammability Zones
Select each zone to explore

Each clog will surface within one or more of the following COMPASS zones. By locating clogs within COMPASS, leaders move from reactive problem-solving to precise system intervention.

C
Clarity of Direction
▼

Signals confusion, drift, or competing priorities in the strategic direction. When this zone heats up, people are unclear what the strategy actually requires of them day-to-day.

Warning signals: Strategy described differently by each leader; too many unranked priorities; teams treat strategy as an annual event not daily guidance; people wait for clarity before acting.
Most common clogs: Decision Bottlenecks, Leadership Avoidance, Cultural Resistance.
O
Organisational Alignment
▼

Signals fragmentation across functions. When this zone heats up, different parts of the organisation are executing to different priorities or conflicting incentives.

Warning signals: KPIs conflict across functions; incentives reward wrong behaviours; silos resist shared outcomes; customer issues bounce between teams unresolved.
Most common clogs: Structural Misfit, Misaligned Metrics & Incentives, Cultural Resistance.
M
Management Discipline
▼

Signals weak follow-through and accountability. When this zone heats up, governance is producing reports rather than decisions, and performance gaps are tolerated rather than corrected.

Warning signals: No clear review cadence; decision rights unclear; leaders tolerate repeated misses; accountability conversations avoided; teams hide problems.
Most common clogs: Decision Bottlenecks, Leadership Avoidance, Cultural Resistance.
P
People & Capability
▼

Signals execution strain or capacity gaps. When this zone heats up, the strategy is demanding capabilities the organisation has not yet built or deployed.

Warning signals: Strategy requires skills not available; over-reliance on consultants without internal transfer; people fear speaking up; performance standards vary across teams.
Most common clogs: Capability Gaps, Leadership Avoidance, Cultural Resistance.
A
Allocation of Resources
▼

Signals misalignment between stated priorities and actual investment decisions. When this zone heats up, money and people are flowing to the wrong places relative to the strategy.

Warning signals: Budget follows history not priorities; too many projects funded simultaneously; leaders commit to outcomes without resourcing; teams compete defensively for resources.
Most common clogs: Structural Misfit, Leadership Avoidance, Cultural Resistance.
S1
Systems & Execution
▼

Signals operational or structural friction slowing execution throughput. When this zone heats up, the machinery of delivery is creating drag rather than enabling flow.

Warning signals: Operating model doesn’t support strategic priorities; critical work delayed by approvals; tools slow delivery; leaders don’t remove blockers; firefighting feels normal.
Most common clogs: Structural Misfit, Technology / Infrastructure Lag, Leadership Avoidance.
S2
Sensing & Adaptation
▼

Signals missed or ignored internal and external feedback. When this zone heats up, the organisation is operating on stale information and has lost its ability to adapt in real time.

Warning signals: Customer churn visible but ignored; competitor moves tracked late; leaders defend outdated forecasts; people hide bad news; teams stick to plans when reality has shifted.
Most common clogs: Capability Gaps, Leadership Avoidance, Cultural Resistance.

Activate the Appropriate Lever

Once the clog and COMPASS zone are identified, select the intervention lever:

  • Standardise — establish consistent frameworks or processes
  • Streamline — simplify workflows or decision pathways
  • Clarify — define roles, ownership, and decision rights
  • Upgrade — modernise systems, tools, or infrastructure
  • Reinforce — strengthen capability through coaching or development
  • Realign — adjust incentives, metrics, or priorities
  • Rebuild Trust — restore credibility through visible leadership action

This enables rapid movement from diagnosis to intervention. The lever must be matched to the root cause, not to the symptom.

Prioritise the Intervention

Assess the level of execution risk to determine urgency:

  • Low — early signal present; limited impact on momentum. Monitor and pre-empt.
  • Medium — clear friction affecting progress; intervention required within 5 working days.
  • High — significant execution risk; immediate action required; escalate to Alignment Brigade.

Severity rating allows leadership teams to prioritise interventions across the strategy portfolio. A portfolio view of all active clogs — their categories, COMPASS zones, and severity ratings — constitutes the Enterprise Unclogging Agenda.

Integration Zone · Collective

Cross-Leadership Clog Ownership

Building the collective discipline of normalising the unclog — where detecting and clearing execution friction becomes a shared leadership habit rather than a crisis response.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Articulate why individual clog ownership is insufficient and collective leadership stewardship is required
  • Establish a “see something, say something” culture where surfacing friction is an act of leadership, not a critique of colleagues
  • Build the 4-question diagnostic rhythm into regular leadership team practice
  • Construct the Enterprise Unclogging Agenda as the collective output of the cross-leadership diagnostic conversation
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Integration Zone

The central shift in this zone is from my clog to our clog. Most execution blockages surface in one function but originate in another. A technology lag in IT may actually reflect a resource allocation decision in Finance. A cultural resistance in Sales may be the downstream consequence of a leadership avoidance pattern at the top. The Alignment Brigade must own the diagnostic conversation collectively.

The “see something, say something” principle is culturally important. In most organisations, surfacing a problem in someone else’s domain is politically risky. Reframe this explicitly: detecting friction early is an act of leadership stewardship, not departmental interference.

Close this zone by having each CXO name one clog they suspect is forming outside their function — and the COMPASS zone where they believe it is accumulating. This is the seed of the Enterprise Unclogging Agenda.

Normalising the Unclog — From Crisis to Habit
The collective discipline

In most organisations, when execution hits a snag, the response is messy. Leaders vent in hallways, complain about “poor execution” in silos, and fixes are applied inconsistently. It is reactive, exhausting, and typically produces a cycle of constant firefighting rather than genuine improvement.

The purpose of the Integration Zone is to standardise how the leadership team finds and fixes blockages. By using a shared unclogging process, the entire leadership team operates with a common language and a reliable toolkit. Fixing execution friction becomes a normal part of how the organisation runs — not a panicked response to a crisis.

When “unclogging” is a standard discipline, flagging a problem is no longer seen as a failure or a critique of a colleague. It is treated as valuable data. Leaders build a culture where detecting friction early is celebrated — because it is how the organisation learns to adapt.

This gives the Alignment Brigade a clear shared path:

  • Is there a clog?
  • What category does it fall into?
  • Which part of the system is it affecting?
  • Which lever do we pull to fix it?

When leaders standardise how they find and fix blockages, they develop a genuine organisational superpower: the ability to maintain the flow of strategy. They stop letting friction build up in the corners of the organisation. The result is a team that does not just talk about strategy — they execute it with discipline, transparency, and a collective “we’ve got this” attitude.

The “See Something, Say Something” Principle
Shared stewardship of execution

Even though every KR has a specific leadership owner, blockages rarely stay in one lane. A technical delay in IT might actually be a people problem in Marketing or a process clog in Finance. Because execution is a team sport, the unclogging tool is for everyone.

If any leader sees friction forming — even if it is not in their area of execution — they are encouraged to surface it. This is not about stepping on toes. It is about shared stewardship. Surfacing a blockage early is an act of leadership, ensuring that small hitches do not turn into organisation-wide delays.

◆ Facilitator Exercise

Ask each CXO to complete the following statement silently, then share in the group:

“The clog I suspect is forming outside my function is [clog category] in the [COMPASS zone]. The signal I’m observing is [signal]. I’m not raising it because [barrier]. I should raise it because [consequence of not raising it].”

The barriers that emerge — political risk, uncertainty, not wanting to appear critical — are the cultural patterns that must be addressed before the unclogging discipline can become a genuine team habit.

The Integration Zone closes with each CXO contributing to a shared Enterprise Unclogging Agenda — the collective view of where clogs are forming, their severity, and the levers required. This replaces the informal “hallway complaints” with a structured, visible, collectively owned priority list.

Integration Application · In Practice

The Clog Clinic

A structured 3-part exercise where participants practise moving from execution symptom to precise diagnosis to targeted intervention — using a real stalled KPI from their own organisation.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Apply the 4-step Unclogging activation to a real stalled KPI from their current portfolio
  • Distinguish the symptom from the root cause through the structured Challenger conversation
  • Select a precise lever with a specific owner, action, and completion date
  • Evaluate diagnostic quality against the four Observer criteria
◆ Facilitator Guidance — The Clog Clinic

This exercise is non-negotiable and must use real data. Hypothetical clogs produce hypothetical insights. The Diagnostician must bring an actual stalled KPI — one that has been visible for more than two review cycles without resolution. The discomfort of working on a real problem in front of colleagues is precisely the point.

Watch for the symptom-to-lever skip. The most common failure is jumping from observable symptom directly to lever selection without naming the clog. A lever applied to the wrong clog will not clear it — and may make the underlying problem worse. If this happens, pause and redirect: “Wait. Before we select the lever, have we correctly classified the clog? Let’s go back to Q3.”

Watch for Cultural Resistance overuse. It is the most comfortable diagnosis because it externalises the problem. Challenge it: “Is this genuinely a culture issue — or is it a Leadership Avoidance clog that has produced cultural symptoms?”

Watch for vague levers. “We’ll improve communication” is not a lever. A lever is a structural change — a redesigned decision right, a reset KPI, a rebuilt process, a named accountability. Push until the action is concrete enough to be owned.

The Diagnostic Conversation — 10 Minutes Per Person

Participants work in triads: Diagnostician · Challenger · Observer. Roles rotate after each round so every participant runs the diagnostic once.

👤 Diagnostician

Presents the stalled KPI. Reasons aloud through the four questions. Must not skip Q3.

👤 Challenger

Guides through the four questions in sequence. Challenges comfortable answers. Does not give the diagnosis — draws it out.

👤 Observer

Listens and evaluates against the four criteria. Provides structured feedback after the round.

Four Questions — in sequence, without skipping:

Q1 — Where is the friction?

“Which COMPASS zone best describes where the pain is showing up — C, O, M, P, A, S1, or S2?”

Q2 — What is the signal?

“Of the signals in that zone — which one most precisely describes what you are observing?”

Q3 — What is the clog?

“Based on that signal — what type of blockage is at the root? Structure, capability, decision, metrics, culture, technology, or leadership?” The Challenger challenges: “Are you sure that’s the root — or is that the symptom of something deeper?”

Q4 — What lever would you pull?

“Given that diagnosis — what is the first action you would take? Not a general direction. A specific action, with an owner and a date.”

The Alignment Brigade Challenge — Full Group

Each triad nominates one diagnosis to bring to the full group — ideally the one they found most difficult to crack. The nominated Diagnostician presents in 90 seconds:

“Our KR was [X]. The friction zone was [Y]. The signal was [Z]. We diagnosed [clog]. Our proposed lever is [action] — owned by [person] — by [date].”

The full group then challenges for three minutes:

  • Is the COMPASS zone correctly identified?
  • Is the clog category the root cause or just a surface symptom?
  • Is the lever precise enough — or is it still general pressure?

The facilitator closes each challenge with:

“What would have to be true for that lever to actually clear the clog?”

Observer Evaluation Criteria
4 criteria for diagnostic quality
Criterion 01 — The Diagnostician Reasoned Through It

Did the Diagnostician go straight to the comfortable answer — or genuinely reason through the diagnostic sequence? A good diagnostic conversation shows visible thinking, not pattern-matching to the most familiar clog.

Criterion 02 — The Clog Was Distinguished from the Symptom

Was the clog correctly separated from what was initially observed? The symptom is what leadership sees first (e.g. slow delivery). The clog is the root cause (e.g. decision bottleneck). These must remain distinct.

Criterion 03 — The Challenger Used Curiosity, Not Pressure

Did the Challenger draw out the diagnosis through questions — or push the Diagnostician toward a conclusion? A good challenger maintains diagnostic curiosity throughout and challenges without prescribing.

Criterion 04 — The Intervention Was Concrete

Did the conversation end with a specific, time-bound action that both parties understood and accepted? “We’ll improve communication” is not a lever. A lever is a structural change with a named owner and a date.

Micro-Climb Summary — Strategic Unclogging
Lens 8 synthesis
Awareness

A Strategic Clog is an internal blockage that prevents strategic intent from flowing into execution. The 7-clog diagnostic map — aligned to ABCV Directional Integrity — gives leaders the vocabulary to name the type of friction rather than reacting to the symptom.

Intelligence

Clogs are not failures — they are design signals. Where strategy stretches the operating system beyond its current capacity, friction will appear predictably. The three disciplines of execution engineering (identify, classify, intervene) and the 7 levers restore flow without reactive pressure.

Extrapolating

COMPASS provides the coordinate system for locating clogs within the enterprise execution architecture. The combination of clog category + COMPASS zone delivers the precision needed to select the correct lever. The 4-step activation translates diagnosis into structured intervention.

Integration Zone

Clogs rarely stay in one lane. The Alignment Brigade must own the diagnostic conversation collectively, building a “see something, say something” culture where surfacing friction early is an act of leadership — and the Enterprise Unclogging Agenda becomes the shared instrument of execution restoration.

“When unclogging becomes a habit, everything changes. Friction is no longer a crisis — it is a signal telling you how the organisation needs to evolve next. You stop just managing a plan and start engineering flow.”

◆ Facilitator Closing Note

Close by asking each leader to name one clog that has been present in their organisation for more than six months — visible, discussed, but not yet cleared. Ask them: “What has it actually cost the organisation in strategic momentum? And what is the real reason it hasn’t been cleared yet?” The answers to the second question are almost always the true clogs beneath the clogs. That conversation is the beginning of genuine execution engineering.
