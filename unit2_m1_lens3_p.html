What Are We Actually Testing?

Understanding why well-written objectives still fail — and the four conditions that determine whether they hold

1.1.1 — The Problem with Well-Written OKRs
Foundation
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section you will be able to
  • Explain why well-written OKRs still fail when underlying assumptions go unnamed
  • Describe the ABCV-MBT framework as a pre-execution integrity filter for strategic direction
  • Distinguish between an OKR that is measurable and one that is strategically grounded

A well-written OKR can still fail. Not because the writing is imprecise. Not because the team lacks capability. But because the strategic conditions it assumed — the Arena, the Boundaries, the Competition, the Value Proposition — were never verified before execution began.

This is the problem ABCV-MBT solves. It moves strategy from belief to testable commitment by naming the conditions that must be true for a strategy to hold — and then requiring leaders to verify them.

The Must-Be-True (MBT) Condition

A Must-Be-True condition is a specific, verifiable assumption that a strategic objective requires in order to remain valid. It is not a risk. It is not a concern. It is a named condition: either it is true, or the objective rests on unstable ground.

The power of naming MBTs is not in the naming itself. It is in what naming reveals: which parts of the strategy have been confirmed, and which parts have simply been assumed.

1.1.2 — The Four ABCV Integrity Checkpoints
Core Framework
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section you will be able to
  • Identify what each of the four ABCV lenses tests in a strategic direction
  • State the Must-Be-True condition each lens requires in order for a strategy to hold
  • Apply one ABCV lens to a live objective from your own context

ABCV provides four lenses through which to test the integrity of any strategic direction. Click each lens to explore what it examines, the question it asks, and the Must-Be-True condition it requires.

Arena
Customer End Game
🎯▼

Strategic Logic: A strategy is only as valid as the customer outcome it is designed to deliver. Arena defines the precise destination — not the product, not the service, but the functional, emotional, and social outcome the customer is truly pursuing.

When Arena is not verified, strategy can be perfectly executed in the wrong direction. The product works. The team performs. But the customer has moved on to a different end-game.

➤ Interrogative Anchor: What functional, emotional, and social outcome is the customer truly pursuing — and has it shifted?
MBT Condition: The customer outcome we have designed this strategy around is still the right outcome — and has not materially shifted since our last verified check.
Boundaries
Friction Architecture
⚓▼

Strategic Logic: Every strategy operates within a friction architecture — the regulatory, structural, and behavioural constraints that determine what can and cannot be delivered. Boundaries names these explicitly so that strategy is designed around real constraints, not assumed away from them.

A strategy that ignores its Boundaries produces plans that are coherent in design and impossible in execution. The friction was always there; it simply wasn’t named.

➤ Interrogative Anchor: What internal or external friction is preventing strategy from delivering at scale?
MBT Condition: The regulatory, structural, and behavioural constraints operating on this strategy remain within a range that our execution model can absorb.
Competition
True Landscape
🌍▼

Strategic Logic: Competition is not limited to those who look like you. The true competitive landscape includes all alternatives the customer can choose — including doing nothing, self-service, or a solution from an entirely different industry.

Organisations that define competition too narrowly build advantage against the wrong rivals. Disruption almost always arrives from outside the frame they were watching.

➤ Interrogative Anchor: Who else is solving this problem — including those who don’t look like competitors?
MBT Condition: The competitive landscape we have mapped includes all meaningful alternatives the customer has — not just traditional industry rivals.
Value Proposition
Coherent Advantage
💡▼

Strategic Logic: A value proposition is not a marketing statement. It is the coherent set of choices — what you do, what you do not do, and why your combination of both is difficult to replicate — that creates defensible advantage in the Arena you have chosen.

Strategies that expand too broadly, or accept the wrong kind of growth, erode the Value Proposition over time. Distinctiveness is not inherited. It must be maintained by design, checked at each strategic decision.

➤ Interrogative Anchor: Does our distinctive value architecture still create coherent, defensible advantage?
MBT Condition: The strategic choices we are making with this objective strengthen — or at minimum do not dilute — our distinctive value architecture.

Why These Four Lenses?

The strategic failures that ABCV is designed to prevent — and why naming conditions changes what leaders are responsible for

1.2.1 — The Industry Illusion & Arena Shift
Strategic Pattern
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section you will be able to
  • Explain why industry-category thinking creates strategic blind spots
  • Distinguish between an industry definition and an arena definition of competitive space
  • Apply the three customer need dimensions — functional, emotional, social — to your own context

Nokia built the best phones in the world for the market that existed. But the Arena had shifted: the customer’s end-game was no longer a phone — it was a connected, intelligent, personalised device. Nokia’s execution was excellent. Its Arena was wrong.

Netflix understood something Blockbuster did not: the customer’s true end-game was not a physical rental — it was frictionless access to content. The Arena had always been about convenience and selection. Blockbuster optimised the store. Netflix redefined what the store was for.

The Arena has three dimensions that must all be examined:

  • Functional — What specific task or problem the customer needs resolved
  • Emotional — How the customer wants to feel during and after the experience
  • Social — How the customer wants to be seen by others through their choice

Most strategies address the functional dimension adequately. The emotional and social dimensions are where strategic drift goes undetected — and where early Arena shifts first become visible, if leaders are asking the right questions.

1.2.2 — Naming the Must-Be-Trues
From Belief to Commitment
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section you will be able to
  • Connect the ABCV structure to the discipline of naming Must-Be-True conditions
  • Articulate why naming MBT conditions transforms ambition into tested commitment
  • Write at least one MBT condition per ABCV lens for one of your current Key Results

An MBT condition is not a hope. It is not a risk register entry. It is a specific, named condition that can be investigated, confirmed, or disconfirmed. The act of naming it is an act of strategic honesty: this is what we are betting on.

Each ABCV lens generates a different category of MBT condition:

  • Arena MBTs — Conditions about what the customer is truly trying to achieve, and whether that outcome remains stable
  • Boundaries MBTs — Conditions about the operational, regulatory, and structural environment within which execution must succeed
  • Competition MBTs — Conditions about who the real alternatives are, and whether the competitive frame still reflects reality
  • Value Proposition MBTs — Conditions about whether the organisation’s distinctive advantage is being maintained, not eroded, by the strategy being pursued

When leaders name MBT conditions explicitly, they transform ambition into tested commitment. The strategy either rests on confirmed conditions — or it is a well-written aspiration waiting for reality to intervene.

Arena. The CFO tracks whether the model works — but whether the underlying customer demand is still real and growing is often outside the financial reporting frame.

⚠ Symptom: “The model works… so why is performance lagging?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Arena lens reframes financial health as dependent on customer-need relevance. Revenue can be temporarily maintained even as the underlying arena is shifting away from your value proposition.

✓ Right question: “Is the customer end-game we designed this model around still the right end-game?”
What Gets Overlooked

Competition. Operational leaders optimise the machine — but if the machine is serving the wrong arena, efficiency just accelerates the organisation in the wrong direction.

⚠ Symptom: “We’ve optimised everything — what are we missing?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Competition lens asks: who else is solving the customer’s problem, even those who look nothing like us? Operational excellence means nothing if the alternatives are more convenient for the customer.

✓ Right question: “Are our efficiency gains making us meaningfully more competitive against the real alternatives customers have?”

Competition. People alignment and engagement are the CHRO’s primary lens — but a highly engaged team executing the wrong strategy still loses to a competitor solving the problem better.

⚠ Symptom: “Our people are committed — why isn’t it translating?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Competition lens reveals that motivation and alignment are necessary but insufficient if strategic direction points at the wrong competitive target. Energy without direction is enthusiasm without results.

The Arena lens asks: did we build for what the customer is actually trying to achieve — or for the specification handed to us? Adoption requires arena-alignment, not just technical delivery.

What Gets Overlooked

Boundaries. The CMO creates demand — but if the operational infrastructure cannot fulfil that demand, the campaign creates frustration and brand damage rather than growth.

⚠ Symptom: “We created demand — why can’t we fulfil it?”

The Boundaries lens identifies the internal and external friction that must remain within tolerance for demand to convert to delivered value. Demand without delivery capacity is brand erosion, not brand growth.

✓ Right question: “Do our operational boundaries allow us to fulfil the demand our marketing is designed to generate?”
What Gets Overlooked

Value Proposition. Revenue growth is the CCO’s scorecard — but growing by winning the wrong business can dilute the organisation’s distinctive identity and long-term positioning.

The Value Proposition lens asks: does this contract strengthen or dilute our distinctive edge? Not all revenue is strategic revenue. Some growth makes the organisation less coherent, not more competitive.

✓ Right question: “Does this deal sharpen our value architecture or compromise it for short-term volume?”
What Gets Overlooked

Arena. Cost reduction and supply chain resilience are the CPO’s primary mandate — but savings that are not connected to the customer’s end-game do not translate into strategic progress.

The Arena lens asks: does this procurement decision improve our ability to deliver on the customer’s actual need? Cost efficiency that doesn’t serve the customer’s end-game is operational tidiness, not strategic advantage.

✓ Right question: “Does this cost initiative strengthen our ability to compete in the arena our customers actually operate in?”

Boundaries. Strategy clarity is the CSO’s domain — but without explicit identification of operational friction, strategies that are coherent in design break down repeatedly in execution.

⚠ Symptom: “Why does execution keep breaking after alignment?”

The Boundaries lens identifies structural, cultural, and regulatory friction points that strategy must be designed around — not assumed away. Clarity without friction-mapping is strategy without implementation physics.

💣 Mine 1 — The Ghost Ship

A SaaS platform achieved 85% retention in year one. By year three, churn reached 70%. Nothing in the product changed. Support tickets were low. The platform worked exactly as designed. Post-exit interviews revealed: customers had changed what they were trying to achieve — and the platform never followed.

💣 Mine 2 — The Ferrari in the Mud

A leading logistics firm invested R120m in a new digital operations platform. Eighteen months in, less than 30% of staff were using it consistently. Field teams had developed workarounds. Middle management had quietly reverted to spreadsheets. The system was technically excellent. The organisation was not designed to absorb it.

💣 Mine 3 — The Invisible Disruption

A financial advisory firm was ranked #1 in client satisfaction for four consecutive years. Then revenue began declining. No competitor had taken their clients. No scandal. No service failure. The problem: a fintech app was letting clients self-manage what the firm had always managed for them — and clients preferred it for 60% of use cases.

💣 Mine 4 — The Identity Crisis

A boutique consulting firm built its reputation on deep-sector expertise and bespoke strategy work. Under revenue pressure, they began accepting large implementation contracts — work that paid well but required different skills, different team profiles, and a different culture. Within two years, they won more work but lost their most experienced strategists and their distinctiveness with clients who valued exactly what they had been.

Chapter Summary — Lens 3
Close Out

This session was about ensuring your strategic direction is structurally sound before the first move of execution is made. We’ve all seen well-written OKRs collapse — not for lack of effort, but because the assumptions holding them up were never tested. The ABCV–MBT framework served as our strategic integrity filter: the stress test to ensure Key Results are anchored in customer reality, resilient to friction, and true to your organisation’s identity.

Here is a recap of how we moved from individual functional perspectives to a unified executive judgement.

1 — Awareness: The Integrity Checkpoints

We began with the What. The ABCV lenses interrogated strategy across four critical dimensions:

  • Arena — The specific customer end-game we are playing for
  • Boundaries — The operational or regulatory “clog points” that could slow us down
  • Competition — The alternatives (not just direct rivals) competing for the same need
  • Value Proposition — The distinctive edge that defines your organisational identity

For every lens, you identified the Must-Be-True (MBT) condition — the “load-bearing” assumption that must hold for the strategy to survive. This is the early warning system: exposing risks before they become expensive failures.

2 — Intelligence: Why Strategic Integrity Matters

This stage explored the Why. Most strategies don’t fail because of bad intentions — they fail because of unexamined assumptions.

We shifted thinking from rigid industry categories to Customer Arenas, recognising that customers don’t just buy products — they chase functional, emotional, and social outcomes. By naming MBT conditions early, you moved your strategy from a hopeful ambition to a battle-tested commitment.

3 — Extrapolating: Locating the Blind Spots

Next, the Where. Every CXO sees the world through a specialised lens:

  • CEOs lean toward narrative and stakeholder credibility
  • CFOs hunt for financial viability and return logic
  • COOs focus on operational feasibility and delivery rhythm
  • CHROs look at culture and leadership readiness

While these perspectives are vital, they naturally create selective attention. Applying the ABCV–MBT lens across all roles surfaced the hidden risks that usually only emerge halfway through the execution cycle.

4 — Integration Zone: Collective Intelligence

The session culminated in the Converging Zone — the moment your team consolidated individual perspectives into a single enterprise interpretation. By reviewing consolidated responses, you were able to:

  • Identify patterns of agreement and surface strategic disagreements
  • Determine the most critical “deal-breaker” execution conditions
  • Agree on the severity of the MBT requirements

The ABCV–MBT checkpoints have transformed your OKRs from polished aspirations into resilient execution signals, grounded in the hard realities of the enterprise system.
