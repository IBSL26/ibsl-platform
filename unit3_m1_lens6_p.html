Awareness · What

What is Establishing Performance Expectations?

Naming the distinction that determines whether a delivery goal becomes a metric to be monitored or a commitment to be owned.

◆ In this section you will
  • Distinguish between setting expectations (instruction) and establishing expectations (commitment)
  • Identify the three conditions — Clarity, Capability, Commitment — that must be present for a KPI to become a personal leadership promise
  • Map an Expectations Chain for a delivery goal you currently own
  • Understand the purpose and structure of the Alignment Dialogue
Setting vs Establishing Expectations
The defining distinction

Execution does not begin when delivery goals are written. Execution begins when the people responsible for those goals internalise the expectation and accept ownership of the result.

Dimension Setting Establishing
ApproachTop-down declaration; target assignedAlignment process; ownership secured
FocusClarity of instructionMutual readiness for execution
DialogueLimited; understanding assumed to equal commitmentStructured; feasibility tested, commitment secured
ExecutionCompliance-driven; cautious, upward escalationCommitment-driven; initiative, proactive solving
AccountabilityConditional; defensive ownershipPersonal; proactive ownership of outcome

Think of the last time you communicated a delivery expectation. Was it set or established? How do you know?

The Three Conditions of Established Expectations
Clarity · Capability · Commitment

A KPI becomes a personal leadership promise only when three conditions are simultaneously present. All three must be confirmed before execution begins.

Condition 1
Clarity — The individual understands exactly what success looks like
▼

Clarity is not just knowing the number. It means understanding the full picture of the expected outcome: what must be delivered, by when, at what standard, and how success will be recognised. Most expectation conversations stop at the number. Establishing expectations requires confirming that both parties share the same picture of the result.

Test: Ask someone on your team to describe what success looks like for their key result. If the description differs from yours, clarity has not yet been established.
Condition 2
Capability — The system can realistically support delivery
▼

Capability refers not only to the individual’s skill, but to the system surrounding them. Are the resources, decisions, cross-functional dependencies, and coordination mechanisms in place to support delivery? A person may understand and want to own a target that the current system cannot deliver. Establishing expectations surfaces this before execution begins — not after the deadline has passed.

Condition 3
Commitment — The person consciously accepts ownership of the outcome
▼

Commitment is the moment the expectation crosses from instruction to promise. The individual does not merely acknowledge the target — they accept personal responsibility for ensuring the outcome is delivered. This cannot be assumed from nodding agreement. It must be actively tested through dialogue. The Alignment Dialogue is the mechanism designed to confirm this transition has occurred.

The Alignment Dialogue asks: “On a scale of 1–10, how aligned are you with the expectation for you to deliver this outcome?” A score below 9 means Commitment is not yet established.

The Expectations Chain
Mapping who else influences the outcome

Very few delivery goals are achieved by a single person acting alone. The Expectations Chain identifies everyone whose work directly affects the delivery of a Key Result or KPI.

Why Establishing Expectations Determines Execution Outcomes

Compliance vs Commitment — The Execution Dynamic
The ignition point

The moment when expectations are clarified between leader and contributor is the moment a performance target transitions from organisational intention to personal responsibility. If that transition does not occur, execution becomes fragile regardless of how well the system has been designed.

Dimension Compliance-Driven Commitment-Driven

AccountabilityActivity ownership — responsible for tasks, not final resultOutcome ownership — personally accountable for the result
EnergyReserved; discretionary effort withheldFully invested; discretionary effort freely given
How it sounds“I completed everything that was asked of me.”“I’ll make sure this gets delivered.”

Five Mechanisms: Why Expectation Alignment Determines Outcomes
The behavioural logic
Mechanism 1
From Instruction to Ownership
▼

When expectations are merely communicated, people interpret them as instructions. They understand what must be achieved but do not yet feel personally accountable for ensuring the outcome is delivered. Structured dialogue moves the individual from acknowledging the requirement to accepting ownership of the result.

Mechanism 2
Reducing Interpretation Ambiguity
▼

Individuals interpret what success requires differently depending on their role, expertise, and operational context. Without alignment, people may pursue the same goal while working from different assumptions. Establishing expectations creates a shared interpretation of what must be delivered, why it matters, and how success will be recognised.

Mechanism 3
Surfacing Constraints Before Execution Begins
▼

If dependencies are not identified during the expectation conversation, they only become visible later — often when deadlines are approaching. Establishing expectations allows leaders and contributors to identify cross-functional dependencies, resource needs, and potential risks before they become execution crises.

Mechanism 4
Strengthening Accountability
▼

Accountability is strongest when individuals perceive that expectations placed on them are clear, achievable, and meaningful. Establishing expectations ensures both parties share a common understanding of the responsibility involved, the support available, and the standards for evaluating success — transforming accountability from formal obligation to shared commitment.

Mechanism 5
Building the Behavioural Foundation
▼

Strategy execution is fundamentally a behavioural challenge. Establishing performance expectations ensures that individuals are not simply aware of what must be done but are psychologically prepared to take responsibility for doing it. When this foundation is in place, purpose is understood, confidence increases, collaboration improves, and accountability becomes proactive.

Engagement & Accountability — The Two Casualties
What breaks down first
First Casualty
Engagement Weakens — Observable but Subtle
▼
  • Work progresses, but discretionary effort is limited
  • Individuals wait for direction rather than proactively addressing emerging problems
  • Cross-functional coordination slows; people focus on their own responsibilities
  • Engagement becomes transactional — roles are performed, collective ownership does not materialise
Signal: work is happening, but energy is not invested. People are present but not propelled.
Critical Long-Term Impact
Accountability Dilutes — The Deeper Cost
▼
  • Performance discussions revolve around explanations rather than ownership
  • Conversations focus on constraints rather than solutions
  • Cross-functional dependencies create delays because responsibility for resolving them is unclear
  • Multiple teams feel partially responsible; none is fully accountable
The paradox: teams appear to be working hard, meetings are frequent, updates are detailed — yet the organisation consistently struggles to achieve what it set out to deliver. The issue is not effort. It is that ownership was assigned without being secured.

The Expectation Architecture

A shared leadership practice for establishing expectations consistently across the organisation — from the Future to the Alignment Dialogue.

◆ In this section you will
  • Apply the four-step Expectation Architecture as a consistent leadership practice
  • Use the Alignment Dialogue and Score → Evaluate → Resolve coaching model
  • Interpret alignment scores accurately and respond to each score range
  • Capture the three leader reflections that convert a conversation into a commitment record
The Four-Step Expectation Architecture
A shared practice for every leader
Step 1
Start With the Future
Purpose
Connect the expectation to the organisation’s Success in Practice — so the individual understands why the outcome matters, not just what it is.
Leader framing
“As part of our goal to become the most responsive partner in our market, we need to reduce complaint response times across the service teams.”
Why it matters
When expectations begin with the Future, individuals understand the contribution their work makes to the broader direction. This shifts the conversation from compliance to purpose.
Step 2
Clarify the Delivery Expectation
Purpose
Define the specific outcome required — in result terms, not activity terms. Confirm a shared picture of what success looks like.
Leader framing
“The outcome we need is a reduction in resolution time from 48 hours to 12 hours by Q3 2026.”
Why it matters
Clarity is tested by asking the individual to describe success in their own words. If the description differs from yours, clarify further before proceeding.
Step 3
Identify the Expectations Chain
Purpose
Map who else must contribute for the result to be achieved. Ensure dependencies are visible before execution begins.
Explore
Which teams must collaborate? Whose decisions influence delivery? Where might coordination be required? Which external actors are involved?
Why it matters
This step helps the individual understand the execution system surrounding the expectation — they are not delivering alone.
Step 4
Confirm Ownership of the Result
Purpose
Confirm that the individual accepts responsibility for ensuring the outcome is delivered. This is the moment the expectation moves from instruction to commitment.
The question
“On a scale of 1–10, how aligned are you with the expectation for you to deliver this outcome?”
What it means
9–10 = proceed with confidence. Below 9 = move to Score → Evaluate → Resolve before execution begins.

The Alignment Dialogue — Score → Evaluate → Resolve
The coaching model

When the alignment score is below 9, the leader does not challenge the score or move past it. The number is data. The Score → Evaluate → Resolve sequence transforms that signal into a resolved commitment.

Score It
The individual provides their alignment number — a signal of readiness
▼
ScoreSignalYour action
9–10Clear ownership; ready to deliverProceed; confirm and document
7–8Partial alignment — uncertainty remainsMove to Evaluate
5–6Significant concerns affecting confidenceMove to Evaluate; expect multiple concerns
Below 5Expectation not viable in current formDo not proceed — redesign the expectation
Evaluate It
Understand what is preventing full alignment
▼

The leader explores the reasons behind the score — not to challenge it, but to understand it. Use diagnostic questions:

  • “What influenced the score you gave?”
  • “What feels unclear about the expectation?”
  • “What concerns do you have about delivering this outcome?”
  • “Where do you anticipate obstacles or constraints?”

Common concerns that surface: unclear outcome definition, cross-functional dependencies not confirmed, capacity or resource constraints, confidence in the delivery pathway or timeline.

Resolve It
Work with the individual to resolve the concern
▼

“What would help move your alignment from where it is now to a 9 or 10?”

This question shifts the conversation toward solutions. Resolution may involve: clarifying the outcome, adjusting priorities or timelines, identifying additional support, confirming collaboration across the chain. Once resolved, ask the alignment question again to confirm ownership has been established.

After the dialogue, capture three reflections:
1. Alignment Rating — Where does commitment stand?
2. Primary Concern Identified — What surfaced?
3. Action for Elevation — What will move alignment upward?

Write the Resolve question in your own words. Then apply it to a real expectation alignment scenario you are currently facing.

Micro-Climb Summary
Lens 6 synthesis
Awareness

Setting communicates. Establishing secures ownership. The three conditions — Clarity, Capability, Commitment — must all be confirmed. The Expectations Chain and Alignment Dialogue are the mechanisms that close the gap.

Intelligence

Five mechanisms explain why expectation alignment determines execution outcomes: shifting instruction to ownership, reducing ambiguity, surfacing constraints early, strengthening accountability, and building the behavioural foundation of execution.

Extrapolating

Every CXO function has a characteristic tendency to set rather than establish expectations. The first casualty is engagement; the deeper cost is diluted accountability. Surface alignment masks unresolved ownership beneath it.

Integration Zone

The Expectation Architecture (Future → Clarify → Chain → Confirm Ownership) and the Alignment Dialogue (Score → Evaluate → Resolve) create a shared leadership discipline that transforms expectation-setting into a commitment practice.

“Strong performance cultures are not built by telling people what is expected. They are built by ensuring that expectations are mutually understood, realistically supported, and consciously accepted by the people responsible for delivering them.”
