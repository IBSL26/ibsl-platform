Facilitator Guide

Lens 5 — Session Overview

Use this tab to orient yourself before each section. It contains purpose, timing, tone, and key facilitation questions for the full lens.

◆ Lens Intent

Lens 5 bridges strategy and execution accountability. Many organisations have measurement systems but lack management systems. Participants should leave understanding the distinction — and knowing how to build the management side. The emphasis is on design thinking: how to construct a system that drives behaviour, not just tracks it. This is one of the more technical lenses in S2R® — keep the energy applied.

◆ Key Facilitation Questions
  • “In your organisation, are you measuring what happened — or managing what will happen next?”
  • “If your team stopped producing reports tomorrow, what decisions would you lose the ability to make?”
  • “Is your current PM system designed to generate compliance — or to build commitment?”
  • “Where in your organisation does measurement transparency break down? Who does not see their numbers?”
  • “When you review performance, are you looking at activity or at cumulative progress toward target?”
◆ Tone & Watch Points

Avoid: turning the FACES or EXECUTION frameworks into a compliance checklist. They are diagnostic and design tools, not audit instruments.

Watch for: leaders who equate “we have a scorecard” with “we have a PM system.” Probe: “What happens after you look at the scorecard? What changes? Who decides what?”

The Solid Gold reframe (Rating 3 = Solid Gold) is intentional and often powerful. Bring it to life with an example of a consistent contributor overlooked because they never dramatically exceed — they reliably deliver.

Awareness · What

Foundations of Performance Management

Establishing the conceptual ground: what performance management actually is, how it differs from measurement, and the functional architecture that makes it work.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Distinguish between performance measurement (what happened) and performance management (what we do about it)
  • Apply the correct sequence: define success first, then design the management system, then design the measurement infrastructure
  • Describe each of the five FACES functions and their role in a complete PM system
  • Identify the Three Sights of Progress and explain how they operate at individual and organisational levels
  • Apply the 1–5 Rating Scale and articulate the appropriate leadership response for each rating
Measurement vs Management
The core distinction

Performance management is not performance measurement. This distinction is the single most important conceptual anchor in Lens 5 — and the one most organisations get wrong.

Measurement answers: What happened? It is an input — data, results, scores, dashboards.
Management answers: What do we do about it? It is the leadership response that turns data into decisions, actions, and accountability.

Most organisations have built sophisticated measurement systems and mistakenly believe they have performance management systems. A measurement system without a management architecture produces reports. A management system converts those reports into movement.

The Correct Sequence

Step 1
Define Success
▼

Before any system is designed, leadership must be explicit about what success looks like. What are we trying to achieve? By when? At what level of quality or scale? Without this, measurement has no target and management has no compass.

Step 2
Design the Management System
▼

Design the system for managing toward success. What conversations will we have? How often? Who is accountable? What decisions will be made, and by whom? The management architecture must exist before measurement is collected — otherwise data accumulates with no system to act on it.

Step 3
Design the Measurement Infrastructure
▼

Design the measurement tools that will feed the management system. What data do we need? How frequently? In what format? Measurement should serve the management conversations already defined, not generate reports that then search for a purpose.

Step 4
Manage Using Data
▼

Execute the management system using measurement data as the evidence base for conversations, interventions, recognition, and course correction. This is where most organisations start — collecting data before having a system to use it.

◆ Facilitator Note

Ask: “In your organisation, which comes first — the data collection system or the definition of success? How many reports exist that nobody acts on?” Most will recognise that measurement infrastructure was built first and management systems are retrofitted — or entirely absent.

The FACES Framework
Five functions of PM

A complete performance management system serves five interconnected functions. These are simultaneous functions that every well-designed PM system must perform.

F
Foster a Results Culture
▼

The PM system must reinforce a culture in which results matter, are visible, and are celebrated or addressed consistently. Culture is shaped by what gets measured, what gets discussed, and what gets rewarded. A PM system that avoids difficult conversations actively undermines results culture — no matter how sophisticated the dashboard.

Facilitation cue: “What does your PM system reward? Does it reward activity or results? Compliance or commitment?”
A
Align Individual Performance
▼

Every individual must see a clear line of sight between their daily work and the organisation’s strategic outcomes. Without this alignment, effort is not wasted — it is misdirected. The PM system must decompose strategic outcomes into team and individual targets that are genuinely connected, not merely labelled as strategic.

C
Create Measurement Transparency
▼

People perform at a higher level when they can see their own performance data and understand how it is calculated. Transparency is not just about sharing data — it is about ensuring that the logic of measurement is understood and trusted. Hidden formulas and opaque scoring destroy confidence in the PM system.

E
Embed Shared Accountability
▼

The PM system must embed accountability as a shared expectation, not a top-down imposition. Shared accountability means teams co-own the results — they participate in setting targets, understand the evidence, and are part of the intervention process. Systems that locate accountability solely in the leader create dependency and fragility.

S
Support Continuous Development
▼

The PM system must identify what performance reveals about capability gaps, development needs, and growth trajectories. Performance data is a development signal, not just an accountability score. Leaders who use PM data only for ratings miss its most powerful application: designing targeted development that improves future performance.

◆ Facilitator Note

Ask participants to rate their current PM system against each FACES dimension (1–5). Typical gap: most organisations score highest on “Create Measurement Transparency” (they have dashboards) and lowest on “Embed Shared Accountability” (accountability lives with the leader, not the team).

The Three Sights of Progress
What gets measured

A well-designed PM system tracks performance across three distinct categories. Each sight provides a different angle on whether the organisation is progressing toward its strategic intent. At the individual level, weightings are assigned according to role and function.

Sight 1
Strategy Category
What it measures
Strategic OKRs directly connected to the organisation’s multi-year strategy. This sight asks: Are we making progress on the things that matter most for where we are going?
Individual weighting
Typically 30–50% at senior levels; lower for frontline roles. Reviewed quarterly with executive visibility.
Sight 2
Operational Category
What it measures
Departmental KPIs tracking the operational engine — efficiency, output, quality, and service delivery. This sight asks: Are our operations performing at the standard required to sustain strategy?
Individual weighting
Typically 30–50% across most role levels; highest for frontline and specialist roles. Reviewed monthly or fortnightly.
Sight 3
Behavioural & Values Alignment
What it measures
How individuals demonstrate the organisation’s values and leadership behaviours — assessed through 360° feedback and peer observation. How results are achieved matters as much as what results are achieved.
Individual weighting
Typically 20–40% across role levels; higher for leadership and customer-facing roles. Assessed bi-annually or annually via structured 360°.
◆ Facilitator Note

A common gap: organisations measure Sight 1 and Sight 2 rigorously but treat Sight 3 as a subjective add-on. Surface this: “In your current system, what weight does behavioural alignment carry in the final performance rating? Is it measured formally or informally?”

The 1–5 Rating Scale
Ratings & leadership responses

A rating scale only has value if it is consistently understood and each rating triggers a defined leadership response.

Rating 1 — Poor
Leadership response: Immediate PIP within 30 days
▼

Definition: Performance is significantly below the minimum standard. Output is insufficient, quality is unacceptable, or behaviour is actively damaging.

Leadership response: Immediate intervention. A formal performance improvement plan within 30 days. Weekly check-ins. Assess whether the gap is capability, motivation, or role-fit. A Rating 1 sustained beyond 60 days without visible movement is a role-fit decision.

Rating 2 — Needs Improvement
Leadership response: Structured development plan
▼

Definition: Performance is below the expected standard in one or more significant areas. The individual shows some capability but has consistent gaps in output, quality, or behaviour.

Leadership response: Structured development plan with specific targets and milestones. Fortnightly check-ins. Clear articulation of the standard required to achieve a Rating 3 by the next review cycle.

Rating 3 — Meets Expectations  SOLID GOLD
Leadership response: Recognition & retention conversation
▼

Definition: Performance fully meets the standard expected for this role, at this level, in this context. Output is consistent, quality is reliable, behaviour is aligned. This is not “average.” This is exactly what the role requires.

The Solid Gold Reframe: Most PM systems implicitly treat a Rating 3 as mediocre — a design flaw that erodes morale and incentivises performative overreach. A Rating 3 is “Solid Gold”: a person who reliably delivers and represents the backbone of execution in any high-performing organisation.

Leadership response: Recognition of consistent contribution. Exploration of growth aspirations — without implying that remaining at a 3 is a failure. Many exceptional contributors are sustainably and appropriately at a 3.

Rating 4 — Exceeds Expectations
Leadership response: Stretch opportunities; retention investment
▼

Definition: Performance consistently goes beyond what the role requires. The individual proactively adds value, solves problems beyond their mandate, and elevates the team. A Rating 4 is a pattern, not an accident.

Leadership response: Explicit recognition and visibility with senior leadership. Conversation about stretch opportunities, expanded scope, or accelerated development. Failure to invest in a sustained Rating 4 is a retention risk.

Rating 5 — Outstanding
Leadership response: Mandatory structural follow-through
▼

Definition: Performance is transformational within the context of the role. The individual not only exceeds their own targets but demonstrably elevates others and advances the organisation. A Rating 5 is rare and must be ratified, not self-assigned.

Leadership response: A Rating 5 carries a leadership obligation — increased scope, a promotion discussion, a compensation review, or a high-impact project assignment. A Rating 5 acknowledged verbally with no structural follow-through is a trust-destroying event. The individual will note that outstanding performance produced no change — and will adjust their effort accordingly.

◆ Facilitator Note

The Solid Gold reframe generates significant discussion. Invite reflection: “What message has your body language, tone, or incentive system sent to people rated 3?” Also surface the Rating 5 accountability point: “In the last three years, how many people received a Rating 5? What happened to them afterwards?”

Intelligence · Why

PM as an Execution Bridge

Understanding the structural logic of how performance management connects strategy to individual action, and why most PM systems fail to make that connection.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Explain the four functions of PM as an execution bridge
  • Distinguish between compliance-driven and commitment-driven PM architectures across six dimensions
  • Apply the two-step Rules of the Game framework to prepare and execute a performance cascade
  • Articulate the difference between how leaders and teams are measured, and why it matters
PM as the Execution Bridge
Four bridge functions

A performance management system performs four critical bridging functions between strategy and execution. When any one is missing, the bridge develops a structural gap — and strategy leaks through it.

Bridge 1
Decomposing the Big Picture
▼

Strategy lives at the enterprise level. PM translates it into progressively smaller, actionable units: enterprise outcomes → business unit contributions → team targets → individual responsibilities. Without decomposition, strategy remains a declaration and never becomes a task.

Bridge 2
Defining the Logic of Success
▼

The PM system must make explicit what “success” looks like at each level — as a measurable, observable outcome with defined evidence. When the logic of success is ambiguous, individuals define it for themselves — and those self-definitions are rarely aligned.

Bridge 3
Calibrating the Rhythm
▼

Strategy operates on an annual or multi-year timeline. Individual performance operates daily. The PM system must establish a structured rhythm connecting these timelines. Without rhythm, PM becomes an event rather than a system.

Progress per Period = (End Target − Baseline) ÷ Number of Periods
This establishes the expected incremental progress in each review period, creating a visible trajectory rather than a binary pass/fail at year-end.

Bridge 4
Structuring the Dialogue
▼

Data without conversation is noise. The PM system must structure the dialogue that turns performance data into decisions. Define: who speaks, how frequently, about what, and with what authority to decide. The quality of the PM conversation is the quality of the PM system.

◆ Facilitator Note

Ask participants to identify which bridge function is most underdeveloped. “Where does your strategy most commonly leak? At decomposition, at defining success, at the rhythm, or at the quality of the conversation?”

Compliance vs Commitment Architecture
The design choice

Every PM system is built on one of two architectural orientations: compliance or commitment. The choice is rarely made explicitly — but it is always made. The six dimensions below reveal which architecture is currently operating.

◆ Facilitator Note

Most organisations have compliance systems that use commitment language. The test: “When performance data shows underperformance, what is the first instinct of the leader — to fix the person, or to understand the system?” Invite leaders to identify the one structural change that would shift their system most decisively toward commitment.

Rules of the Game
Two steps of the cascade

Before performance can be managed, the rules must be established and understood by everyone expected to play. Two steps structure this process.

Step 1: Prepare the Foundation
  • Rating Scale: Agree the 1–5 scale, definitions of each level, and evidence required to differentiate between them. Ambiguity here creates inconsistency and destroys trust.
  • Performance Thresholds: Define the minimum acceptable standard for each role. What constitutes a sustained Rating 2 requiring intervention?
  • Evidence Standards: Determine what counts as evidence of performance — output volume, quality metrics, peer feedback, customer satisfaction. Must be explicit, not assumed.
  • Review Routine: Establish the cadence: how frequently performance is reviewed, in what format, with what participants. A routine that is skipped destroys PM system credibility.
Step 2: The Cascade
  • Verify the Data: Before any cascade conversation, confirm that data is accurate, current, and consistently interpreted. A cascade built on flawed data produces misaligned targets and erodes trust in the entire PM system.
  • Assign Ownership: Every metric must have a single named owner — not a team, not a function. Collective ownership without individual ownership is shared irresponsibility.
  • Focus on Learning: The cascade conversation is not just target-setting. What does this target reveal about capability requirements? What barriers must be removed? Leaders who conduct the cascade purely as a negotiation miss its most important function.
◆ Facilitator Note

The most common failure in Step 1 is the evidence standard. Ask: “If two managers each gave someone a Rating 4, would they be using the same evidence?” Step 2 often breaks down at “Assign Ownership” — most cascade conversations end with team-level accountability that no single person is actually responsible for.

Leader vs Team: A Critical Distinction
Who is measured on what

One of the most frequently misunderstood aspects of performance management is the distinction between how a team is measured and how its leader is measured. Conflating the two creates misaligned incentives and obscures accountability.

Dimension The Team Is Measured On The Leader Is Measured On
FocusActivities, outputs, and behaviours within the review periodCumulative outcome trajectory across the full performance cycle
Time horizonCurrent period: what was done, how it was doneEnd-of-cycle: are we on track to reach the target by year-end?
EvidenceDeliverable completion, quality scores, behavioural indicatorsCumulative progress calculation; gap to target; trend direction
Review frequencyMonthly / quarterlyQuarterly / bi-annually

The practical implication: A team can complete every planned activity on time (strong team performance) while the leader’s cumulative outcome trajectory is still off track (weak leadership performance). The leader is accountable not just for managing tasks — but for ensuring tasks are producing the outcomes the strategy requires.

◆ Facilitator Note

This is a powerful reframe for leaders who conflate team busyness with strategic progress. “Is your current PM review looking at your team’s activities — or at your cumulative outcome trajectory? When last did you review whether your team’s activities are actually producing the outcomes the strategy requires?”

Extrapolating · Where

PM in the Executive Context

Extending PM architecture to the enterprise level: how executive teams align their measurement signals, where execution gaps emerge, and how industry context is shifting.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Describe the Alignment Brigade concept and the executive team as a measurement alignment system
  • Identify the CXO Hot Zone for each executive function and its primary PM contribution
  • Diagnose the Three Execution Gaps and propose interventions for each
  • Contextualise the PM design challenge against the industry shift toward continuous PM
The Alignment Brigade
Executive team as PM system

At the enterprise level, the performance management system does not sit in a software platform or a policy document. It sits in the executive team. The C-suite is, collectively, the organisation’s measurement alignment system. When the executive team is not aligned on what they are measuring, why, and what they are doing about it, the enterprise PM system is structurally broken regardless of the tools in place.

The Alignment Brigade concept recognises that each executive function carries a unique measurement responsibility — a “hot zone” of the enterprise that they are best positioned to track, interpret, and act upon. When these hot zones are integrated through a shared cadence and common language, the executive team becomes a precision measurement system. When they operate independently, they produce competing narratives of organisational performance.

The critical question for any executive team: “When we look at the same performance data, do we interpret it through the same lens — or through the lens of our function? And when the data tells us to act, do we act as a system or as a collection of functions?”

◆ Facilitator Note

Ask the executive team to independently write down the top three enterprise performance priorities right now — then compare answers. The degree of divergence is a direct measure of Alignment Brigade dysfunction. “If your executive team cannot agree on the top three, how does the organisation below you know where to focus?”

CEO — Enterprise Visibility: The CEO holds the integrated view of whether the organisation as a whole is on trajectory. The CEO’s PM role is to ensure the executive team is interpreting performance coherently and that the collective response is coordinated. The CEO is the only function with authority to enforce measurement alignment across all hot zones. When CEO PM visibility is absent, each function optimises its own metric at the expense of enterprise coherence.

CFO — Financial Signals: The CFO interprets the financial performance signal: revenue, cost, margin, liquidity, and capital efficiency. In the PM context, the CFO translates financial data into strategic resource allocation signals — identifying where investment is producing returns and where it is absorbed without output. When CFO PM insight is isolated, financial data becomes a constraint rather than a strategic navigation tool.

COO — Execution Rhythm: The COO tracks the execution engine: throughput, cycle times, operational quality, and delivery consistency. The COO ensures operational performance rhythm is matched to strategic demand — the engine is not just running, but running in the right direction at the right speed. When COO PM is activity-focused rather than outcome-focused, the organisation confuses motion with progress.

CHRO — Capability & Behaviour: The CHRO tracks the capability and behaviour signal: talent pipeline health, performance distribution, leadership effectiveness, and cultural alignment. The CHRO ensures the organisation has the human capability to execute the strategy — and that behaviour is aligned to values, not just outcomes. When CHRO PM data is absent from executive reviews, capability gaps become execution crises rather than development investments.

CTO/CIO — System Enablement: The CTO/CIO tracks system enablement: technology performance, digital capability, data quality, and system reliability. This function ensures technology is an execution accelerator, not an execution constraint. Poor system performance is a PM variable — it directly affects what people can deliver. When technology performance is excluded from PM reviews, teams are held accountable for gaps caused by system failures.

CMO — Market Signal: The CMO tracks brand perception, customer acquisition costs, pipeline health, campaign performance, and market share trajectory. The CMO provides the leading indicators of future revenue — the signal that tells the enterprise whether the market is responding to the strategy. When CMO PM data is lagged, the enterprise acts on market signals that are already 3–6 months old.

CCO — Revenue Velocity: The CCO tracks win rates, deal cycle times, customer retention, revenue per customer, and commercial pipeline conversion. The CCO translates commercial activity into revenue trajectory signals connecting market position to financial performance. When CCO PM is focused on activity rather than velocity, the organisation mistakes effort for momentum.

CPO — Supply Reliability: The CPO tracks supply reliability, product pipeline health, vendor performance, and input cost management. The CPO ensures the supply and product infrastructure can reliably support execution — that the organisation can deliver what it has committed to deliver. When CPO PM is reactive, supply disruptions become execution crises that consume leadership capacity.

CRO — Risk & Governance: The CRO tracks regulatory compliance, risk appetite alignment, incident frequency, and control effectiveness. The CRO ensures strategic ambition is pursued within a coherent risk framework — enabling bold action without reckless exposure. When CRO PM is siloed, risk appetite is interpreted differently across functions, creating asymmetric exposure.

CSO — Strategic Coherence: The CSO tracks whether the organisation’s current activities, resource allocation, and performance trajectory are aligned to its multi-year strategic intent. The CSO provides the strategic integrity check — ensuring operational performance is not winning at the expense of strategic positioning. When CSO PM is absent, operational urgency systematically erodes strategic intent over time.

◆ Facilitator Note

Invite participants to work through their own role card first, then two closely related roles. Key question: “In your last executive performance review, were all ten hot zones represented? What does the absence of a hot zone from the executive PM conversation produce in the organisation below?”

The Three Execution Gaps
Where PM breaks down

Three structural gaps account for the majority of PM system failures at the enterprise level. Each gap is a predictable consequence of a specific design flaw.

Gap 1
Competing Definitions of Progress
▼

Different functions or leaders are using different definitions of what constitutes progress — finance says margin, operations says throughput, sales says pipeline. Without a unified definition of progress at the enterprise level, each function optimises its own metric at the expense of collective momentum.

Intervention: Establish an enterprise progress definition in the executive PM session. Agree on the three to five metrics that, if achieved, confirm the strategy is working. Everything else is a supporting indicator, not a competing definition of success.

Gap 2
Measurement Blind Spots
▼

The organisation is systematically not measuring something that matters. Common blind spots: quality of cross-functional collaboration, health of the leadership pipeline, customer experience at key touchpoints. What is not measured is not managed — and eventually becomes a crisis.

Intervention: Map the current measurement system against the Three Sights of Progress. Ask: “What would we need to see to know our strategy is working before the financial results confirm it?”

Gap 3
Incentive Misalignment
▼

The incentive structure rewards behaviour inconsistent with the strategic intent. Leaders are rewarded for short-term financial performance while the strategy requires long-term capability investment. People do not ignore the strategy — they follow the incentives. When incentives and strategy diverge, strategy loses.

Intervention: Audit the current incentive structure against strategic priorities. Redesign to reward the behaviours the strategy actually requires. Where are incentives producing the wrong behaviour? Which strategic priorities have no incentive attached?

◆ Facilitator Note

Ask the group to vote (anonymously if needed) on which Execution Gap is most present in their organisation. In most executive contexts, Gap 1 and Gap 3 will dominate. The discussion about why these gaps persist despite being well understood is where the real facilitation work happens. The answer is usually political: fixing the gap requires someone to give up control of a metric or accept shared accountability they have historically resisted.

The Continuous PM Shift
Industry context

41% of organisations have moved to real-time or near-real-time feedback systems, replacing annual or bi-annual performance review cycles with continuous check-in models. This shift reflects a fundamental change in how high-performing organisations understand the relationship between feedback and performance.

Trend 1
From Annual to Continuous
▼

The annual performance review is being supplemented or replaced by structured quarterly conversations, monthly check-ins, and continuous pulse feedback. The shift recognises that annual feedback is too infrequent to change behaviour and too delayed to be developmental.

Trend 2
From Rating-Centric to Development-Centric
▼

Leading organisations are shifting from the rating (a backward-looking judgement) to development planning (a forward-looking investment). The rating becomes an input to development, not the conclusion of the conversation. Associated with higher engagement, stronger retention, and more rapid capability improvement.

Trend 3
From Individual to Team Performance
▼

As organisations become more cross-functional, individual performance ratings are being complemented by team performance assessments. Most outcomes require coordinated contribution — not individual heroics. Organisations that exclusively measure individual performance create hidden disincentives for collaboration.

◆ Facilitator Note

Invite participants to locate their organisation on the spectrum from traditional (annual, rating-centric, individual) to leading-edge (continuous, development-centric, team-integrated). “Where are you now — and where do you need to be in three years?”

Integration Zone · Collective

Designing for Enterprise Performance

Synthesising awareness, intelligence, and context into an integrated enterprise PM design: the collective progress signal, three elements of enterprise PM, and the EXECUTION framework.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Define the Collective Progress Signal and explain how it anchors three types of executive question
  • Apply the three elements of enterprise PM design: Reporting Cadence, Periodic Measures, and Cumulative Progress
  • Calculate cumulative progress percentage using the S2R® formula and interpret the result
  • Apply the EXECUTION framework as a design and diagnostic tool for enterprise PM systems
The Collective Progress Signal
Anchoring executive conversations

At the integration level, the executive team converges on a single shared signal: are we collectively making progress toward our strategic intent? This Collective Progress Signal anchors three categories of executive question.

Question Type 1
Execution Traction
▼

“Are our current activities generating the progress we expected?”

This assesses whether the execution engine is producing results at the rate and quality the strategy requires. Execution Traction questions are the most frequent in a well-functioning executive PM rhythm — they are the standing agenda item in every performance review. The risk: if only Traction questions are asked, the executive team optimises for current-period efficiency and loses sight of cumulative trajectory.

Question Type 2
Execution Balance
▼

“Are we investing our resources in the right proportions across our strategic priorities?”

This steps back from individual metric performance to assess whether the portfolio of activity is balanced. High-performing organisations regularly discover that 80% of their resource is concentrated on the priority representing 30% of their strategic value — because that priority is most familiar or most politically visible.

Question Type 3
Execution Intervention
▼

“Where must we intervene — with resources, decisions, or leadership attention — to prevent a performance gap from becoming a strategic failure?”

The most consequential question in the executive PM dialogue. It moves from analysis to action: a specific decision about where leadership will direct energy, resources, or authority to recover a trajectory that is currently off track. Organisations that defer intervention decisions until the gap is irreversible have compliance PM systems masquerading as management systems.

◆ Facilitator Note

In most organisations, executive PM conversations are dominated by Traction questions with insufficient time for Balance and Intervention. “When last did your executive team have an Execution Balance conversation? When last did a performance review result in an explicit, named Intervention decision with an accountable owner and a deadline?”

Three Elements of Enterprise PM Design
When, what now, how far

A complete enterprise PM design integrates three elements. Each answers a different question at a different time horizon. All three must be present for the PM system to function as a genuine management architecture.

Element 1
Reporting Cadence — When
▼

Reporting Cadence defines the structured rhythm: when performance is reviewed, by whom, and with what mandate. Without a defined cadence, performance management reverts to crisis management — leaders look at the numbers only when something goes wrong.

  • Daily: Frontline operational metrics; team leader visibility
  • Weekly: Departmental progress checks; emerging issues flagged
  • Monthly: Functional performance review; Periodic Measure analysis
  • Quarterly: Enterprise PM review; Cumulative Progress assessment; Intervention decisions
  • Annually: Full PM cycle review; rating calibration; system redesign
Element 2
Periodic Measures — What Now
▼

Periodic Measures are the performance data points collected and reviewed within each reporting period. They answer: What happened in this period? What is our current performance level? Periodic Measures must be:

  • Current: Reflecting performance within the most recent review period
  • Comparable: Measurable against the same standard as previous periods
  • Actionable: Capable of informing a decision or intervention in the next period

A Periodic Measure that is only collected, never acted on, is a reporting cost with no management value.

Element 3
Cumulative Progress — How Far
▼

Cumulative Progress is the most strategically significant element and the most frequently absent. It answers: Across the full performance cycle, how far have we moved from where we started toward where we need to be?

Cumulative Progress % = (Baseline − Current) ÷ (Baseline − Target) × 100

Example: Complaint resolution baseline = 48 hrs, target = 12 hrs, current = 40 hrs.
Progress = (48−40) ÷ (48−12) × 100 = 8 ÷ 36 × 100 = 22% of journey complete.

This formula reveals whether the organisation is on trajectory to reach its target by end of cycle — not just whether the current period improved on the previous one. A single-period improvement can mask a cumulative trajectory that is far behind target.

◆ Facilitator Note

The Cumulative Progress formula is a practical tool participants can apply immediately. Ask them to identify one current strategic priority and calculate their cumulative progress percentage. This frequently reveals that organisations are further behind their end-of-cycle target than their most recent period improvement suggests — a critical insight for Intervention decisions.

The EXECUTION Framework
Nine design principles

The EXECUTION framework synthesises the nine design principles of a high-quality enterprise PM system. Each letter represents a principle that, when absent, creates a specific and predictable PM failure mode. Use as a design tool and a diagnostic.

E
ENTERPRISE
Strategy First

The PM system must be anchored to enterprise strategy, not functional preference. Every metric should be traceable to a strategic outcome. The enterprise intent is the filter through which all PM design decisions are made.

X
eXECUTION
Line-of-Sight

Every individual must see a clear line of sight from their daily work to enterprise strategy. Line-of-sight is the translation mechanism that makes strategy personal. Without it, individuals execute their role description rather than the strategy.

E
EVIDENCE
Evidence-Based Measurement

Performance assessments must be grounded in defined, agreed evidence — not perception or assumption. Evidence-based measurement protects the integrity of the PM system and builds trust. Without it, ratings are subjective and disputes are inevitable.

C
COLLECTIVE
Collective Ownership

Accountability for results must be shared across the team and organisation, not concentrated in a single leader. Collective ownership means everyone with a contribution to an outcome has a stake in its measurement and a responsibility for its achievement.

U
UNDERSTAND
Outcomes Not Activity

The PM system must measure outcomes — the actual change produced — not just activities undertaken. A team can be 100% active while producing 0% of the required outcome. Leaders must measure impact, not effort.

T
TRANSPARENT
Measurement Logic

The logic by which performance is calculated, rated, and rewarded must be transparent to everyone subject to it. Hidden formulas and opaque scoring destroy confidence in the system and generate cynicism that undermines the entire PM architecture.

I
INCENTIVE
Incentive Alignment

The incentive structure must reward the behaviours and outcomes the strategy requires. Misaligned incentives are one of the most reliable predictors of strategy failure. When what gets rewarded diverges from what the strategy needs, people follow the incentive — this is a design failure, not a motivation failure.

O
ONGOING
Review Rhythm

PM must be an ongoing rhythm, not a periodic event. An annual review cycle produces information too infrequently to inform in-year course correction. The review rhythm must be consistent and embedded in the operational calendar so that PM is the way work is managed — not a separate and burdensome process.

N
NAVIGATE
Collective Insight

The ultimate function of a PM system is navigation: using performance data, collectively interpreted, to make better decisions about where to go and how to get there. A PM system that collects data but does not generate collective insight is a reporting system dressed as management.

What is the cost of that gap? What is the one change that would address it?” This becomes the bridge into the Application section.

Application · In Practice

MyHealth Live Portal — PM Design Simulation

Applying the full lens to a realistic case: designing the performance management architecture for a hospital chain launching a digital patient engagement portal.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Apply PM design principles to a realistic organisational context
  • Build a PM Scorecard Architecture connecting strategic outcomes to individual accountability
  • Design scoring logic, reporting cadence, and periodic measures for a live PM scenario
  • Calculate and interpret cumulative progress percentages from baseline data
  • Propose a reporting tool design serving both operational and executive PM needs
◆ Facilitator Note — Simulation Setup

Briefing: Present the MyHealth Live Portal context. Participants work individually through each task first (15 min), then compare outputs at the table. The debrief should focus on design choices, not on whether the “answer” is correct — there is no single correct answer. The richness is in the reasoning.

Suggested timing: Individual work 15 min → Table discussion 15 min → Group debrief 15 min. Tasks 5 and 6 can be combined for groups that move quickly through the earlier tasks.

Case Context: MyHealth Live Portal

Organisation: A hospital chain operating 12 facilities across three regions. The board has approved a digital transformation initiative: the MyHealth Live Portal, a patient-facing platform enabling online appointment booking, real-time test results, and direct clinical communication.

Strategic Context: The portal is a strategic priority for the next 18 months. Three strategic outcomes have been defined by the executive team:

Outcome 1
Reduce patient complaint resolution time: 48 hrs → 12 hrs
▼

Baseline: 48 hours   Target: 12 hours
Key Result: Portal-based complaint submission and tracking live in all 12 facilities within 6 months; average resolution time below 12 hours within 12 months.

Outcome 2
Reduce clinical referral decision time: 10 days → 3 days
▼

Baseline: 10 days   Target: 3 days
Key Result: Digital referral pathway active for 80% of non-urgent referral types within 9 months; average decision time below 3 days within 15 months.

Outcome 3
Increase cross-facility clinical collaboration: 30% → 70%
▼

Baseline: 30% of cases involve cross-facility input   Target: 70%
Key Result: Shared case management module live within 6 months; cross-facility consultation rate at or above 70% within 18 months.

Task 1: PM Scorecard Architecture

Design a PM Scorecard for the Chief Operations Officer. The scorecard must integrate all Three Sights of Progress and reflect the organisation’s strategic outcomes.

  • Identify three to five metrics for each of the Three Sights (Strategic OKRs, Operational KPIs, Behavioural/Values Alignment)
  • Assign a weighting to each Sight that reflects the COO’s role and strategic context
  • Confirm that at least one metric in the Strategic OKR category is directly traceable to the MyHealth Live Portal strategic outcomes
  • Identify the single metric you consider the most critical lead indicator of overall execution traction
Facilitator Notes — Task 1

Task 2: Scoring Logic Design

Design the scoring logic for Outcome 1 (complaint resolution time). The logic must define how performance is translated into a 1–5 rating.

  • Define what performance level earns each rating from 1 to 5 for the complaint resolution time metric
  • Specify what evidence is required to confirm each rating (not just the number — how it is verified)
  • Identify any contextual factors that might legitimately affect the rating (e.g. facility size, patient volume)
  • Describe the leadership response that each rating should trigger in the monthly review
Facilitator Notes — Task 2

Task 3: Baseline Simulation

Using the Cumulative Progress formula, calculate the current cumulative progress % for each strategic outcome. Assume the following data at month 6 of an 18-month cycle:

  • Outcome 1: Current = 40 hrs | Baseline = 48 hrs | Target = 12 hrs
  • Outcome 2: Current = 8 days | Baseline = 10 days | Target = 3 days
  • Outcome 3: Current = 42% | Baseline = 30% | Target = 70%

Cumulative % = (Baseline − Current) ÷ (Baseline − Target) × 100

Expected progress at month 6 of an 18-month cycle = 33%. Which outcome is most at risk? What Execution Intervention would you recommend?

Facilitator Notes — Task 3

Task 4: Reporting Cadence Design

Design the full Reporting Cadence for the MyHealth Live Portal PM system, spanning from daily operational tracking through to the quarterly executive review.

  • For each cadence level (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly), specify: what is reviewed, by whom, and what decisions can be made at that level
  • Identify which CXO Hot Zones must be present at the quarterly executive review and what each must bring
  • Design the escalation rule: at what threshold does a monthly performance gap automatically trigger a quarterly Execution Intervention discussion?
  • How would the cadence need to change at month 12 if Outcome 3 is still below 50%?
Facilitator Notes — Task 4

Task 5: Periodic Measures Selection

Select the Periodic Measures that will be tracked monthly across the three strategic outcomes. Each measure must be current, comparable, and actionable.

  • Identify three to four Periodic Measures for each strategic outcome; specify: what it tracks, how frequently collected, who owns the data, what action it triggers if it deteriorates
  • Identify one leading indicator for each outcome — a measure that signals future performance before the outcome metric itself changes
  • Identify any measures that would represent a Measurement Blind Spot if excluded
Facilitator Notes — Task 5

Task 6: Reporting Tool Design

Design the structure of the reporting tool supporting the MyHealth Live Portal PM system. This is a content and structure design task: what information must the tool present, to whom, and at what level of detail?

  • Executive Summary view: What does the CEO see on a single page that tells them whether the Portal strategy is on track?
  • Operational Detail view: What does the COO see to manage execution and flag issues for escalation?
  • Team Performance view: What does a facility-level team leader see that connects their daily work to the strategic outcomes?
  • Identify the three most critical EXECUTION principles that must be embedded in the tool’s structure
Facilitator Notes — Task 6
Micro-Climb Summary
Lens 5 synthesis
Awareness

PM is not measurement — it is the leadership response to measurement. The correct sequence: define success, design the management system, design the measurement infrastructure, then manage using data.

Intelligence

PM is an execution bridge with four functions: decomposing the big picture, defining the logic of success, calibrating the rhythm, and structuring the dialogue. The architecture choice — compliance vs commitment — shapes every conversation.

Extrapolating

The executive team is the organisation’s measurement alignment system. Each CXO hot zone contributes a distinct signal. The Three Execution Gaps explain most PM failures at enterprise level.

Integration Zone

The Collective Progress Signal anchors three executive question types: Traction, Balance, Intervention. Cumulative Progress calculation reveals whether the trajectory will reach target — not just whether this period improved.

Application

The MyHealth Live Portal simulation applies PM design across scorecard architecture, scoring logic, baseline calculation, cadence design, periodic measures, and reporting tool structure — integrating all lens content into a live design challenge.

“A performance management system that only measures has not yet become a management system. The moment measurement becomes the basis for a decision, a conversation, and an accountable intervention — that is the moment execution begins.”
