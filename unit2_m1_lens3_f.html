Section 1.1 · Awareness — What

What are the ABCV–MBT Integrity Checkpoints?

Interrogative Anchor · Leaders have defined their OKRs. Now the question is not whether they are well-written — but whether they are strategically sound. The ABCV–MBT framework is the stress test for reality.

📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Tab 1 · Awareness

Tab intent: This opening tab introduces the ABCV–MBT framework as a strategic integrity filter. Create a felt sense of productive discomfort: "Are our OKRs actually grounded in reality?" Leaders must feel the danger of unnamed assumptions before they can value the discipline of naming them.

Opening frame: Before content, ask: "How many of you have experienced a well-crafted strategy that fell apart once execution started? Not because of effort, but because of assumptions we hadn't named until they broke?" Pause. Use the silence. Then introduce ABCV–MBT as the tool that names those invisible assumptions before they become expensive failures.

On the checkpoint cards: Invite leaders to expand each card and pause on the MBT condition. Ask them to mentally apply it to one of their own Key Results before the group moves on. Suggested tab time: 20–25 minutes.

1.1.1 — The Problem with Well-Written OKRs
Strategic Context
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Understand why well-written OKRs still fail — and why unnamed assumptions are the primary culprit
  • Recognise the ABCV–MBT framework as a pre-execution stress test for strategic integrity
  • Distinguish between an OKR that is measurable and one that is strategically grounded
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.1.1

Key message: Most strategies don't fail from lack of effort — they fail from unnamed conditions. The cost of discovering those conditions during execution is exponentially higher than naming them now.

Discussion prompt: "Think of a goal that failed in your organisation. Was it a lack of effort, or was there an assumption that turned out to be wrong — one you didn't name until it broke?" Suggested time: 5–7 minutes.

The leaders have done the hard work of defining what 'Success in Practice' looks like. They have translated those visions into OKRs that look great on paper — measurable, ambitious, and with deadlines.

The next step is to move past 'well-written' OKRs and ask: are they strategically sound?

Most strategies don't fail because of a lack of effort; they fail because of 'unnamed conditions.' We often build our goals on a bed of invisible assumptions that we only discover once execution begins and things start to break. By then, the cost of correction is massive.

The ABCV–MBT framework is the 'stress test' for reality. ABCV gives the interrogation structure — the four lenses of strategy. MBT (Must-Be-True) forces leaders to name the 'load-bearing walls' of their goals. For every checkpoint, leaders must identify the specific condition that must remain true for their Key Result to survive. This is not about ticking boxes — it is about ensuring OKRs are anchored in the real world.

The ABCV–MBT Checkpoints serve as a strategic early-warning system. By surfacing conditions now, we don't just plan for success — we build the resilience to handle the moment the world changes.

Section 1.2 · Intelligence — Why

Why Conduct ABCV–MBT Checkpoints?

Ignition Point · Most strategic plans look bulletproof on paper. They wither in execution not from a lack of hustle — but from the weight of unexamined assumptions left unnamed.

📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Tab 2 · Intelligence

Tab intent: Shift leaders from passive understanding to personal conviction. They should finish this tab believing their current planning process has a structural blind spot that this framework corrects. The goal is not comprehension — it is recognition.

Opening provocation: "We instinctively define ourselves by our business cards. But here is the cold truth: industry categories were made for administrators, not strategists." Pause. Let it land. Then ask who has been stung by a competitor they didn't see coming. Suggested tab time: 20–25 minutes.

1.2.1 — The Industry Illusion & the Arena Shift
Core Reframe
Learning Objectives
  • Identify why industry-category thinking creates strategic blind spots
  • Distinguish between an “industry” and an “arena” definition of competitive space
  • Apply the customer end-game lens (Functional, Emotional, Social) to their own context
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.2.1

The Nokia moment: Use the smartphone analogy specifically. Nokia defined themselves as a telecoms company. The smartphone redefined the arena entirely. Ask: "Who in your industry is playing a different game that you're only just starting to notice?"

The three dimensions: After presenting Functional, Emotional, Social needs, ask leaders to apply them to their primary customer. Most find the Emotional and Social dimensions are rarely discussed in their strategy sessions — which is exactly the blind spot. Suggested time: 6–8 minutes.

The cold truth: Industry categories were made for administrators, not for strategists. Customers don't wake up thinking “I need a banking product today.” They think: “I need to feel secure about my family's future.” When we define our world by our industry, we watch our traditional neighbours while a stranger from a different 'industry' walks away with our customers.

The Three Dimensions of Customer Need

Functional

The actual task they need to finish. The concrete outcome they are paying for.

Emotional

How they want to feel while doing it. “I want to feel in control.” “I want this to be effortless.”

Social

How they want others to see them. “I want to be seen as a pioneer.” “I want credibility.”

Facilitator Example — Netflix

Functionally: video. Emotionally: escape. Socially: connection to the global water-cooler conversation. If Netflix had only thought they were in the “DVD-by-mail industry,” they'd be a footnote in history. Ask: "What industry definition is keeping your organisation from seeing its true arena?"

1.2.2 — Naming the Must-Be-Trues
From Ambition to Commitment
Learning Objectives
  • Connect the ABCV structure to the MBT discipline of naming hidden assumptions
  • Articulate why naming Must-Be-True conditions transforms ambition into tested commitment
  • Identify at least one MBT condition for one of their own Key Results
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.2.2

The MBT concept: Help leaders distinguish between a strategy and a hope. Use this language: "Every OKR is built on assumptions. MBT forces you to name them out loud — because if you can't name them, you can't monitor them, and if you can't monitor them, you can't protect your strategy."

Practical check: Ask each leader to try naming one MBT condition for one of their Key Results right now. If they struggle, that difficulty is itself the lesson. Suggested time: 7–8 minutes.

Even a great lens can miss a hidden crack. Every strategy is built on a “load-bearing” assumption — something we believe to be true but haven't said out loud. The MBT turns these whispers into explicit commitments.

ARENAAre we solving a real customer end-game, or just pushing a product?
BOUNDARIESWhat are the “laws of physics” here? What regulations or internal frictions will push back?
COMPETITIONWho else is solving this problem — even the competitors who don't look like us?
VALUE PROPDoes this goal actually make us more distinct, or are we just blending in?

By naming MBT conditions, we transform strategy from a “hopeful ambition” into a tested commitment. The ABCV–MBT checkpoints ensure that when you hit the gas, the wheels are actually attached to the car.

Section 1.3 · Extrapolating — Where

Where Does Directional Integrity Break Down?

The Alignment Brigade · Every executive role is trained to protect a different part of the enterprise system. These specialisations create selective attention — and predictable strategic blind spots across the leadership mandate.

📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Tab 3 · Extrapolating

Tab intent: Make the ABCV framework personal to each leader's role. Not blame — illumination. Leaders should finish knowing which ABCV lens is most likely their organisational blind spot and why it creates specific downstream consequences.

On the hot zone grid: Ask each leader to click their own role card first. Then invite the group: "Look at the gaps across all roles. Which lens appears most frequently as an overlooked dimension?" That answer is the team's most critical strategic vulnerability. Suggested tab time: 20–25 minutes.

What Gets Overlooked

Boundaries. The CEO holds the vision and drives narrative — but operational friction is rarely in the chief executive's focus until execution stalls significantly.

⚠ Symptom: “Why are we not seeing the results when our strategy is clear?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Boundaries lens forces explicit naming of the regulatory, structural, and behavioural friction standing between the vision and its delivery. Vision without friction-mapping is a speech, not a strategy.

✓ Right question: “What specific conditions must remain true for execution to sustain the pace we've planned for?”
What Gets Overlooked

Arena. The CFO tracks whether the model works — but whether the underlying customer demand is still real and growing is often outside the financial reporting frame.

⚠ Symptom: “The model works… so why is performance lagging?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Arena lens reframes financial health as dependent on customer-need relevance. Revenue can be temporarily maintained even as the underlying arena is shifting away from your value proposition.

✓ Right question: “Is the customer end-game we designed this model around still the right end-game?”
What Gets Overlooked

Competition. Operational leaders optimise the machine — but if the machine is serving the wrong arena, efficiency just accelerates the organisation in the wrong direction.

⚠ Symptom: “We've optimised everything — what are we missing?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Competition lens asks: who else is solving the customer's problem, even those who look nothing like us? Operational excellence means nothing if the alternatives are more convenient for the customer.

✓ Right question: “Are our efficiency gains making us meaningfully more competitive against the real alternatives customers have?”
What Gets Overlooked

Competition. People alignment and engagement are the CHRO's primary lens — but a highly engaged team executing the wrong strategy still loses to a competitor solving the problem better.

⚠ Symptom: “Our people are committed — why isn't it translating?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Competition lens reveals that motivation and alignment are necessary but insufficient if strategic direction points at the wrong competitive target. Energy without direction is enthusiasm without results.

✓ Right question: “Are we aligning our people's energy toward the right competitive arena, or building capability for yesterday's contest?”
What Gets Overlooked

Arena. Technical delivery is the CTO's primary accountability — but whether the solution addresses the customer's actual end-game (functional, emotional, social) is often assumed, not tested.

⚠ Symptom: “We delivered the system — why isn't adoption there?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Arena lens asks: did we build for what the customer is actually trying to achieve — or for the specification handed to us? These are not always the same thing. Adoption requires arena-alignment, not just technical delivery.

✓ Right question: “Does this solution address the functional, emotional, and social outcome the customer is actually pursuing?”
What Gets Overlooked

Boundaries. The CMO creates demand — but if the operational infrastructure cannot fulfil that demand, the campaign creates frustration and brand damage rather than growth.

⚠ Symptom: “We created demand — why can't we fulfil it?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Boundaries lens identifies the internal and external friction that must remain within tolerance for demand to convert to delivered value. Demand without delivery capacity is brand erosion, not brand growth.

✓ Right question: “Do our operational boundaries allow us to fulfil the demand our marketing is designed to generate?”
What Gets Overlooked

Value Proposition. Revenue growth is the CCO's scorecard — but growing by winning the wrong business can dilute the organisation's distinctive identity and long-term positioning in ways that compound over time.

⚠ Symptom: “We're closing deals — but at what cost to who we are?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Value Proposition lens asks: does this contract strengthen or dilute our distinctive edge? Not all revenue is strategic revenue. Some growth makes the organisation less coherent, not more competitive.

✓ Right question: “Does this deal sharpen our value architecture or compromise it for short-term volume?”
What Gets Overlooked

Arena. Cost reduction and supply chain resilience are the CPO's primary mandate — but savings that are not connected to the customer's end-game do not translate into strategic progress.

⚠ Symptom: “We've saved money — but has it actually mattered?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Arena lens asks: does this procurement decision improve our ability to deliver on the customer's actual need? Cost efficiency that doesn't serve the customer's end-game is operational tidiness, not strategic advantage.

✓ Right question: “Does this cost initiative strengthen our ability to compete in the arena our customers actually operate in?”
What Gets Overlooked

Boundaries. Strategy clarity is the CSO's domain — but without explicit identification of operational friction, strategies that are coherent in design break down repeatedly in execution.

⚠ Symptom: “Why does execution keep breaking after alignment?”
What the ABCV Lens Reveals

The Boundaries lens identifies structural, cultural, and regulatory friction points that strategy must be designed around — not assumed away. Clarity without friction-mapping is strategy without implementation physics.

✓ Right question: “Which operational friction points have we built our strategy around — and which are we still hoping won't materialise?”

Pacing: Step 1 is individual and takes 15–20 minutes. Steps 2–4 are collective and take 20–30 minutes together. Build in transition time between individual and group phases. Suggested total tab time: 40–50 minutes.

📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Step 1

Briefing for participants: Each person works independently. They select up to three objectives from their current OKR/strategic plan and apply all four ABCV lenses to each. The MBT condition they name must be specific enough that someone else could attempt to verify it. Vague MBTs (“the market must support us”) are not sufficient — push for precision.

Watch for: Participants who struggle to name MBT conditions. This is a signal that the objective itself may not be sufficiently grounded. Use this as productive friction: “If you cannot name a Must-Be-True, what does that tell you about this objective?”

Complete the ABCV–MBT analysis for up to three of your current strategic objectives. For each objective, name one Must-Be-True condition across all four lenses.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify shared ABCV themes and recurring MBT conditions across leadership perspectives
  • Surface areas of genuine strategic disagreement as productive leadership dialogue
  • Arrive at a consolidated set of high-priority MBT conditions the team agrees require verification
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Step 2

Process: Ask each participant to share one MBT condition from each lens they found most revealing — not most comfortable. Look for patterns: which lens recurs as most challenging? Which MBT conditions appear in multiple participants’ papers? These are your organisation’s strategic pressure points.

Key facilitation move: Where participants disagree on what the MBT condition should be, do not resolve the disagreement prematurely. Disagreement on MBTs often signals that the objective itself has not been defined precisely enough. Sit in that discomfort productively. Suggested time: 15–20 minutes.

Capture the team’s consensus MBT conditions and any critical disagreements that require further leadership conversation.

Learning Objectives
  • Assign clear leadership ownership to each priority MBT condition
  • Distinguish between strategic ownership and operational accountability
  • Establish which MBTs are high-priority for immediate verification
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Step 3

Key distinction: “Lead Owner” does not mean the person doing the verification work. It means the person accountable for ensuring the MBT condition is verified before the dependent strategy is executed. Every MBT must have a named owner — shared ownership is no ownership.

Watch for: Leaders who assign themselves as owner of every high-priority MBT — this signals either genuine accountability or reluctance to trust colleagues. Leaders who avoid ownership of any MBT — this signals disengagement from the strategic process. Both are worth naming. Suggested time: 10–15 minutes.

Assign leadership ownership to each priority MBT condition identified in Step 2.

Learning Objectives
  • Map the cross-functional dependencies required to verify each priority MBT condition
  • Identify which external partners or data sources are needed for verification
  • Clarify the handoffs between strategy ownership and verification execution
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Step 4

Purpose: MBT verification rarely falls within the ownership of a single leader. This step surfaces the cross-functional coordination required to confirm or disconfirm each condition. It often reveals that the organisation’s structures make verification harder than it should be — which is itself a strategic insight.

Closing the integration: End this tab with a commitment question: “Which one MBT condition, if left unverified, represents the greatest risk to our strategy’s integrity? Who is leaving this room responsible for that verification?” Suggested time: 10–15 minutes.

💣 Mine 1 — The Ghost Ship

A SaaS platform achieved 85% retention in year one. By year three, churn reached 70%. Nothing in the product changed. Support tickets were low. The platform worked exactly as designed. Post-exit interviews revealed: customers had changed what they were trying to achieve — and the platform never followed.

💣 Mine 2 — The Ferrari in the Mud

A leading logistics firm invested R120m in a new digital operations platform. Eighteen months in, less than 30% of staff were using it consistently. Field teams had developed workarounds. Middle management had quietly reverted to spreadsheets. The system was technically excellent. The organisation was not designed to absorb it.

💣 Mine 3 — The Invisible Disruption

A financial advisory firm was ranked #1 in client satisfaction for four consecutive years. Then revenue began declining. No competitor had taken their clients. No scandal. No service failure. The problem: a fintech app was letting clients self-manage what the firm had always managed for them — and clients preferred it for 60% of use cases.

💣 Mine 3 — The Identity Crisis

A boutique consulting firm built its reputation on deep-sector expertise and bespoke strategy work. Under revenue pressure, they began accepting large implementation contracts — work that paid well but required different skills, different team profiles, and a different culture. Within two years, they won more work but lost their most experienced strategists and their distinctiveness with clients who valued exactly what they had been.

This session was about ensuring strategic direction is structurally sound before the first move of execution is made. We’ve all seen well-written OKRs collapse — not for lack of effort, but because the assumptions holding them up were never tested. The ABCV–MBT framework served as our strategic integrity filter: the stress test to ensure Key Results are anchored in customer reality, resilient to friction, and true to the organisation’s identity.

Here is a recap of how we moved from individual functional perspectives to a unified executive judgement.

1 — Awareness: The Integrity Checkpoints

We began with the What. The ABCV lenses interrogated strategy across four critical dimensions:

  • Arena — The specific customer end-game we are playing for.
  • Boundaries — The operational or regulatory “clog points” that could slow us down.
  • Competition — The alternatives (not just direct rivals) competing for the same need.
  • Value Proposition — The distinctive edge that defines our organisational identity.

For every lens, the team identified the Must-Be-True (MBT) condition — the “load-bearing” assumption that must hold for the strategy to survive. This is the early warning system: exposing risks before they become expensive failures.

2 — Intelligence: Why Strategic Integrity Matters

This stage explored the Why. Most strategies don’t fail because of bad intentions — they fail because of unexamined assumptions.

We shifted thinking from rigid industry categories to Customer Arenas, recognising that customers don’t just buy products — they chase functional, emotional, and social outcomes. By naming MBT conditions early, the team moved the strategy from a hopeful ambition to a battle-tested commitment.

3 — Extrapolating: Locating the Blind Spots

Next, the Where. Every CXO sees the world through a specialised lens:

  • CEOs lean toward narrative and stakeholder credibility
  • CFOs hunt for financial viability and return logic
  • COOs focus on operational feasibility and delivery rhythm
  • CHROs look at culture and leadership readiness

While these perspectives are vital, they naturally create selective attention. Applying the ABCV–MBT lens across all roles surfaced the hidden risks that usually only emerge halfway through the execution cycle.

4 — Integration Zone: Collective Intelligence

The session culminated in the Converging Zone — the moment the team consolidated individual perspectives into a single enterprise interpretation. By reviewing consolidated responses, the team was able to:

  • Identify patterns of agreement and surface strategic disagreements
  • Determine the most critical “deal-breaker” execution conditions
  • Agree on the severity of the MBT requirements

The ABCV–MBT checkpoints have transformed your OKRs from polished aspirations into resilient execution signals, grounded in the hard realities of the enterprise system.
