Performance Gravity — and the KISS Antidote

Understanding the force that pulls organisations back toward average — and the four precise decisions that counter it.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Define Performance Gravity and explain how it threatens sustained performance
  • Identify and distinguish the three mechanisms through which Performance Gravity operates
  • Articulate the four KISS disciplines and explain what each counters
  • Apply the KISS reflection to your own function with honesty and specificity
The Moment After the Win

You have done the hard work. Direction has been clarified, execution has been designed, accountability has been established, enterprise conditions have been aligned, and organisational health has been measured. Momentum is building.

This is the moment most leadership programmes celebrate and close. We do not. Because this is precisely the moment where most organisations begin to lose what they have built — not through failure, not through crisis, but through the quiet force that pulls every organisation back toward the performance of the average.

Performance Gravity is not failure. It is the natural organisational tendency to drift back toward average after periods of strong performance — not because leaders stop trying, but because success quietly erodes the very disciplines that produced it.

✎ Reflection
The Three Mechanisms of Performance Gravity
Select each to explore
Mechanism 1
Momentum Illusion
The belief that past discipline is sustaining present performance
▼

When an organisation achieves strong results, a dangerous misinterpretation follows. Leaders attribute ongoing performance to current capability rather than to the accumulated effect of past discipline. Meetings become slightly less structured. Accountability conversations become slightly less direct. Review cycles become slightly less rigorous. Each individual change is small enough to justify. Together they represent the beginning of a performance decline that will only become visible six to twelve months later.

Mechanism 2
Complexity Creep
The accumulation of weight that once served a purpose and has since become drag
▼

High-performing organisations attract initiatives. Success generates confidence, confidence generates ambition, and ambition generates proliferation. New projects launch, new committees form, new approval pathways add. Each addition appears justified. Collectively they create an organisation that is progressively heavier, slower, and more internally focused. Every unnecessary practice is not merely wasteful in isolation — it is a claim on the organisational attention that should be available for practices that matter.

Mechanism 3
Leadership Drift
The gradual softening of the behavioural standards that produced the original results
▼

The most dangerous form of Performance Gravity operates at the level of leadership behaviour. When performance is strong, the urgency that made difficult leadership behaviours necessary begins to fade. The honest performance conversation becomes slightly more diplomatic. The accountability standard applied with discipline during rebuilding is applied slightly more flexibly when pressure is off. The behaviours leaders relax when performance is strong are precisely the behaviours that produced the strong performance in the first place.

KISS — The Four Disciplines
Keep · Improve · Stop · Start

KISS was introduced earlier in this programme as a simplicity principle. In Lens 11, it reaches its most important application: the leadership discipline that determines whether the organisation sustains everything it has built.

K
KEEP
What is working and must be protected

The conscious decision to identify, name, and actively protect the practices, routines, behaviours, and systems producing reliable results. Effective practices are vulnerable — they depend on specific leadership attention, team behaviour, and structural support. Keep requires leaders to make the implicit explicit: what specifically is producing this result, and what would we need to protect to ensure it continues? Keep is the antidote to Momentum Illusion.

✎ Reflection
I
IMPROVE
What has shifted and requires recalibration

The honest recognition that something which was working well is beginning to work less well — and that the gap between current and required performance is growing slowly enough to ignore but fast enough to be dangerous. Improve is the discipline of responding to the signal before it becomes the crisis. The leadership cost of neglecting Improve: early corrections that would have cost a conversation become later interventions that cost a restructuring.

✎ Reflection
S
STOP
What is harming performance and must be eliminated

The most difficult and most neglected of the four disciplines. Stop requires leaders to apply a single ruthless question to every meeting, process, initiative, reporting requirement, and committee: if we were designing this organisation today, knowing what we know, would we introduce this? The organisations that sustain performance over long cycles are not distinguished by how many things they do but by how precisely they select what they carry.

✎ Reflection
S
START
What needs to be introduced to sustain the next level

The forward-facing investment that sustained performance requires. Start asks: what is the organisation not yet capable of that future performance will require? It requires examining emerging strategic requirements, internal capability gaps, and leadership development needs. Start is the anticipatory discipline — the investment in future fitness that current performance makes possible.

Intelligence · Why

Why Sustaining Performance Requires a Different Discipline Than Achieving It

The finite and infinite game distinction, Success Inertia, and the deep logic of each KISS discipline.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Distinguish between the discipline of achieving and the discipline of sustaining performance
  • Explain Success Inertia and why it is the greatest threat to sustained performance
  • Apply each KISS discipline with the specificity the practice requires
  • Understand why consistency is harder than intensity — and why it matters more

The discipline of sustaining performance does not reward intensity. It rewards consistency — the unglamorous, unrewarded, structurally demanding practice of maintaining the standards and rhythms that produce results, not because the organisation is under pressure, but because the organisation has decided that this is simply how it operates.

The Finite and Infinite Game

Achieving performance is a finite game. It has a target, a timeline, and a finish line. The discipline required is intensity — concentrated effort toward a defined outcome within a specific window of time. When the target is reached, success is declared.

Sustaining performance is an infinite game. It has no finish line. The objective is not to win the game. It is to remain capable of playing — at an increasingly high level — as the rules, the terrain, and the competition continuously change. Leaders who treat the quarterly review as a finish line rather than a checkpoint are applying the correct discipline to the wrong game.

✎ Reflection
Success Inertia — The Greatest Threat

Organisations do not execute strategy in a static environment. The definition of good performance is constantly being recalibrated by the environment itself. What delivers strong results today may deliver only average results in six months — not because the organisation has done anything wrong, but because the environment has moved.

The greatest threat to sustained performance is not failure. It is Success Inertia — the organisational conviction that the formula that produced today’s win will produce tomorrow’s survival. Success Inertia is Performance Gravity in its most advanced form. The KISS disciplines of Stop and Start are designed to interrupt it before it calcifies into institutional resistance to change.

✎ Reflection
Why Stop Is the Most Neglected Discipline

Of the four KISS disciplines, Stop is the one most organisations understand intellectually and most consistently fail to practise. Every initiative launched has a sponsor, a team, a budget, and stakeholders whose interests are served by its continuation. Starting things generates visible activity and social reward. Stopping requires acknowledging that a previous decision was wrong or at least no longer relevant.

The discipline of Stop requires applying one ruthless question to every meeting, process, initiative, report, and approval layer: if we were designing this organisation today, knowing what we know, would we introduce this? If the honest answer is no, the question is not whether to stop it but when and how.

The organisations that sustain performance over long strategic cycles are not distinguished by how many things they do. They are distinguished by how precisely they select what they carry. Every unnecessary practice is a claim on the organisational attention that should be available for the practices that matter.

Select your role to read your domain.

CEO
Strategic Coherence
▼
How Drift Emerges
The strategic narrative fragments. Business units interpret priorities differently. Initiatives proliferate without connection to intent.
Early Warning Signals
Leadership meetings reveal different interpretations of priorities. Teams emphasise activity over strategic outcomes. Initiatives compete for attention without a clear hierarchy.
CFO
Capital Discipline
▼
How Drift Emerges
Resource allocation responds to historical spending and internal influence rather than strategic priorities. Strategic initiatives struggle for funding.
Early Warning Signals
Strategic initiatives underfunded while legacy activities fully resourced. Budget conversations focus on cost containment rather than strategic investment.
COO
Execution Cadence
▼
How Drift Emerges
Performance reviews become less rigorous. Escalation pathways slow. The rhythm that maintained execution momentum becomes a reporting exercise.
Early Warning Signals
Review meetings shortened or postponed. Operational issues persist across multiple cycles. Teams rely on ad hoc interventions rather than structured escalation.
CHRO
Organisational Health
▼
How Drift Emerges
Trust and clarity begin to erode. Leaders become less direct. Teams begin protecting information rather than sharing it.
Early Warning Signals
Reduced willingness to surface problems early. Increased interpersonal friction. Health Thermometer scores declining on Politics and Morale traits.
CTO / CIO
Technology Enablement
▼
How Drift Emerges
Systems that once accelerated execution become bureaucratic infrastructure. Integration gaps create manual workarounds that become normalised.
Early Warning Signals
Teams create and rely on manual workarounds. Fragmented data systems slow decision-making. Leaders spending time reconciling data rather than acting on it.
CMO
Market Intelligence
▼
How Drift Emerges
Depth of customer intelligence declines as attention turns inward. Marketing continues but reflects yesterday’s customer rather than today’s requirements.
Early Warning Signals
Customer satisfaction metrics decline subtly before visibly. Product adjustments lag behind shifting expectations. Competitor responsiveness accelerates relative to the organisation.
CCO
Revenue Conversion
▼
How Drift Emerges
Sales rigour softens as pipeline appears healthy and recent wins create confidence. Win rates decline before revenue figures reveal the pattern.
Early Warning Signals
Pipeline growth without proportional revenue. Declining win rates. Increasing sales cycle duration. Price sensitivity increasing — often a signal that value proposition communication has weakened.
CPO
Supply Resilience
▼
How Drift Emerges
Procurement decisions prioritise short-term cost reduction over strategic partnership and supply resilience. Critical dependencies develop without visibility.
Early Warning Signals
Supplier reliability declines. Cost volatility increases. Procurement conversations focus narrowly on price at expense of resilience and long-term value.
CRO
Risk Intelligence
▼
How Drift Emerges
Emerging risks acknowledged but not integrated into executive decision-making. Risk conversations become compliance exercises rather than strategic guidance.
Early Warning Signals
Increasing operational surprises. Risk discussions reactive rather than anticipatory. Risk registers maintained but not influencing strategic choices.
CSO
Strategy Translation
▼
How Drift Emerges
Strategic intent remains clear at executive level but operational translation becomes uneven. OKRs drift from strategic objectives.
Early Warning Signals
Teams struggle to connect operational priorities to strategic direction. OKRs feel like performance management targets rather than strategic commitments.

The S2R® Collective Intelligence Review

The four instruments built across this programme brought together as a quarterly operating rhythm that detects drift early and sustains performance across strategic cycles.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Explain why the S2R® Collective Intelligence Review is an operating rhythm, not a reporting session
  • Describe the four sequential conversations and what each produces
  • Understand how individual insights combine to produce collective execution intelligence
  • Commit to the quarterly cadence as the mechanism that sustains performance

The four instruments built across this programme — ESRG Scanner, Enterprise COMPASS, KISS Reflection, and Organisational Health Thermometer — form a single integrated quarterly operating rhythm: the S2R® Collective Intelligence Review. Select each tool to understand its role.

ESRG Scanner
Led by: COO, CTO/CIO, CHRO, CFO · Conversation: Diagnose the Operating Conditions
▼

Examines whether the organisation’s Environment, Systems, Resources, and Governance conditions are enabling or constraining strategic delivery. Surfaces structural bottlenecks not visible within individual functions.

  • Is the environment enabling the collaboration and clarity that execution requires?
  • Are systems allowing work, data, and decisions to move smoothly across the organisation?
  • Do teams have the capability, capacity, and sustainability required?
  • Are decision rights clear and accountability standards applied consistently?
Enterprise COMPASS
Led by: CEO, CSO · Conversation: Align the Enterprise
▼

Evaluates whether the organisation remains aligned across seven critical execution zones: Strategic Direction, Organisational Alignment, Management Discipline, People and Capability, Resource Allocation, Sensing and Adaptation, and Systems and Execution.

  • Are leaders at every level describing strategic priorities in consistent language?
  • Are the seven execution zones strengthening, stable, or showing signs of deterioration?
  • Where is execution maturity advancing — and where is it at risk of regressing?
KISS Reflection
Led by: All CXOs for their domains · Conversation: Reinforce the Performance Discipline
▼

Each executive shares what their function is Keeping, Improving, Stopping, and Starting. Converts individual functional experience into collective enterprise learning. The value is in the patterns that emerge when multiple KISS reflections are examined together.

  • What are we Keeping — and what protection mechanism has been designed for it?
  • What early correction is being made before the drift becomes a gap?
  • What is being stopped — and what organisational weight does that release?
  • What is being started — and what future capability does that build?
Organisational Health Thermometer
Led by: CHRO, CEO · Conversation: Monitor the Behavioural Climate
▼

Reviews health signals emerging across teams. Thermometer readings are shared and compared. Where early warning signals appear, leaders apply ACE-IT behavioural guardrails to stabilise the environment and restore healthy execution conditions.

  • Which teams are showing declining scores — and on which of the five health traits?
  • Where is variance between functions significant enough to indicate structural issues?
  • Which ACE-IT guardrail is most urgently needed?

When conducted consistently, the S2R® Collective Intelligence Review produces collective execution intelligence — the shared, real-time awareness of where performance is strengthening, where it is drifting, and what specific leadership action is required to sustain the trajectory.

Lens 11 · S2R® Programme Close

The final micro-climb of the S2R® Strategy2Results® programme addresses the question that no strategy programme asks but every organisation eventually faces: now that performance has been achieved, how is it kept?

Awareness — The What

Performance Gravity operates through Momentum Illusion, Complexity Creep, and Leadership Drift. KISS — Keep, Improve, Stop, Start — provides the four precise decisions that counter each mechanism at every performance cycle.

Intelligence — The Why

Sustaining performance is an infinite game requiring consistency, not a finite game requiring intensity. Success Inertia — the conviction that yesterday’s formula produces tomorrow’s survival — is the greatest threat. Stop and Start interrupt it.

Extrapolating — The Where

Performance drift emerges simultaneously across ten executive domains. Each CXO guards a specific execution stabiliser, monitoring specific gravitational pull and applying KISS before drift becomes deterioration.
