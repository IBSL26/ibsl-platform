Lens 7 — Session Overview

Use this tab to orient yourself before each section. It contains the lens intent, key facilitation questions, tone guidance, and watch points for the full session.

◆ Lens Intent

Lens 7 closes the loop opened by Lenses 5 and 6. KPIs have been established, targets have been set, and expectations have been secured. Now the most important discipline of execution begins: the performance review conversation. This lens makes that conversation structured, honest, and useful — not a compliance ritual, but a genuine dialogue about execution reality.

The centrepiece is the Review–Realign–Restore Cycle. Leaders must leave this lens understanding that measurement is not reporting — it is an execution recovery system. The Accountability Loop role play in Application is non-negotiable: it is where the conceptual work becomes a leadership skill.

The Integration Zone raises the lens to enterprise level through the Collective Intelligence Circle, shifting leaders from functional reporting to shared execution intelligence.

◆ Key Facilitation Questions
  • “Think of the last significant performance gap your organisation experienced. When did you first see it coming? What would have had to be true for you to see it three months earlier?”
  • “Does your organisation measure activity or progress? What’s the difference in your context?”
  • “If you had to activate the Accountability Loop with a member of your team tomorrow — which of the three root causes would you most likely be diagnosing? And are you equipped for that conversation?”
  • “When your executive team last reviewed performance together, were you seeing functional KPIs or were you seeing the enterprise execution system? What’s the difference?”
  • “At the end of your last review cycle, did your organisation learn anything — or did it just record scores?”
◆ Tone & Watch Points

Watch for: leaders who conflate reporting with reviewing. Reporting describes what happened. Reviewing examines why, and determines what must change. Probe: “If your dashboard went dark for 60 days and you only had conversations to guide you — how confident would you be about execution progress?”

Watch for: leaders who treat the Accountability Loop as a confrontation tool. It is a diagnostic discipline. Its purpose is to restore momentum, not assign blame. If the root cause conversation feels uncomfortable, that discomfort is a signal that the culture is still treating performance gaps as verdicts rather than data.

Watch for: leaders in the Integration Zone who revert to functional reporting in the Collective Intelligence Circle. The circle is explicitly designed to surface cross-functional dependencies. If every CXO only discusses their own function, the exercise has failed. Push the horizontal conversation.

Timing: Awareness and Intelligence can move at pace. Extrapolating benefits from a brief discussion of real functional examples. Reserve adequate time for the role play in Application and for the full three rounds of the Collective Intelligence Circle.

Awareness · What

What is Performance Measurement?

Establishing performance measurement as an execution visibility discipline — and introducing the Review–Realign–Restore Cycle as the mechanism that keeps strategy correctable.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Distinguish between activity visibility and progress visibility in organisational performance
  • Articulate the four strategic purposes of performance measurement: visibility, accountability, learning, and course correction
  • Explain how the Review–Realign–Restore Cycle converts measurement data into execution action
  • Describe the three stages of the cycle and the specific role of the Accountability Loop in Stage 2
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Awareness

Open with the core provocation: "Work is happening. Teams are busy. Initiatives are underway. But can you confidently say the work being done is actually advancing the strategy?" This question unlocks the gap that this entire lens addresses — the difference between activity visibility and progress visibility.

The four strategic purposes are the conceptual anchor. Spend time here. Most leaders can name visibility; fewer spontaneously name learning and course correction. These are the purposes that make performance measurement a leadership discipline rather than a reporting system.

The Review–Realign–Restore Cycle is the operational centrepiece. Walk each stage with precision: Review establishes evidence-based visibility; Realign diagnoses root cause; Restore rebuilds momentum. The key facilitation moment is when leaders understand that most performance gaps have a diagnosable cause — and that the three root causes (accountability, skill, structural misfit) require fundamentally different responses.

From Activity to Progress — The Visibility Shift
Core concept

The performance management system has been set and mechanisms for establishing performance are in place. Commitments have been secured. Now the most important discipline of execution begins.

Strategy execution does not fail at the level of intention. It fails at the level of visibility. Performance measurement is the discipline that makes execution visible. It answers a simple but decisive question:

Are we actually moving closer to our Success in Practice?

Most organisations are extremely good at measuring activity: how many projects were launched, how many meetings were held, how many deliverables were completed. These indicators provide evidence that work is occurring. But activity alone does not indicate whether the organisation is actually moving closer to the strategic outcome it set out to achieve.

Performance measurement therefore asks a different set of questions. Instead of asking "What work was done?" the organisation begins asking "What changed because of the work?" Instead of reporting effort, the organisation examines evidence of progress. This shift transforms performance management from an administrative reporting exercise into an execution intelligence system.

The Four Strategic Purposes of Performance Measurement
Click each to explore

At its core, performance measurement serves four strategic purposes within the organisation. Select each to explore.

Purpose 01
Creates Visibility
▼

Leaders can observe where the organisation is gaining momentum and where it is stalling. Strategic priorities that once existed only as intentions begin to appear as measurable progress signals. Without measurement, leaders are flying blind — confident in effort but uncertain about direction.

Facilitator prompt: “Where in your organisation does execution happen in the dark? Where are you most likely to be surprised at the end of a reporting period?”
Purpose 02
Establishes Accountability
▼

When KPIs are clearly linked to enterprise OKRs, ownership becomes visible. Leaders and teams understand which part of the strategy they are responsible for advancing. Accountability without measurement is aspiration without a feedback loop — it depends entirely on personal commitment rather than organisational structure.

Facilitator prompt: “If your KPIs disappeared from your scorecard but the targets remained unchanged — how many would still be actively owned?”
Purpose 03
Enables Learning
▼

Performance reviews reveal patterns about how work actually unfolds inside the organisation. Leaders begin to see which approaches accelerate progress and which create friction or delay. Over time, disciplined review conversations accumulate into an organisational understanding of how execution actually works — a capability that compounds over successive strategy cycles.

Facilitator prompt: “What did your organisation actually learn from its last strategy cycle? Not what it achieved — what it learned about how execution works inside your system?”
Purpose 04
Enables Course Correction
▼

Early signals allow teams to intervene while progress is still recoverable. Problems are addressed when they are small enough to resolve rather than discovered only when targets have already been missed. This purpose is why measurement frequency matters: a signal detected in week four is fundamentally different from the same signal detected in week twelve.

Facilitator prompt: “What is the average gap in your organisation between when a performance problem begins and when leadership first becomes aware of it? What does that gap cost?”
The Review–Realign–Restore Cycle
3 stages

Within the S2R® system, performance measurement operates through a continuous execution cycle called the Review–Realign–Restore Cycle. Its purpose is to ensure that measurement does not end with a score being recorded. Instead, measurement becomes the starting point of a leadership conversation that protects momentum, strengthens accountability, and restores progress when execution drifts.

Stage 1
Review
Stage 2
Realign
Stage 3
Restore
Establishing Evidence-Based Visibility

The cycle begins with Review. The purpose is to establish a clear and shared understanding of what progress has actually occurred. Execution reality must be grounded in evidence rather than perception.

In the S2R® system, the review process begins with the KPI owner conducting a first-level self-assessment. The individual examines the agreed targets and assesses progress against the defined rating scale — submitting verifiable evidence that demonstrates what progress has occurred since the previous review period. Evidence may include operational data, financial results, project milestones, customer metrics, or any other proof that demonstrates whether the intended activity produced the expected movement.

At the same time, the leader conducts a parallel assessment — independently evaluating the level of progress achieved. When both perspectives are brought into the review conversation, the organisation avoids two common distortions: the individual evaluating without challenge, and the leader imposing evaluation without understanding operational realities.

  • Three questions anchor the review:
  • Did the agreed activity actually occur?
  • Did the activity produce the expected progress toward the target?
  • What does the available evidence tell us about execution reality?
Diagnosing the Source of Performance Gaps

The second stage begins when the review reveals a gap between expected progress and actual progress. Execution gaps are not unusual — in complex organisations they are inevitable. The purpose of realignment is not to assign blame but to diagnose the true source of the gap so the organisation can intervene intelligently.

When a gap is identified, the leader activates the Accountability Loop — a structured diagnostic process designed to restore traction and ownership without collapsing into unproductive performance pressure. Most performance gaps emerge from one of three underlying conditions:

  • Lack of accountability — the individual understands the task but has lost the motivational connection to it. Recovery: reconnect purpose and ownership by revisiting the broader organisational context of the KPI.
  • Lack of skill — the individual is committed but lacks the capability required to achieve the expected level. Recovery: targeted coaching, peer support, formal training, or timeline adjustment.
  • Misfit of role or structural constraint — the individual's strengths do not align with the specific responsibilities. Recovery: structural alignment discussion, task redistribution, or repositioning. Note: the performance conversation must remain separate from the role conversation.
Rebuilding Momentum

Once the root cause has been identified and addressed, the cycle moves to Restore. The purpose is to convert insight into concrete forward action — rebuilding momentum and re-establishing execution traction.

  • Corrective actions are defined clearly
  • Specific next steps are agreed
  • Responsibilities are reaffirmed
  • Follow-up timelines are established

The KPI owner leaves the conversation with a renewed understanding of what must happen next and by when. The leader confirms how progress will be monitored going forward. This stage ensures that the review conversation produces visible forward movement, not just reflection.

Within the S2R® system, the alignment state of the KPI is also updated at this stage. The revised state reflects whether the enabling conditions for execution — clarity, capability, and ownership — are now fully restored, providing an ongoing signal of organisational readiness.

Intelligence · Why

Why Performance Measurement Determines Execution Velocity

Understanding why the discipline of review conversations — not the sophistication of the scorecard — is the factor that determines whether strategy execution sustains momentum or quietly stalls.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Explain why execution discipline — not strategy design — is the primary determinant of execution velocity
  • Identify the four predictable consequences of weak review discipline and their cumulative effect on strategy
  • Recognise the three progress signals that surface in healthy review conversations and respond appropriately to each
  • Articulate why consistent performance reviews build leadership credibility as well as organisational momentum
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Intelligence

The key shift in this section is from measurement as tool to measurement as leadership discipline. Most leaders accept the logic of measurement; fewer have internalised that the rhythm of review conversations is itself a strategic capability — and that allowing that rhythm to weaken has predictable, compounding consequences.

The four consequences of weak review discipline are cumulative, not isolated. Missed early warnings lead to diffused accountability; diffused accountability suppresses learning; suppressed learning erodes leadership credibility. Walk this chain deliberately so participants can recognise it in their own organisations.

For the three signals: Progress Confirmation is not neutral — it is a leadership opportunity to reinforce the behaviours that generated momentum. Many leaders skip past confirmation because there is nothing to fix. Redirect them: reinforcing what works is as strategically important as correcting what doesn't.

Execution Discipline — The Real Differentiator
Core argument

The difference between organisations that sustain execution momentum and those that quietly stall rarely lies in the elegance of their strategy design. Most leadership teams are capable of producing sophisticated strategic plans.

The real difference emerges after the strategy is launched. It appears in the discipline with which leaders return to the strategy and ask a difficult but necessary question: Are we actually progressing?

Execution discipline is the organisational habit of repeatedly examining progress, confronting evidence honestly, and adjusting behaviour before small deviations become systemic failures. Without this discipline, even well-designed strategies slowly lose traction.

Performance measurement must be understood not simply as a reporting system but as an execution discipline. The difference between the two determines whether performance management becomes a compliance ritual or a genuine engine of execution progress.

Four Consequences of Weak Review Discipline
Click each to explore

When performance reviews occur inconsistently or superficially, several predictable patterns begin to emerge. These are not random failures — they are the foreseeable consequences of allowing the review rhythm to weaken.

Consequence 01
Early Warning Signals Are Missed
▼

Execution rarely collapses suddenly. Performance deterioration typically begins with small, subtle indicators — a milestone that slips by a few days, a process bottleneck beginning to slow delivery, a capability gap emerging in one team. These signals often appear months before the final KPI target is at risk. But if leaders are not regularly examining performance evidence, these signals remain invisible. By the time the problem becomes visible at the end of the reporting period, the opportunity for early intervention has already been lost.

Facilitator prompt: “In your experience, what is the typical lag between when a performance problem begins and when leadership acts? What would closing that lag by half be worth to your organisation?”
Consequence 02
Gradual Diffusion of Accountability
▼

Accountability is sustained through visibility. When review conversations disappear or become irregular, the psychological signal shifts. Targets remain written on the scorecard, but they no longer appear in leadership dialogue. KPI owners begin to interpret the absence of review as an indication that the target is no longer an active organisational priority. The KPI quietly moves from being operational to being aspirational. Work continues, but the urgency associated with the strategic outcome begins to fade.

Facilitator prompt: “Which KPIs in your current scorecard are operational and which have quietly become aspirational? What made the difference?”
Consequence 03
Loss of Organisational Learning
▼

Every review conversation contains valuable insight about which approaches accelerate progress, where operational friction exists, and which cross-functional dependencies are working and which are slowing things down. When review conversations are reduced to occasional reporting exercises, results are recorded but the organisation never pauses to understand why those results occurred. Without reflection, improvement becomes accidental rather than intentional. The organisation's execution capability stagnates even as it accumulates more strategic ambition.

Facilitator prompt: “What does your organisation know now about how execution works inside your system that it didn’t know eighteen months ago? If the answer is ‘not much,’ that is a review discipline signal.”
Consequence 04
Erodes Leadership Credibility
▼

When leaders set ambitious targets and communicate strategic priorities, they create an expectation that those priorities will remain visible in the leadership conversation. If those targets are not revisited through structured performance discussions, a subtle message spreads: the strategy announcement was more symbolic than operational. Teams become less attentive to new strategic commitments because they have learned that priorities often quietly fade. Consistent performance reviews counteract this by demonstrating that strategic priorities are not temporary announcements but ongoing leadership commitments.

Facilitator prompt: “What is the current level of strategic commitment in your organisation? How much of that is shaped by what leaders consistently review versus what they announce?”

Extrapolating · Where

Where Does Performance Measurement Break Down Across Functions?

Examining where the Alignment Brigade — the collective leadership system — unintentionally weakens the performance measurement discipline through four characteristic fractures.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Recognise that performance measurement breakdown rarely occurs due to absent KPIs — it occurs in how leaders review and interpret measurement together
  • Diagnose the four characteristic fractures: horizontal coordination gap, evidence quality gap, review rhythm breakdown, and learning suppression gap
  • Identify which fracture is most prevalent in their own organisation and its downstream consequences
  • Articulate what each CXO function must do to protect the integrity of the collective measurement discipline
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Extrapolating

Begin by establishing the key provocation: "Performance measurement rarely breaks down because organisations lack agreed goals and KPIs. Most modern organisations have dashboards, reporting tools, and performance frameworks. The problem is rarely the absence of measurement. The breakdown usually occurs in how leaders review and interpret the measurement together."

The four gaps are not theoretical — they are observable patterns. Invite participants to place their organisation honestly on each one. The Horizontal Coordination Gap is typically the most impactful but least visible: each function may report strong progress while the enterprise outcome remains inconsistent. The Learning Suppression Gap is often the most culturally embedded and the hardest to shift because it requires leaders to move from a judgment posture to a curiosity posture.

Use the CXO lens to make this concrete: ask participants to identify which gap their function is most likely to create — and what the first-order impact of that gap is on the organisation.

Performance measurement is ultimately a leadership conversation discipline. When that discipline weakens, several predictable fractures appear across the organisation. Select each gap below to examine where it occurs and what it costs.

Gap 01
The Horizontal Coordination Gap
▼

One of the most common breakdowns occurs when performance reviews happen inside functions rather than across functions. Each department reviews its KPIs independently. Finance examines financial indicators. Operations reviews throughput. Marketing examines campaign performance. Individually, these conversations may be rigorous and technically sound.

But strategy execution rarely moves through a single function. Enterprise outcomes depend on interdependent execution across multiple functions. When performance reviews remain confined within functional boundaries, leaders see partial performance signals rather than the full execution pattern. Each function may report strong progress while the overall strategic outcome remains inconsistent.

The Alignment Brigade closes this gap by introducing periodic cross-functional performance reviews where the executive team collectively examines how the KPIs across functions interact to produce enterprise outcomes.

Facilitator question: “Name a strategic outcome in your organisation that required three or more functions to execute successfully. Where did the handoffs break down — and would a cross-functional review have caught it earlier?”
Gap 02
The Evidence Quality Gap
▼

Performance measurement loses power when review conversations rely on weak or inconsistent evidence. In some cases KPI owners present narrative explanations rather than verifiable indicators. In others, the evidence reflects activity rather than progress toward the outcome. Sometimes information arrives too late to support timely decisions.

When evidence quality declines, review conversations shift away from execution insight and toward interpretation debates. Leaders begin discussing whether the data can be trusted rather than what the data reveals about progress. Over time, the credibility of the performance system weakens.

Closing this gap requires the Alignment Brigade to reinforce a simple but powerful principle: every performance review must be anchored in verifiable evidence that shows observable movement toward the outcome, is timely enough to inform decisions, and is clearly connected to the KPI, target, or Key Result being assessed.

Facilitator question: “In your last performance review, what percentage of the conversation was spent debating the data versus acting on it? That ratio is a proxy for your evidence quality gap.”
Gap 03
The Review Rhythm Breakdown
▼

Even well-designed performance systems weaken if the organisation does not maintain a consistent rhythm of review conversations. Strategy execution unfolds over time. Progress appears gradually through incremental movement across weeks and months. If review conversations occur too infrequently, leaders lose visibility into the trajectory of that movement. Small deviations accumulate unnoticed until they become significant performance gaps.

Irregular review cycles also weaken behavioural discipline. KPI owners begin to treat measurement as a reporting event rather than a continuous execution responsibility.

Healthy performance measurement requires a predictable organisational rhythm. Leaders know when reviews will occur. KPI owners know when evidence must be prepared. Teams expect progress to be examined regularly. This rhythm creates a cultural signal: execution progress is not monitored occasionally — it is observed continuously.

Facilitator question: “How predictable is your organisation’s review rhythm? If a KPI owner was asked to describe the review schedule without referring to a calendar, could they?”
Gap 04
The Learning Suppression Gap
▼

A subtle but corrosive breakdown appears when performance reviews focus exclusively on evaluation. If review conversations concentrate only on whether targets were achieved, teams often become cautious about discussing obstacles openly. People instinctively defend results rather than explore the deeper causes behind them. This dynamic suppresses organisational learning.

Yet strategy execution is inherently experimental. Organisations are constantly discovering what works, where friction exists, and how coordination across functions actually unfolds. Healthy performance reviews therefore balance accountability with curiosity. Leaders examine evidence rigorously, yet they also explore what the organisation is learning from execution patterns.

Questions shift from “Why was the target missed?” to “What is the system teaching us about how execution actually works?” When this learning dimension becomes part of review conversations, performance measurement evolves from a scoring mechanism into a capability-building system.

Facilitator question: “What is the dominant mood in your organisation’s performance review meetings — defence or curiosity? What would shift it?”
Integration Zone · Collective

Cross-Leadership Performance Intelligence

Building the Collective Intelligence Circle — the mechanism through which the executive leadership team transforms individual PM review signals into a shared view of enterprise execution momentum.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Articulate why no single function can safeguard the integrity of performance measurement alone
  • Explain the purpose and structure of the Enterprise Performance Intelligence Circle
  • Conduct each of the three rounds: Collective Trajectory, Early Warning Signals, and Execution Learning
  • Use the Synthesis Layer to convert collective signals into enterprise decisions
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Integration Zone

The shift in this section is from function to enterprise. The critical reframe is the question that must guide the entire zone: “Is the chain of execution across our functions holding together?” This is a fundamentally different question from “Is my function achieving its KPI?”

The three rounds work in sequence — do not allow leaders to skip ahead or blend them. Round 1 establishes the baseline. Without the collective trajectory being visible first, Round 2 warning signals lack context and Round 3 learnings lack a system to learn about.

The Synthesis Layer at the end is where the real intelligence emerges. Protect time for it. If the session runs short, compress the individual round discussions but do not sacrifice the synthesis conversation — that is where the enterprise decision is made.

Key facilitation discipline: every contribution must be evidence-based. When a CXO makes an assertion without evidence, redirect immediately: “What does the PM review data tell you? What does your evidence say?” This single discipline separates a Collective Intelligence Circle from an opinion forum.

The Collective Intelligence Principle
Why enterprise performance requires collective review

Because performance measurement spans the entire organisation, no single function can safeguard its integrity alone. The responsibility rests with the executive leadership collective. The collective intelligence ensures that performance reviews remain connected to enterprise outcomes rather than fragmenting into isolated departmental conversations.

This means regularly stepping back and asking deeper questions:

  • Are our review conversations revealing how execution is truly unfolding?
  • Are the signals we are observing early enough for corrective action?
  • Are we interpreting the evidence together or only within our functions?
  • Are we learning from execution patterns or simply recording performance scores?

The conversation shifts from asking “Is my function achieving its KPI/KR?” to asking “Is the chain of execution across our functions holding together?” When leaders observe performance horizontally in this way, coordination improves and performance measurement begins to reveal the true state of enterprise execution, not just isolated functional success.

The Enterprise Performance Intelligence Circle — Three Rounds
Step through each round

The executive team gathers around a shared board or digital canvas. Each leader contributes three signals drawn directly from their PM review sheets. The process unfolds in three disciplined rounds.

Round 1
Collective Trajectory
Round 2
Early Warnings
Round 3
Execution Learning
Establishing the Collective Trajectory of Strategy Execution

Each leader begins by stating the current percentage progress toward their KR/KPIs. This percentage is not an estimate or narrative opinion — it is derived from the PM ratings and evidence documented in their PM review sheet.

The contribution follows a simple structure: the leader states the overall progress signal, briefly identifies the evidence supporting that signal, and clarifies whether execution momentum is accelerating, stable, or slowing.

  • Example: The COO may report that operational readiness KPIs show 72% progress toward the strategic Key Result, supported by throughput improvements and cycle-time reductions documented in the review sheets.
  • Example: The CFO may report that financial indicators show 65% progress toward the quarterly profitability milestone, supported by margin improvements and cost optimisation evidence.

As each leader shares their progress signal, the circle begins to reveal a pattern. Some functions may show strong forward momentum; others may show slower movement toward their targets. The executive team begins to see the collective trajectory of strategy execution.

Surfacing Early Warning Indicators

Once the progress signals are visible, the circle moves to the second signal: early warning indicators. Each leader shares one or two warning signals that emerged during their PM review conversations. These signals must again be grounded in evidence drawn from the review sheets.

Early warnings typically appear before a KPI/KR is formally off target. They often emerge as subtle patterns that indicate potential future friction. Examples might include:

  • An operational process beginning to slow delivery times
  • A capability gap that may affect upcoming milestones
  • A supply constraint that could delay implementation of strategic initiatives
  • A change in customer feedback signalling emerging dissatisfaction

The purpose of sharing these signals is not to escalate problems but to surface emerging risks early enough for collective intervention. When these warnings are shared across the circle, leaders often discover that the signal appearing in one function is connected to activity in another — what appeared as an isolated operational issue may reveal itself as a cross-functional execution dependency.

Capturing Execution Learning

The final round focuses on execution learning. Every review conversation produces insight about how the organisation actually executes strategy. During this round, each leader shares one key learning emerging from their PM reviews. This learning must be anchored in evidence from the review conversations.

  • The CHRO may highlight that performance review discussions revealed a leadership capability gap affecting accountability conversations
  • The CTO may share that system visibility improvements significantly accelerated operational decision-making
  • The CMO may highlight a pattern in customer experience data that revealed which service interactions most strongly influence trust

These learnings allow the executive team to develop a shared understanding of how execution works inside the organisation. Over time, the organisation accumulates a body of practical execution intelligence that improves how strategy is implemented.

Many organisations conduct performance reviews. Few organisations convert those reviews into collective strategic intelligence. The Collective Intelligence Circle ensures that insights emerging from PM review sheets do not remain isolated within functions — they are brought together in a structured conversation that allows the Alignment Brigade to observe execution as a living system.

The Synthesis Layer — Enterprise Intelligence Decisions
Closing the circle

After the three rounds, add a synthesis layer to the board. This is where the collective signals are interpreted and a shared enterprise decision is made. The session must end with a clear enterprise decision — not a summary of what was discussed, but a concrete action agreed by the Alignment Brigade.

Integration Application · In Practice

Building the Collective Intelligence Board & The Accountability Loop

Two structured exercises: building and running the Enterprise Performance Intelligence Board across three zones, and activating the Accountability Loop through a structured role play.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Build and populate a three-zone Enterprise Performance Intelligence Board using evidence from PM review sheets
  • Conduct a disciplined Accountability Loop role play using the Review–Realign–Restore framework
  • Diagnose root cause (accountability, skill, or structural misfit) without defaulting to pressure or blame
  • Capture concrete corrective actions and restore alignment toward 9–10
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Application

Exercise 1 (Board Structure) works best when participants use their real PM review data rather than hypothetical inputs. If participants have completed PM review sheets from their organisations, this exercise produces genuine enterprise intelligence. If not, work from the three examples in the Intelligence section as proxies. The board must end with a populated Synthesis Layer and a named enterprise decision.

Exercise 2 (Role Play) is non-negotiable. This is where the Accountability Loop becomes a practised skill rather than a described concept. The most common failure mode is that the CXO in the role play defaults to pressure rather than diagnosis. If this happens, pause and redirect: “You’ve identified the gap. Now diagnose the cause. Is this an accountability issue, a skill gap, or a structural constraint? What evidence do you have for that?”

Allow adequate time for observer feedback after the role play. The observer evaluation criteria are specific — use them. The quality of the debrief determines how much of the skill transfers into actual leadership practice.

Review–Realign–Restore in practice
► Scenario

You are a CXO responsible for the OKR: “Reduce customer complaint resolution time from 48 hours to 12 hours.”

The latest performance review shows that progress has stalled and complaint resolution is still averaging 36 hours. The target deadline is six weeks away. You must now activate the Accountability Loop.

👤 CXO Role

Conducts the accountability conversation. Must diagnose the root cause without blaming. Uses the Accountability Loop to identify whether the issue is accountability, skill, or structural misfit. Aims to reach an alignment of 9–10.

👤 Report Role

Responsible for execution of the improvement initiative. Responds authentically to the conversation. May exhibit defensiveness, uncertainty, or genuine constraint — reflecting real organisational dynamics.

👤 Observer Role

Evaluates the quality of the conversation against the criteria below. Provides structured feedback after the role play. Does not intervene during the exercise.

Accountability Loop Diagnosis

The CXO must determine whether the root cause is:

Root Cause A
Lack of Accountability

Individual understands the task but has lost motivational connection. Recovery: reconnect to purpose and enterprise context.

Root Cause B
Lack of Skill

Individual is committed but lacks capability required. Recovery: coaching, training, timeline adjustment.

Root Cause C
Misfit / Structural Constraint

Strengths do not align with responsibilities. Recovery: structural alignment, redistribution, or repositioning.

Observer Evaluation Criteria

Criterion 01
Evidence Was Used in the Discussion
▼

Did the CXO anchor the conversation in verifiable performance data rather than opinion or assumption? Was the 36-hour average cited specifically? Were trends, milestones, or other evidence referenced? A conversation without evidence quickly becomes a debate about effort rather than progress.

Criterion 02
Leader Avoided Blame and Focused on Diagnosis
▼

Did the CXO approach the conversation with curiosity rather than judgment? Was the tone diagnostic rather than prosecutorial? Key signal: if the Report became defensive and the CXO escalated pressure in response, the blame dynamic was activated. A skilled leader redirects defensiveness by returning to evidence and narrowing to root cause.

Criterion 03
Root Cause Was Correctly Identified
▼

Did the CXO arrive at a root cause diagnosis through genuine dialogue — or did they assume one at the outset? The root cause must emerge from the conversation, not be imposed on it. A misdiagnosed root cause leads to a mismatched recovery response that will fail even if executed well.

Criterion 04
Clear Corrective Actions Were Agreed
▼

Did the conversation end with specific, time-bound corrective actions that both parties understood and accepted? Was an alignment score agreed? Was a follow-up mechanism established? A review conversation that does not produce a named next action has not completed the Restore stage of the cycle.

Micro-Climb Summary — Performance Measurement
Lens 7 synthesis
Awareness

Performance measurement is not a reporting exercise but an execution visibility discipline. KPIs only become meaningful when regularly reviewed through structured conversations grounded in evidence. The Review–Realign–Restore cycle establishes the accountability loop through which leaders examine progress, identify gaps, and restore execution momentum.

Intelligence

The rhythm of performance reviews determines execution velocity. When review conversations are disciplined and evidence-based, early warning signals appear before performance failure occurs. Accountability remains active, organisational learning accumulates, and leaders can intervene early enough to sustain momentum.

Extrapolating

Performance measurement weakens when reviews remain confined within functions, evidence quality is inconsistent, review rhythms become irregular, or conversations focus only on evaluation rather than learning. These four fractures reduce the organisation’s ability to observe execution as a coordinated enterprise system.

Integration Zone

The Collective Intelligence Circle synthesises performance insights through three rounds: collective trajectory, early warning signals, and execution learnings. Through this structured exchange, the leadership team develops a shared view of enterprise execution momentum, surfaces emerging risks early, and captures learning that strengthens future performance.

“The result is an organisation that does not simply measure performance. It continuously restores performance. And that capability is one of the defining characteristics of organisations that consistently convert strategy into results.”

◆ Facilitator Closing Note

Close by asking each leader to name one performance KPI they own that has been consistently reviewed — and one that has been allowed to drift. Ask them to commit to scheduling its next review within five working days, with a structured evidence submission required before the conversation. The five-day commitment makes the discipline visible rather than aspirational.
