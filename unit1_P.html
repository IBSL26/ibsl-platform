Section 2.1 — Defining Behavioural Strategy
The Core Concept
By the end of this section, you will
  • Define behavioural strategy in your own words
  • Understand the core question behavioural strategy asks of every leader
  • Distinguish behavioural strategy from traditional strategic analysis

Behavioural strategy merges strategic management theory with insights from cognitive and social psychology. Grounded in the work of scholars such as Daniel Kahneman and advanced by thinkers like Olivier Sibony, this discipline brings rigour to how decisions are actually made — not just how they are presented.

It acknowledges that strategic choices are never purely objective. Instead, they are the final output of invisible mental filters — shortcuts, ignored data, and trusted patterns — that operate beneath the level of conscious analysis. By identifying these biases, leaders can transition from making decisions based on unexamined belief to making decisions based on rigorous, debiased evaluation.

Where traditional strategy asks 'What should we do?'

Behavioural strategy asks an equally important prior question: 'How does the way we think shape what we believe we should do — and are those thinking patterns helping or hurting us?'

"The way we think"

Refers not to intelligence or expertise, but to the mental processes running beneath our decisions — the shortcuts we use, the patterns we trust, the data we unconsciously ignore. These processes are largely invisible to us, which is precisely what makes them dangerous in high-stakes contexts.

"What we believe we should do"

Is where those hidden processes surface. Every strategic recommendation, every resource allocation, every competitive call — each is filtered through the thinking of the people who made it. The analysis may look objective. The thinking behind it rarely is.

"Helping or hurting us"

Is the strategic stakes. The same mental shortcuts that make experienced leaders fast and decisive can also make them systematically wrong — overconfident about forecasts, blind to disconfirming signals, or trapped in consensus thinking.

So, the question points directly to a practical problem: if we do not understand how we're thinking, we can't evaluate whether our thinking is sound.

Behavioural Strategy: The application of insights from psychology and behavioural economics to reduce the impact of cognitive biases, emotional distortions, and group dynamics on strategic decisions — enabling leaders to make better choices about direction, resource allocation, and competitive positioning. (Sibony, and others)

Section 2.2 — The Architecture of Human Thinking
Dual-Process Framework
By the end of this section, you will
  • Understand the dual-process framework developed by Kahneman
  • Distinguish System 1 (intuitive) from System 2 (analytical) thinking
  • Recognise which system tends to dominate in high-pressure strategy sessions

To understand this internal hidden thinking architecture, the dual-process framework developed by Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman provides a powerful lens for understanding how strategic thinking unfolds in practice.

Kahneman's dual-process framework reveals that strategic thinking is a dynamic interaction between two internal systems rather than a single linear path. System 1 provides fast, pattern-based intuition, while System 2 offers the slow effortful analysis required for complexity.

For those with a mandate for strategy management, the ability to recognise which system is dominant is the difference between a reactive impulse and a disciplined well thought through choice.

🧠 System 1 vs System 2 — The Two Thinking Modes ▼
SYSTEM 1 — Fast Thinking
Automatic, intuitive, emotionally driven
  • • Operates below conscious awareness
  • • Draws on pattern recognition and past experience
  • • Produces rapid, confident judgements
  • • Vulnerable to cognitive biases
  • • Dominant in high-pressure strategy sessions
In practice: The gut feeling a leader has about a market opportunity — formed in seconds from years of experience.
SYSTEM 2 — Slow Thinking
Deliberate, analytical, effortful
  • • Requires conscious attention and cognitive effort
  • • Analyses data, weighs options, considers trade-offs
  • • Slower but more reliable for complex problems
  • • Easily overridden by System 1 under time pressure
  • • Requires psychological safety to activate
In practice: The structured analysis that follows — or should follow — to validate or challenge that gut feeling.

Section 2.3 — Functional Bias: The Elephant Perspective
Collective Coherence
By the end of this section, you will
  • Understand how functional expertise creates invisible filters
  • Recognise the "Elephant Perspective" challenge in collective strategy-making
  • Identify where silo thinking creates white space in your organisation

While the dual-process framework explains how individual judgement is formed, strategy in organisations is rarely an individual act. It is a collective process, shaped by multiple leaders — each bringing their own experience, expertise, and perspective.

Even when each individual is thinking clearly within their own context, the organisation as a whole can still drift into misalignment. Not because people are wrong — but because they are seeing different parts of the same reality.

Every executive leader develops deep expertise within their domain. That expertise is essential — it drives competence, speed, and confidence in decision-making. But it also creates invisible filters. These filters shape: what leaders notice, how they interpret information, what they prioritise, and what they believe needs to be done.

When left unexamined, these differences do not surface as disagreement — they surface as execution friction: delayed decisions, misaligned KPIs, teams moving with energy but in different directions.

The Elephant Perspective — Read in Full▼

There is an old story — told in many forms across many cultures — about six men who are blind from birth. One day they are led to an elephant, an animal they have heard spoken of but never encountered, and each is invited to discover it.

The first man reaches out and touches the side of the elephant. It is vast, flat, and solid. "An elephant," he declares, "is like a wall."

The second finds a tusk and wraps his hands around its smooth, curved length. "Not at all," he says. "It is like a spear."

The third takes hold of the trunk as it curls and twists. "You are both wrong. It is like a snake."

The fourth encircles a leg and feels the rough, pillar-like mass beneath his palms. "It is a tree trunk."

The fifth grasps the ear and feels its wide, flat sweep. "It is a fan."

The sixth grabs the tail. "It is a rope."

Each man is telling the truth. And each man is completely wrong. None of them is lying. None is careless. Each has gathered real, tactile evidence and drawn a reasonable conclusion. The gap is not in their observation. The gap is in their assumption that the part they touched is the whole they needed to understand.

The classic "Elephant" parable illustrates a structural requirement for strategic leadership: the shift from individual accuracy to collective coherence.

At the enterprise level, strategic quality is determined by an organization's ability to: Surface these partial truths; Hold them in tension; Integrate them into a unified understanding of reality.

Section 3.1 — Functional Bias in Practice: The MyHealth Case
Interactive CXO Explorer

If behavioural biases were merely random fluctuations, they could be treated as statistical noise. Instead, they are systematic, predictable, and cumulative. Left unmanaged, they act as a silent rot, compromising the integrity of even the most technically rigorous strategic frameworks.

When a strategic direction is announced, every executive in the room hears the same words. But they do not hear the same message. Years of professional training shape how leaders interpret opportunity, risk, and priority.

The CFO listens for financial exposure. The COO hears operational feasibility. The CHRO listens for capability implications. The CMO hears the customer promise. Each interpretation is rational. Each perspective is valuable. But when these perspectives remain unspoken, functional bias begins quietly reshaping the strategy.

The White Space — The Panoramic Scan▼

"If every single person in this room performed perfectly within their own silo, would we actually get the result we're after?"

Despite perfect scores, the Emergency Rooms saw a 40% spike in 'panic visits' from patients who received raw, uninterpreted lab results at 9:00 PM on a Friday. Customer Satisfaction (NPS) plummeted because patients felt overwhelmed, not 'empowered'.

This is the WHITE SPACE — the unspoken friction between the functions — the hand-offs, the emotional context of the patient — that no one owned, even though everyone hit their targets.

The "White Space" is the gap between functional mandates where no one is technically "in charge," but where the patient/customer/value experience actually lives.

Three-point check to colour the white space:

  1. Does my "Done" actually create more work for you? — Vertical Success
  2. If the system is "Green" but the customer/employee is "Red," where do we neutralize the impact? — White space
  3. Has the enterprise won? — Enterprise Consequence

Every strategy begins with a reading of reality. Leaders scan the external environment, assess internal capability, and form a view of what is true about the organisation's position. This diagnostic work provides the foundation on which everything else is built. It is also where the first — and most consequential — distortions enter.

The problem is that the human mind cannot process all available information equally. It selects. And the criteria for selection are rarely objective.

The Biases That Shape Diagnosis

Availability Bias governs what gets noticed. Information that is recent, vivid, or frequently discussed is weighted far above its actual strategic significance. A competitor's recent product launch or a difficult quarter just closed can dominate diagnostic conversations in ways that distort the overall picture.

Anchoring Bias governs what gets used as a reference point. The first number on a slide, the previous year's result, the analyst's initial estimate — these become gravitational centres around which all subsequent thinking orbits.

Confirmation Bias closes the loop. Once leaders have formed an initial view — however provisional — they unconsciously seek information that supports it.

A strategy built on a flawed diagnosis is not a strategy. It is a well-organised response to a problem the organisation has imagined.

Status Quo BiasGroupthinkCareer-Risk BiasSilo Bias

This phase should be the most generative. It is where imagination, expertise, and creative tension produce the range of paths from which a choice will eventually be made. In practice, it is often the most constrained — not by a decision to limit options, but by the invisible narrowing that happens before options are ever formally tabled.

The Biases That Narrow the Option Space

Status Quo Bias is the most pervasive. The current strategy naturally pulls thinking toward what already exists. Instead of exploring bold or different options, teams tend to make small adjustments to the current approach.

Functional and Silo Bias means options tend to be generated within organisational lanes rather than across them. The genuinely transformative options — those that require cross-functional integration — are structurally disadvantaged.

Career-Risk Bias is perhaps the most damaging and least visible. Leaders have a well-developed instinct for what it is safe to propose. Radical options carry personal risk. So they do not get advocated. They may not even get thought.

The options that reach a decision are not the best available options. They are the options that survived a social and professional filter that operated before the formal process began.

OverconfidenceLoss AversionAuthority BiasHalo EffectSunk-Cost

The decision phase is especially vulnerable because of how people think under pressure. As a choice gets closer, the need for certainty replaces comfort with uncertainty. Confidence increases, even when not fully justified. Dynamics in the room — who has authority, who is most respected — start to influence the outcome more than the actual evidence.

The Biases That Distort the Chosen Path

Overconfidence Bias inflates the perceived quality of the chosen option. Once a direction is favoured, its upsides are weighted generously and its risks are managed down.

Loss Aversion tilts the scales in a particular direction. When two options are genuinely close in expected value, the one that protects existing positions will almost always prevail.

Authority Bias and the Halo Effect determine whose judgement prevails. The most senior voice, the most admired leader, the external advisor with the strongest reputation — these receive disproportionate weight regardless of the evidence.

The moment of decision is not when judgement is at its clearest. It is when the pressure to commit is highest and the tolerance for challenge is lowest.

Planning FallacyIncentive BiasSelf-InterestConfirmation Bias

The strategy is set. The decision has been made. At this point, leaders often assume that the main risks are behind them. This is a mistake. Bias does not end at execution — it changes form and can become even more costly.

The Biases That Erode Execution

The Planning Fallacy means the execution plan was almost certainly undercooked from the start. Timelines were too optimistic. Resource estimates were too lean.

Confirmation Bias in its action-reinforcing form means green indicators are noted, amber ones rationalised, red ones attributed to temporary factors. By the time the evidence is undeniable, the window for course correction has narrowed considerably.

Incentive Bias fractures alignment. Leaders pursue what they are rewarded for. When incentives and strategy diverge, strategy loses. Consistently.

Execution failure is rarely an implementation problem. It is a continuation of the same behavioural problems that shaped the strategy — now playing out in a context where they are harder to see and more expensive to correct.

5.1 — The Emotional Climate as Atmospheric Pressure▼
By the end of this section, you will
  • Understand how emotional climate shapes strategic thinking capacity
  • Identify the three climate types and their consequences
  • Assess the dominant emotional climate in your own leadership environment

The Emotional Climate of the organization or team is the atmospheric pressure that determines whether the organization's "Thinking Architecture" functions or freezes. As Quy Nguyen Huy's research suggests, emotions are the "gatekeepers" of System 2 thinking. Emotions control whether careful thinking happens.

In a climate of fear or ego, people rely more on instinct (System 1) and less on deliberate, analytical thinking (System 2). The emotional tone set by senior leadership shapes how the organisation thinks and responds over time.

For a leader, managing the emotional climate is a fiduciary responsibility. If you allow a climate of fear or narcissism to take root, you are effectively dismantling the organization's ability to process reality.

  • Friction Load — the amount of hidden resistance and drag in decisions and collaboration
  • Psychological Safety — the extent to which individuals feel safe to speak, challenge, and admit uncertainty
  • Values Alignment — the consistency between stated values, observed leadership behaviour, and everyday decisions under pressure

Unacknowledged emotional states silently override rational plans.

5.3 — Six Principles for Debiasing Strategy Management▼
By the end of this section, you will
  • Understand six practical principles for debiasing your strategy process
  • Identify which principles are already active in your environment
  • Design one concrete intervention you could apply immediately

Awareness alone does not change outcomes. Without deliberate intervention, the same biases will continue to shape strategic conversations and decisions. What matters is how strategy processes are designed — who speaks, how options are generated, how challenge is introduced, and how decisions are tested.

1
Separate ideation from evaluation.

Apply this principle whenever options, solutions, or responses are being generated — whether in strategy design, problem-solving, or execution reviews. Protect the generative phase from early criticism. Premature judgement reduces the range and quality of options considered.

2
Name the emotional temperature before the strategic content.

At the start of any critical conversation — strategy, performance, or course correction — surface the emotional state in the room. A simple question such as "What emotions are people bringing into this discussion?" helps shift the group from reactive thinking to deliberate engagement.

3
Assign a designated challenger role.

In all high-stakes decisions — not just strategy formulation — assign a rotating challenger role. The responsibility is to test assumptions, question prevailing views, and present credible alternatives. This ensures decisions are pressure-tested before and during execution.

4
Use pre-mortem analysis as standard practice.

Apply pre-mortems not only before setting direction, but also before major initiatives, launches, or execution phases. Ask: "If this fails, what are the most likely reasons?" This surfaces risks early, improves planning quality, and strengthens execution readiness.

5
Make the reference class visible.

At every stage — planning, execution, and review — ground decisions in real-world evidence. Compare current plans and performance against similar initiatives in comparable conditions. This reduces overconfidence, improves forecasting, and anchors judgement in reality.

6
Create after-action protocols, not post-mortems.

Institutionalise regular, structured learning reviews throughout execution — not only at the end. Focus on understanding what the environment revealed, and how thinking and decisions shaped outcomes. This builds an ongoing learning system.

Connecting the Dots

This is about breaking down the "I'm an expert in my area" silos. If Marketing only sees customers and Finance only sees costs, they are both looking at a fragmented reality.

The Goal: Build a single version of the truth.

How it works: You stop letting departments "defend" their own data. Instead, you force them to explain how their information affects everyone else.

In Practice: A "Stress Test" — "If the Finance data is right, does the Marketing plan still make sense?"

Putting Up Safety Nets

Smart people make bad choices when they rush. Guardrails are simple rules that force your brain to slow down and switch from "Gut Feel" (System 1) to "Logic" (System 2).

The Goal: Stop the "loudest voice" or the "fastest talker" from winning by default.

How it works: You build "friction" into the meeting. You make it a requirement to disagree before a final vote is taken.

In Practice: The Challenge Clock — 10 minutes dedicated to only finding reasons why the plan won't work.

Clearing the Air

If a room is full of fear or big egos, no one tells the truth. Stabilization is about making the environment "safe" enough for high-level thinking.

The Goal: Move the team from defensiveness to curiosity.

How it works: Leaders must admit they don't have all the answers. This lowers the "atmospheric pressure" and lets the team speak up.

In Practice: Naming the Feeling — "I know we're all anxious about this deadline. Let's acknowledge that before we look at the numbers."

Unit Summary
Foundation Complete

Unit 1: Foundation — Behavioural Strategy Fundamentals establishes the psychological bedrock that underpins all strategic decision-making.

It opens by defining behavioural strategy as the intersection of cognitive psychology and strategic management — making the case that strategy is not a purely rational exercise, but a deeply human one shaped by invisible mental filters. The unit introduces Kahneman's dual-process framework (System 1 fast thinking vs. System 2 deliberate thinking) as the core lens through which leaders should understand their own judgement.

From there, it maps 20 cognitive biases across four families — Action-Oriented, Stability, Interest, and Social — showing how each distorts strategic decisions in predictable, measurable ways, and what the organisational warning signals look like in practice.

The unit then traces where these biases show up across the four phases of any strategy process: Diagnosis, Option Generation, Decision, and Execution — demonstrating that bias is not just a personal failing but a process vulnerability. It adds a second dimension through the emotional climate, showing how fear, ego, or grounded confidence in a leadership environment either amplifies or dampens the quality of thinking.

It closes with practical intervention — six debiasing principles, three pillars for building collective intelligence, and institutional practices for embedding behavioural discipline — culminating in a self-scoring leadership assessment across five commitments.

The central argument of the unit, in one line: the quality of your strategy is a direct function of the quality of the thinking environment in which it was made.
