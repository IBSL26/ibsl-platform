Lens 9 — Session Overview

Orientation, facilitation questions, tone guidance, and watch points for the full lens.

◆ Lens Intent

Lens 9 opens Grounding — Pillar 3 of the DIG framework. The central question is not strategic but systemic: is the organisation's operating system configured to sustain what Direction defined and Influence mobilised? Most strategies fail not because the plan was wrong, but because the internal operating conditions were never aligned to carry the weight of sustained execution.

This lens introduces the ESRG framework — Environment, Systems, Resources, Governance — as the four domains that collectively determine whether performance can be sustained over time. It also introduces two practical instruments: the Cultural Operating Code (translating values into measurable behavioural norms) and the ESRG Execution Scanner (the quarterly diagnostic that operationalises collective intelligence).

The Application exercise — Build the Cultural Operating Code — is the centrepiece. Protect the full 55 minutes. Teams must work with real values and leave with at least three draft norms they can actually use.

◆ Key Facilitation Questions
  • “Most strategies that survive the first quarter fail by the third. What does that tell us about where execution actually breaks down?”
  • “Think of a value your organisation states. What would a leader who genuinely lives that value do differently from one who only states it? That difference is your Cultural Operating Code.”
  • “Which of the four ESRG domains is most misaligned with your current strategic direction — and how long has that misalignment been visible?”
  • “When your team completed the ESRG scanner individually and compared results — what pattern did you see that no single person could see alone?”
  • “A COC norm only becomes real when a leader uses it in a difficult conversation. Which norm from today will you use first — and when?”
◆ Tone & Watch Points

Watch for: the compliance response. When leaders see the ESRG framework, some immediately jump to “we already do this.” Push past self-assessment to evidence: “What would your team say if we asked them whether psychological safety actually exists in your function?”

Watch for: values that sound good but mean nothing. In the COC exercise, every norm must pass the pressure test — does it hold when a senior leader violates it, or does it only apply to junior staff?

Watch for: the ESRG scanner being treated as a performance report. Emphasise: this is a diagnostic, not an assessment. The purpose is intelligence, not judgment. A leader who surfaces a HIGH breach is not admitting failure — they are exercising exactly the discipline the lens teaches.

Timing: Awareness and Intelligence can move at pace. Extrapolating benefits from brief cross-functional discussion of which friction patterns are most recognisable. Reserve the full 55 minutes for Application.

ESRG Alignment — The Grounding Question

Enterprise alignment is one of the most decisive success factors in strategy execution. Even the most carefully designed strategy will struggle to deliver consistent results if the organisation's internal operating conditions are not configured to support it.

This programme builds on the Freedman & Elliott model for high-performance cultures, which recognises that performance is not sustained by strategy alone. It is sustained by the organisational system that surrounds the strategy — the environment people work in, the systems they rely on, the resources they are given, and the governance structures that guide decisions and enforce accountability.

When ESRG alignment is present, execution becomes coordinated, predictable, and scalable. When alignment is absent, friction appears — delays, confusion, rework, decision bottlenecks, and capability gaps that gradually erode execution momentum until strategic intent and operational reality are no longer recognisable as the same thing.

Psychological Conditions
  • Psychological safety so people surface problems early rather than managing impressions
  • Protection from aggression, disrespect, or hostility — safety is a performance condition
  • Onboarding that transmits culture deliberately — culture is either taught or it defaults

Systems
Operational Infrastructure of Execution
▼

Systems determine how work flows, how progress is tracked, and how coordination happens across teams and functions.

Process Clarity
  • Core processes documented, current, and easy to find
  • Handoffs between teams mapped and visible with clearly defined roles
Data Integrity
  • A trusted single source of truth for key operational data
  • Reporting that reflects operational reality, not curated narratives

  • Teams can see where work sits in the end-to-end process
  • Dashboards update fast enough to support real-time decision-making

  • Systems communicate to eliminate duplicate work and data inconsistency
  • Shadow systems and informal trackers controlled and rationalised
Governance Integration
  • Approvals and sign-offs simple, time-bound, and traceable within the system
  • Compliance checks embedded in the workflow — not added after the fact
R
Resources
Capability Capacity of the Organisation
▼

Resources determine whether the organisation has the human, financial, and operational capacity to deliver strategic commitments without degrading its people or performance quality.

Workforce Capacity
  • Staffing levels supporting normal and peak demand without chronic overload
  • Burnout is a strategic risk, not only a wellbeing concern
Capability Alignment
  • Skills matching current work requirements with talent planning anticipating future needs
  • Training accompanying every change in process, system, or strategic direction
Financial Support
  • Budget available for critical strategic initiatives
  • Bureaucratic delays to operational investment are a governance failure, not a resource problem
Operational Resilience
  • Backup coverage for critical roles
  • Knowledge transfer before key staff rotate or exit — institutional knowledge must not reside in individuals
Employee Sustainability
  • Teams able to signal unsustainable workloads early — before performance degrades or talent exits
  • Administrative support so specialists focus on high-value work
G
Governance
Accountability & Decision Discipline
▼

Governance ensures that execution remains visible, accountable, and actively managed — and that the organisation's decision-making architecture supports rather than obstructs strategic momentum.

Clear Accountability
  • Each area of strategic work has a clearly assigned owner
  • Delegation matches responsibility with commensurate authority — accountability without authority is a structural failure
Decision Authority
  • Decision rights clearly defined, communicated, and understood at every level
  • Approval chains short enough to support the pace the strategy demands
Execution Oversight
  • Review forums that make decisions and drive accountability — not only collect status updates
  • Risk discussions accompanying performance discussions as a standard discipline
Ethical Integrity
  • Leaders modelling the ethical behaviour and fairness they expect of others
  • Consequences applied consistently regardless of seniority — selective enforcement destroys credibility
Governance Adaptability
  • Governance structures evolving as operating conditions and strategy change
  • Governance fit for a previous era must be updated — not preserved by inertia
ESRG Synthesis
The interdependence principle

Environment → enables the behavioural and physical conditions for productive, safe, values-aligned work
Systems → enable efficient, visible, and coordinated execution flow
Resources → provide the human, financial, and operational capacity to deliver
Governance → ensures accountability, decision discipline, and execution oversight

The organisation's sustained execution capacity depends on the synchronisation of these four domains. A weakness in any one domain creates friction that the other three cannot fully compensate for. The discipline of ESRG Alignment is the discipline of ensuring all four are working together — coherently, continuously, and in direct service of the strategic direction.

The ESRG Ignition Point — How Each Domain Enables Execution

Sustained execution begins when the organisation's operating conditions actively support the work required to deliver the strategy. Strategic intent provides direction. Execution discipline provides movement. But sustained results emerge only when the enterprise system itself reinforces the work being done — consistently, structurally, and without dependence on heroic individual effort.

The physical and behavioural conditions of work must both be active

The physical dimension enables the mechanics of execution — teams reaching each other quickly, hybrid participation equal, workspace design matching the work the strategy demands.

The behavioural dimension is equally critical. Values that are merely stated do not enable performance. Values that are demonstrable, measured, and enforced create a behavioural environment where conduct is as consequential as results. The Cultural Operating Code translates each value into a named, actionable norm — moving values from aspiration to operation. Psychological safety completes the enabling condition: when people can surface problems early, the organisation gains real-time intelligence about where execution is straining.

Systems must make execution visible — visibility is the precondition for accountability

Execution depends on how work travels from one function to another. When pathways are clear and reliable, teams can see where work sits, how it progresses, and what action is required next. Streamlined systems reduce duplication and ensure everyone operates from a shared source of truth.

Governance integration within systems — approvals, compliance checks, and escalation triggers embedded in the workflow rather than added after the fact — removes the friction that accumulates at every function boundary.

Resourcing the strategy means resourcing the people who carry it

Teams must possess the skills required, the capacity to sustain effort, and the tools to operate efficiently. Financial investment must flow to the initiatives that drive the strategy forward — not default to historical allocation.

Employee sustainability is not a wellbeing afterthought. Burnout degrades execution quality, accelerates talent exit, and erodes institutional knowledge that is often the organisation's most critical and least recoverable asset. Resourcing the strategy means resourcing people to perform over time — not only in the first quarter.

Governance maintains the cadence of accountability that keeps work advancing

Execution requires a consistent rhythm of decisions, reviews, and accountability conversations. When governance is aligned, decision-making becomes predictable and responsive. Leaders convene forums that resolve problems rather than merely report progress.

Governance also carries the responsibility of ethical integrity. The governance system must apply its standards consistently — regardless of seniority, regardless of results. Leaders exempt from accountability by virtue of position or performance numbers destroy governance credibility faster than any single operational failure.

“When all four conditions operate together, execution no longer depends solely on leadership attention. The organisation's operating system begins to reinforce the work itself. That is the ignition point. That is Grounding.”

Worked Example 1 — The IBSL Cultural Operating Code
6 values translated

IBSL's values are: Intentionality · Integrity · Collaboration · Accountability · Excellence · Human-Centricity

The translation discipline: for each value, define the observable, repeatable behaviour that becomes the organisational way of life — expressed as a named acronym that is teachable, measurable, and replicable.

Intentionality
Leading with purpose, clarity, and discipline
AIM
▼
Act with purpose · Interrogate assumptions · Maintain the discipline

Every leader is clear on the why behind every decision, challenges their own thinking before committing, and follows through with consistency. Intentionality is not enthusiasm — it is the discipline of knowing exactly why you are doing what you are doing, and holding that line when conditions make it inconvenient.

360 indicator: Before committing to any initiative, can this leader articulate the strategic purpose it serves, the assumption they are making, and the specific standard they will hold themselves to in executing it?
Integrity
Consistency between words, behaviour, and outcomes
SAY
▼
Say what you mean · Act on what you say · Yield the truth regardless of consequence

Integrity is not about perfection. It is about consistency between word, behaviour, and outcome — even when telling the truth is uncomfortable, even when the outcome is not what was promised. What we say and what we do are the same thing. Always.

360 indicator: Does this leader name what was promised, what was delivered, and what the gap cost — before being asked?
Collaboration
Leveraging diversity of thought and collective intelligence
ONE
▼
Own a shared outcome · Navigate difference with respect · Execute as a unified force

Collaboration is not a meeting. It is the decision to produce something together that no single function could produce alone. The result belongs to the team — not the function that proposed it.

360 indicator: Does this leader present the shared outcome — including what their function gave up to make it possible — or only their function's contribution?
Accountability
Owning results and reinforcing transparency
OAR
▼
Own it · Act on it · Report it

Every commitment is owned by a named person, acted on within an agreed timeframe, and reported on honestly regardless of outcome. Accountability does not begin when something goes wrong. It begins the moment a commitment is made. We do not manage impressions — we report the truth and fix the gap.

360 indicator: Does this leader surface gaps the moment they become visible — with the cause, the impact, and the recovery action already identified?
Excellence
Upholding rigour, learning, and measurable impact
SET
▼
Set the standard · Earn the evidence · Transform through learning

Excellence is not a destination — it is a daily discipline. We define what good looks like, we measure whether we are achieving it, and we use every gap as a learning signal. It is not about being the best in the room. It is about being better than last quarter, and proving it with evidence.

360 indicator: Can this leader answer three questions at any review: What standard did we set? What evidence do we have? What have we changed as a result?
Human-Centricity
Placing people at the core of leadership and execution
SEE
▼
See the person, not just the role · Enable growth deliberately · Ensure dignity in every interaction

Human-centricity is the commitment that leadership serves people — not the other way around. Every interaction must be conducted with the awareness that there is a person on the other side of it. We invest in growth. We protect dignity. We never reduce a person to their output.

360 indicator: Does this leader open with the person — their experience of the quarter, what supported them, what constrained them — before moving to the result?

Extrapolating · Where

Where Are the ESRG Friction Zones?

Recognising the predictable friction patterns in each ESRG domain — and understanding what type of organisational intervention is required to restore alignment.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Identify the predictable friction patterns for each ESRG domain
  • Distinguish physical environment friction from behavioural environment friction and understand why both must be addressed
  • Recognise friction as organisational intelligence — not evidence of failure but signal of where the system must evolve
  • Connect specific friction patterns to the type of intervention each requires
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Extrapolating

Open with the reframe: friction is not a crisis. It begins quietly, in small operational inconveniences that gradually accumulate. The discipline of Extrapolating is recognising these signals early — before they compound.

As you work through each friction domain, invite leaders to identify which friction pattern they recognise most immediately in their organisation. This primes them for the scanner exercise without requiring them to commit to a judgment.

The closing section — Seeing Friction as Organisational Intelligence — is the critical reframe. Emphasise: every instance of friction reveals something precise about the organisation's readiness. The executive team's role is not just to diagnose friction but to transform insights into targeted alignment actions.

E — Environment Friction
Physical + Behavioural

Environment friction occurs across both dimensions. Both must be examined — addressing one without the other produces only partial restoration of execution conditions.

Physical Environment Friction

Teams are asked to work in new ways — cross-functional collaboration, faster decision cycles, hybrid engagement — yet the workspace continues to reflect older patterns. Offices organised around hierarchy rather than coordination. Meeting rooms not equipped for hybrid participation. Teams that depend on each other separated by organisational structures that make quick problem-solving unnecessarily difficult.

When physical environment friction exists, people spend energy navigating obstacles rather than advancing work. The execution cost is real — and largely invisible in any performance report.

Behavioural Environment Friction

Values misalignment is one of the most common and least diagnosed sources of sustained execution failure. When stated values are not translated into observable, specific behaviours and not embedded in performance measurement, they function as decoration rather than direction. The absence of a Cultural Operating Code means cultural expectations remain implicit — and implicit expectations are interpreted differently by different people at different seniority levels. That inconsistency is friction.

360-degree feedback gaps are a reliable indicator of values friction. When performance management assesses only what people delivered — and not how — the measurement system reinforces a culture in which conduct is invisible. High performers who undermine colleagues or avoid accountability are protected by their results. Over time this erodes the trust sustained execution depends on.

Psychological safety failures are a direct execution risk. When people hesitate to raise concerns, execution issues surface too late. When the cultural environment tolerates aggression, disrespect, or the silencing of dissent, the organisation loses its most valuable early warning system: the willingness of people to tell the truth about what is actually happening.

S — Systems Friction

Many organisations attempt to run new strategic priorities through legacy operational systems. Workflows designed for previous business models remain unchanged even though the strategic direction now requires different forms of coordination, reporting, and decision speed. Teams must invent workarounds simply to keep work moving — and workarounds, over time, become the de facto system.

One of the most common forms of systems friction appears in data and reporting architecture. Teams rely on different data sources, dashboards measuring different definitions of performance, or reporting systems that update too slowly to inform real-time decisions. When this occurs, leaders spend more time debating the accuracy of information than interpreting what it means for execution. Data integrity is not a technical problem. It is an execution problem.

Governance integration within systems is a frequently missed alignment condition. When approvals, compliance checks, and escalation triggers sit outside the workflow as separate, after-the-fact processes, they add latency at every function boundary. When systems friction is present, execution becomes dependent on informal coordination and individual effort rather than on reliable organisational infrastructure.

R — Resources Friction

Strategic plans frequently assume that the necessary capabilities already exist within the organisation. In reality, many strategies require skills that must be developed, technologies that must be acquired, or staffing levels that must expand. The assumption of readiness is itself a source of friction.

Capacity constraints represent a particularly damaging form of friction. Chronic understaffing forces teams to operate under constant pressure — increasing error rates, reducing attention to detail, and accelerating burnout. This is not a wellbeing issue at its core. It is a strategic risk. Burnout degrades execution quality, accelerates talent exit, and erodes the institutional knowledge that is often the organisation's most critical and least recoverable asset.

Employee sustainability is therefore a resource alignment condition, not a peripheral concern. The absence of a signal that workloads are unsustainable is not evidence that all is well. It is evidence that psychological safety in the Resources domain is insufficient.

Financial misalignment is a structural contradiction many organisations sustain for longer than they realise. Strategy follows resources. Where the money goes is where the organisation's real priorities reside — regardless of what the strategy document states.

G — Governance Friction

In many organisations, governance structures were originally designed to control risk rather than to enable execution speed. Governance that was fit for a previous strategic era — and has not been updated — is itself a source of friction.

Unclear decision rights produce one of the most visible and costly forms of governance friction. Teams are uncertain about who has the authority to approve a step forward, resulting in delays while decisions are escalated through multiple management layers. Decision rights that are not clearly defined at every level are not a minor administrative gap. They are a structural drag on execution velocity.

Ethical integrity failures are among the most corrosive and least visible sources of governance friction. When the governance system applies standards selectively — when high performers are exempt from accountability by virtue of results, when leaders modelling values misalignment are protected by seniority — the credibility of the governance system erodes. Selective enforcement does not just weaken governance. It delegitimises it. And a governance system that lacks legitimacy cannot sustain the execution discipline the strategy requires.

Leadership absence from execution oversight is often misread as a trust signal. Leaders who delegate governance entirely — absent from review forums, not engaging actively with accountability conversations — undermine the very structures they have commissioned.

Seeing Friction as Organisational Intelligence

Recognising these friction patterns is not about identifying organisational weaknesses. It is about gaining operational intelligence.

  • Values misalignment in the environment signals a gap between cultural aspiration and cultural reality
  • Systems fragmentation signals an infrastructure built for a different strategic era
  • Capacity constraints signal a resource allocation that has not yet caught up with strategic ambition
  • Governance inconsistency signals a leadership system not yet operating at the standard the strategy demands

The role of the executive team is not merely to diagnose friction. It is to transform those insights into targeted alignment actions that restore the conditions required for sustained performance — so that execution becomes a function of organisational design, not a function of individual heroism.

The Execution Muscle Scan
A collective intelligence method

In a mountain expedition, climbers periodically stop to assess whether the conditions around them still support a safe and successful ascent. They check the terrain, the equipment, the team's condition, and the route ahead. The purpose is not to question the destination. The purpose is to ensure the expedition remains properly equipped to continue the climb.

The Execution Muscle Scan is a structured leadership conversation conducted during the quarterly progress review, immediately after performance has been examined. The executive team shifts its attention from outcomes to the conditions that enable those outcomes.

“Are the organisational conditions still supporting our ability to continue the climb?”

Through this process, the executive team transforms operational observations from across the organisation into collective intelligence about execution readiness — and identifies where the enterprise system must be strengthened before the next execution cycle begins.

Enabling the Work — Physical & Behavioural

Physical: Can teams reach one another quickly? Are hybrid participants fully engaged — or experiencing two-tier participation that creates two-tier accountability? Do workspaces reflect current strategic priorities — or legacy signals from a previous planning cycle?

Behavioural: Are values being demonstrated — not stated, but demonstrated — in how leaders conduct themselves in reviews and performance conversations? Is the Cultural Operating Code being actively reinforced, or has it become background noise? Do people feel safe enough to surface execution problems early — or are they managing impressions and absorbing friction silently?

“Do the physical and behavioural conditions of our environment make it easy for people to collaborate, speak honestly, and advance the work?”

Streamlining the Flow

Are processes remaining clear, current, and accessible — or have workarounds accumulated to the point where the workaround has become the de facto system? Are data sources remaining trusted and consistently defined across functions — or are teams spending review time debating accuracy rather than interpreting meaning? Is workflow visibility sufficient — or is execution progress only visible through informal channels?

Are approvals, compliance checks, and escalation triggers embedded in the workflow — or do they sit outside it as separate, time-consuming layers that slow execution at every function boundary?

“Do our systems allow work, data, and decisions to move smoothly and visibly across the organisation?”

Resourcing the Strategy — Capability, Capacity & Sustainability

Do teams possess the skills required for the current phase — not only the skills they had at the start? Are staffing levels sufficient for normal and peak demand? Is financial investment continuing to flow to the initiatives driving the strategy forward — or defaulting to historical allocation patterns?

Critically: are teams operating at a pace and load that can be maintained — or are they absorbing pressure that is quietly degrading performance quality, accelerating talent exit, and eroding institutional knowledge? Can people signal unsustainable workloads early — or has the culture made that signal feel too costly to send?

“Do our teams have the capability, capacity, and sustainability required to deliver the strategy over time — not only this quarter?”

Maintaining the Rhythm — Decision, Accountability & Integrity

Are decision rights remaining clear at every level — or is approval ambiguity reintroducing decision bottlenecks? Are review forums functioning as decision forums — or have they reverted to status update sessions that produce reports rather than actions?

Are accountability standards being applied consistently regardless of seniority or results? Is leadership presence in execution oversight active and visible — or has governance been delegated while leaders signal by absence that the process is optional? Are risk discussions happening alongside performance discussions — or only triggered by crisis?

“Are decisions, accountability, and ethical standards operating at the pace and consistency required to sustain execution?”

Collective Signal Intelligence & The Outcome

The power of the Execution Muscle Scan lies not in any single reflection, but in how it combines signals from across the executive team into a shared picture of execution readiness. An operational challenge visible to one executive may reflect a systems issue identified by another. A capability concern raised by one function may connect to a governance constraint recognised by another. A values misalignment observed in one team may be a downstream consequence of a leadership behaviour pattern that is visible across multiple functions — but only named when leaders look at the picture collectively.

When those signals are shared in the structured format of the scan, they reveal patterns that individual leaders cannot see alone. Individual observation becomes collective intelligence — a shared understanding of where the organisation's execution conditions must be strengthened before friction compounds into failure.

Build the Cultural Operating Code

Teams translate organisational values into observable, teachable, measurable norms — producing a Cultural Operating Code that leadership can use to reinforce, recognise, and correct behaviour consistently across the organisation.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Apply the Values-to-Norms translation discipline to a real value set
  • Produce COC norms that are observable, acronym-based, and pass the pressure test
  • Present and defend a norm against the New Employee, Pressure, and Mirror tests
  • Leave with a concrete commitment to use one norm in the next difficult leadership conversation
◆ Facilitator Guidance — Application

This exercise is the centrepiece of Lens 9. Protect all 55 minutes. Teams must work with real values — not the participant's organisation's values necessarily, but real enough that the translation work is honest. The three value sets are designed to cover distinct organisational characters — ensure different teams work different sets where numbers allow.

Watch for acronyms that are clever but not useful. Redirect: “Start with the behaviour. The acronym is the packaging — not the product.”

Watch for norms that only describe comfortable situations. The test is whether the norm holds when a senior leader violates it, when a high performer contradicts it, when telling the truth is costly. If it doesn't hold under pressure, it is not a norm — it is a preference.

Watch for norms that are too generic. If Transparency and Integrity produce the same norm, the translation work is not done. Push until the norm is specific to the value.

Choose Your Value Set

Each team selects one value set. No two teams should work with the same set where numbers allow — the debrief is richer when different sets are presented in the room.

Build the Cultural Operating Code from the values in your chosen set. You will not complete all six in session — choose the three that feel most relevant or most challenging to translate.

Build the COC — 35 Minutes

Work through three values from your chosen set. For each value:

Step 1 — Define the behaviour
Ask: “What would a leader who genuinely lives this value do differently from one who only states it?” Write the answer as a specific, observable behaviour — not a principle, not an aspiration.

Step 2 — Build the acronym
Take the first letter of three action words that capture the behaviour. Each word must be an observable action — a verb the leader performs, not a quality they possess. The acronym must be memorable enough to use in a corridor conversation.

Step 3 — Write the norm statement
One sentence explaining what the value means in daily practice — specific enough that a new employee reading it on Day 1 would know exactly what behaviour will be recognised and what will be corrected.

◆ Key Considerations for Each Norm
  • Is the norm specific enough to use in a performance conversation?
  • Could it appear in a 360-degree feedback instrument?
  • Would every function in the organisation interpret it the same way?
The COC Challenge — 15 Minutes Full Group

Each team presents their strongest single norm from the three they worked on — the one they found most powerful or most difficult to translate.

The full group applies three tests:

Test 1 — The New Employee Test

“If a new employee read this norm on their first day, would they know exactly what behaviour will be recognised and what will be corrected?”

Test 2 — The Pressure Test

“Does this norm hold when a senior leader violates it? Or does it only apply to junior staff?”

Test 3 — The Mirror Test

“Would every person in this room be comfortable being assessed against this norm in their own 360-degree feedback?”

“A Cultural Operating Code only becomes real the moment a leader uses it in a difficult conversation — when they name the norm that was breached, describe what was observed, and hold the standard regardless of who is in the room. Which norm from today will you use in your next difficult conversation — and when?”

Micro-Climb Summary — ESRG Alignment
Lens 9 synthesis

Lens 9 addresses the question that most strategy execution frameworks never ask directly: Is the organisation itself — its environment, its systems, its resources, its governance — actually built to sustain the performance it is being asked to deliver?

Awareness — The What

ESRG Alignment examines the four domains that collectively determine whether performance can be sustained. The Cultural Operating Code is the most important output of the Environment domain — not a culture document, but the behavioural architecture of the organisation. Values embedded in 360-degree feedback, enforced through consequences, and modelled by leadership before being expected of teams. Without the COC, values remain decoration. With it, they become the standard.

Intelligence — The Why

ESRG alignment drives sustained execution because each domain removes a specific category of friction. The ignition point of Grounding is reached when all four domains work together — not as separate functions, but as a coherent operating system that reinforces the strategy rather than resisting it. At that point, execution no longer depends on heroic individual effort. The organisation's own design carries the work forward.

Extrapolating — The Where

Execution friction is not random. Each domain generates predictable patterns — values misalignment and psychological safety failures in Environment; workarounds and data fragmentation in Systems; capability gaps and unsustainable workloads in Resources; selective accountability and absent leadership in Governance. Recognising these patterns early is not about finding fault. It is about gaining the organisational intelligence required to strengthen the enterprise.

Integration Zone — The How

Because ESRG friction rarely stays within a single function, alignment cannot be owned by a single leader. The Execution Muscle Scan — conducted quarterly, immediately after performance results are reviewed — transforms individual functional observations into collective intelligence. The output: the Enterprise ESRG Alignment Agenda. A leadership instrument for proactive execution architecture, not a complaint register.

Grounding is where strategy execution is sustained. The environment must enable. The systems must streamline. The resources must sustain. The governance must discipline. When all four are working together — coherently, continuously, and in direct service of the strategic direction — the organisation does not just execute its strategy. It becomes capable of executing the next one, and the one after that.

That is not a management competency. That is an organisational superpower.

And what is the real reason it has not been restored?” The answers to the second question are almost always the true friction beneath the friction. That conversation is the beginning of genuine Grounding.
