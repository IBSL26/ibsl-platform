Section 1.1 · Awareness — What

What is Strategy?

Interrogative Anchor · What is strategy? What is strategic intent? What is Success in Practice?

📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.1 · Awareness

Tab intent: This tab establishes the conceptual bedrock of the entire lens. Before any co-creation can happen, participants must share a single, precise definition of strategy and understand its three building blocks: the master definition, the 3W1H framework, and Success in Practice. Do not rush this. Every word in the master definition has strategic consequence.

Opening the session: Before showing any content, ask participants — "Write your definition of strategy in one sentence — right now, without consulting anyone." Collect 3–4 responses. Note the differences on a whiteboard. This gap between answers is exactly the problem this lens solves. Use it as the opening provocation.

Suggested tab time: 25–35 minutes across all three sections.

Facilitator Script

Before this organisation can define where it is going, leaders must agree on what strategy actually means — not in the abstract, but in the precise operational sense that makes every subsequent decision coherent. Open by asking: "If I asked each of you to write your definition of strategy on a card right now — would they match?" The gap between answers is the problem this lens solves.

7 Concepts · Click to expand each
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Define strategy precisely using the S2R® master definition
  • Understand why every word in the master definition carries strategic weight
  • Identify which element of the definition is least clearly present in their organisation
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.1.1

Directing participants through the 7 concepts: Ask participants to expand each concept card individually and read it before moving to the next. Allow 3–4 minutes of individual reading. Then ask: "Which of these seven concepts is least visible in how your organisation currently talks about strategy?"

Key concept to linger on: The distinction between Thinking & Logic (OUTPUT concept) and Process (structured container). Most organisations have process but neglect thinking quality — the definition forces participants to see this gap.

The Value Triad (Creates · Delivers · Sustains): Draw explicit attention to this concept. Ask: "Is your current strategy as strong on Sustains as it is on Creates and Delivers? Where is the investment in sustainability of value?" This often surfaces an uncomfortable silence.

"Strategy is the output of iterative thinking, logic and process to determine what, why, how and when an organisation creates, delivers and sustains value to its stakeholders while navigating the forces within its operating environment."

1.1.2 — Strategic Intent: The 3W1H Framework
4 Dimensions
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Understand the four dimensions of strategic intent — WHAT, WHY, HOW, WHEN
  • Recognise why HOW and WHEN are the most commonly underdeveloped dimensions
  • Apply diagnostic questions to identify which dimension is weakest in their organisation
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.1.2

Before opening the tabs: Draw attention to the Critical Insight box — "Most strategies fail not because WHAT and WHY are unclear, but because HOW and WHEN are underdeveloped." Ask participants to rate their organisation on each dimension privately (1–5) before exploring the content. This creates a diagnostic baseline.

Directing through the 4 dimension tabs: Walk through WHAT, WHY, HOW, WHEN one at a time as a group. For each, ask one diagnostic question from the content and take 2–3 responses. The HOW and WHEN tabs typically reveal the most significant gaps — give them additional time.

HOW tab — key facilitation moment: Ask: "Does your operating model currently match your strategy — or is it a legacy structure that predates your current direction?" This often opens the most honest conversation of the entire Awareness tab.

WHEN tab: Ask: "Have you actually mapped the sequence of moves needed to achieve your strategy — or does the plan treat all initiatives as running in parallel?"

Suggested time: 10–12 minutes.

Critical Insight: Most strategies fail not because WHAT and WHY are unclear — but because HOW and WHEN are underdeveloped. HOW must include the full operating model and capability architecture. WHEN must address market timing, pivot triggers, and sequencing — not merely a project milestone list.

1.1.3 — Success in Practice (SiP): From Intent to Future Reality
2 Forms · 4 Dimensions
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Distinguish between Strategic Intent (direction) and Success in Practice (future-state description)
  • Understand the four observable SiP domains and how they map to the 3W1H dimensions
  • Distinguish between the Headline (one-sentence anchor) and the Storyline (narrative)
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.1.3

The critical facilitation shift: This section asks participants to make a mental move from planning to experiencing. Explicitly name this shift before entering the content — "We are no longer asking 'What are we going to do?' We are asking: if it has already worked, what does the organisation look like?" Insist on present tense throughout any discussion.

The four observable domains: Walk through WHAT→, WHY→, HOW→, WHEN→ and ask participants to briefly describe what their organisation looks like in each domain today. Note the gaps — this is a preview of the SiP exercise that follows in the Integration Zone.

Headline vs Storyline distinction: Ask: "Does your organisation currently have a Headline — one sentence everyone repeats? If not, what does that tell you about strategic alignment?" Then: "Could every person in this room write one consistent Storyline — or would you get ten different versions?"

Closing Awareness: After section 1.1.3, pause before moving to Intelligence. Ask participants — "What is one word that describes the gap between where your organisation currently is and the SiP you just imagined?" Capture the words — they will resurface in the Integration Zone.

Suggested time: 8–10 minutes.

The Critical Facilitation Move

"Before we move forward, we must be clear — this is the position we are committing to." Shift the question from "What are we going to do?" to "If this works, what will it look like in reality?" Position the team inside the future, not outside it. Everything must be described in present tense as observable conditions — not intentions or plans.

The 3W1H must show up in lived form across four observable domains:

WHAT →

How the value is experienced by customers

WHY →

Why stakeholders trust, choose, and advocate for you

HOW →

How the organisation actually operates (rhythm, coordination, capability)

WHEN →

The pace, sequencing, and responsiveness visible in execution

The SiP narrative is anchored into four observable domains: Customer Experience & Value · Operational Capability & Execution Rhythm · People & Culture Dynamics · Enterprise Value Creation. This structure forces completeness, cross-functional alignment, and the elimination of functional bias.

"If our strategic intent is sound and our strategy works — what will the organisation actually look like, feel like, and perform like in practice?"

Headline — Strategic Vision Anchor

One sentence capturing the strategic destination. Leaders repeat it in meetings, town halls, and board discussions. Signals the destination without describing the route.

Storyline — Strategic Narrative

A narrative of how the organisation operates when success has been achieved, structured across four observable future-reality dimensions. Leaders describe an operating reality they intend to build.

Section 1.2 · Intelligence — Why

Why Strategic Intent Must Precede Success Definition

Ignition Point · Why must intent be clear before success can be described? Why must both be co-created?

📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.2 · Intelligence

Tab intent: This tab answers the "why bother?" question. Participants need to understand why co-creation is not a collaborative nicety but a strategic necessity — and why the Strategy Engine's three components must all be active for strategy to be durable. This tab is the ignition point for the Integration Zone work that follows.

Transition from Tab 1: Open with — "In Tab 1 we established what strategy is. Now the question is: why does it so often fail to hold? The answer is almost never lack of intelligence or effort. It is almost always a failure of co-creation, thinking quality, or iteration discipline."

Suggested tab time: 20–25 minutes.

1.2.1 — Why Intent and SiP Must Be Co-Created
3 Irreplaceable Results
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Understand why co-creation of Strategic Intent and SiP is a strategic requirement, not a preference
  • Recognise the three irreplaceable results that only co-creation can produce
  • Reflect honestly on whether their organisation's strategic intent has been genuinely co-created
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.2.1

Opening the section: Before reading the content, ask participants — "Was your current strategic direction genuinely co-created by the leadership team — or was it shaped at the top and distributed downward?" Take 3–4 honest responses. Then read the three bullet points on screen and ask: "Which of these three results is most absent from your current strategy process?"

Key distinction to make explicit: "Compliance" versus "genuine shared construction." Ask: "Can you tell the difference in your organisation between a leader who is aligned and a leader who has complied? What does that difference look like in execution?"

Closing insight: Emphasise the last line — "This is the defining shift Lens 1 is designed to produce." Tell participants: everything in the Integration Zone tab is designed to create the conditions for this shift in their own team, today.

Suggested time: 7–8 minutes.

The Strategic Intent Statement and SiP are not written by one executive and distributed. They are co-created by the leadership team. This is not a procedural preference — it is a strategic requirement.

  • Leaders agree on the destination — not through compliance, but through genuine shared construction.
  • Trade-offs become visible early — the tensions between functions surface in the room, not in execution.
  • Collective ownership is built — the only ownership that sustains during execution pressure.

When executives participate in shaping both outputs, strategy moves from individual interpretation to shared strategic imagination. This is the defining shift Lens 1 is designed to produce.

1.2.2 — The Strategy Engine: Thinking · Process · Iteration
3 Interlocked Components
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Understand the three interlocked components of the Strategy Engine — Thinking Logic, Process, and Iterative Nature
  • Recognise why most organisations over-invest in Process and under-invest in Thinking Logic
  • Identify which component is weakest in their current strategy discipline
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.2.2

Directing through the three layer tabs: Walk through each layer — Thinking Logic, Process, Iterative Nature — one at a time. For each, read the "Where it fails" content aloud and ask: "Does this failure mode sound familiar in your organisation?"

The critical red box: Draw explicit attention to the red highlight — "Most focus heavily on Process, add some Iteration, and largely ignore Thinking Logic." Ask participants: "Where would you honestly place your organisation on this spectrum right now?" Allow 2–3 candid responses.

Thinking Logic vs Process: This distinction often surprises participants. The insight is that well-structured process can execute a wrong strategy efficiently. Ask: "Can you think of a time when your strategy process was rigorous but reached the wrong conclusion — and how long did it take to recognise that?"

Iterative Nature: Ask: "How often does your organisation genuinely revisit and revise its strategy based on new evidence — versus defending a settled position?"

Suggested time: 8–10 minutes.

Strategy is the output of three interlocked components that must run simultaneously. Confusion between these components is itself a strategic failure mode.

1.3.1 — The Four SiP Dimensions: Observable Indicators
4 Dimensions · Click indicators to expand
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Understand the four SiP dimensions as observable, multi-indicator realities
  • Identify the specific signals that indicate whether strategy is working in each domain
  • Diagnose which dimension shows the most significant gaps in their current organisation
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.3.1

Directing through the dimension tabs: Walk through each dimension — D1 Customer Experience, D2 Operational Capability, D3 People & Culture, D4 Enterprise Value — one at a time. For each, expand 2–3 indicator rows and ask participants: "In your organisation — is this indicator in the 'What good looks like' column, or the 'What's missing' column?"

D1 — Customer Experience: Focus on the CARE model (Connection, Access, Results, Effort). Ask: "Which of these four value types does your organisation deliver most consistently — and which is the most exposed gap?"

D2 — Operational Capability: Ask: "Does your organisation have Priority Clarity — can every leader in this room state the top three priorities without checking a document, and would the answers match?" Allow the discomfort if answers diverge.

D3 — People & Culture: Ask: "What is the Dialogue Quality in your strategy meetings — honest challenge or performative consensus?"

D4 — Enterprise Value: Ask: "Is your growth strategy-driven or market-driven? Can you trace your current growth to specific strategic choices?"

Suggested time: 12–14 minutes.

Each SiP dimension is a multidimensional observable reality — not a single metric. These are the specific signals that tell an executive team whether the strategy is working in that domain.

📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.4.1

Purpose before the SiP exercise: This section is a bias-awareness primer before the SiP exercise. Participants must understand their natural lens before they can transcend it.

Directing participants: Ask each participant to click their assigned executive role card. Give them 2 minutes to read their role's bias and integration challenge. Then ask: "Does this description of your role's natural bias feel accurate? Where does it resonate most strongly?"

1.4.2 — Strategy Maturity Assessment: 26 Areas
6 Groups · Score 0–3
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Assess their organisation honestly across 26 strategy components in 6 groups
  • Identify which group scores lowest and what that reveals about critical corrective action needed
  • Generate a total maturity score that provides a baseline for S2R® progress tracking
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.4.2

Setting up the assessment: Before participants begin scoring, read the instruction aloud — "Score the organisation as it actually is right now — not as it was last year, not as it will be after the current initiative completes. Honest assessment is the only kind that is useful." Emphasise that this is not a performance evaluation — it is a diagnostic tool.

Individual vs collective scoring: For maximum insight, ask each participant to score independently first — without discussion. Allow 8–10 minutes of silent individual scoring across all six groups. Then compare scores across the group. Significant divergence on the same item is itself a strategic finding — it signals that different leaders have fundamentally different views of organisational reality.

Group debrief after scoring: Use these three questions in sequence:

  • "Which group (A–F) scored lowest across the team — and was there consensus on that?"
  • "Where was the widest divergence in scores? What does that disagreement tell us?"
  • "What is the single most important corrective action this score points to in the next 90 days?"

Using the export function: Direct participants (or the lead facilitator) to use "Screen View" or "Save as PDF" to generate the maturity report. This becomes a baseline document for the organisation's S2R® journey — it should be retained and revisited at the end of the programme.

Score interpretation: 0–26 = Foundational Gaps (strategy is fragile, personality-dependent). 27–52 = Developing Capability (key dimensions present but integration incomplete). 53–78 = Strategic Maturity (strategy is embedded as organisational capability). Most first-time groups score in the 20–45 range — normalise this and focus on the direction of travel, not the absolute score.

Suggested time: 15–20 minutes including individual scoring and group debrief.

1.4.3 — Collective SiP Exercise: Four Dimensions per Executive Role
10 Executive Roles · 4 Dimensions each
Learning Objectives — By the end of this section, participants will
  • Describe the future organisation across all four SiP dimensions from their assigned executive role
  • Write in the present tense of the future — as if the strategy has already succeeded
  • Contribute a perspective that the group can then synthesise into a collective enterprise SiP
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.4.3

This is the core deliverable of Lens 1. Everything prior has been preparation for this exercise. Protect the time — do not compress it.

Setting up the exercise:

  • Assign executive roles before participants begin — do not allow self-selection. The learning comes partly from being required to inhabit a perspective that is not naturally yours.
  • Remind participants: "Write in the present tense of the future. Not 'we will have...' but 'we have...' Not 'we aim to...' but 'customers experience...'"
  • Reinforce the Flammable risk: "As you write, notice when you are defaulting to your functional comfort zone. Push yourself into the dimensions that feel less natural."

Individual writing time: Allow 10–12 minutes for each participant to complete all four dimensions from their assigned role. This must be done in silence — the independent perspective is what makes the synthesis valuable. Group discussion before individual writing contaminates the outputs.

Running the synthesis: After individual contributions are complete, use the "View All Responses" function to display contributions side by side. Facilitate the synthesis using four questions:

  • "Where do all perspectives converge? This is your strongest strategic signal."
  • "Where do perspectives compete or contradict? This is where trade-off decisions are needed."
  • "Which SiP dimension is least well-described across all contributions? This is your white space — who owns it?"
  • "Which functional perspective dominates the collective narrative? How do we rebalance?"

Producing the collective output: The facilitator (or a nominated scribe) drafts a single integrated SiP from the synthesis discussion. This draft becomes the working SiP for the organisation — it will be tested, refined, and embedded across subsequent lenses. It does not need to be perfect today; it needs to be genuinely shared.

Suggested time: 20–30 minutes including individual writing, synthesis discussion, and collective draft.

Each leader describes all four SiP dimensions from their assigned role's perspective, written in the present tense of the future — as if the strategy has already succeeded.

Facilitator Note

After all leaders complete, lead a structured synthesis: identify convergence (where all align), tension (where perspectives compete), missing dimensions (where no leader adequately described the future state), and dominant bias (which functional perspective over-represents the narrative).

Headline · Storyline · Synthesis
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.5.3

The synthesis move: After all CXO rows in 1.5.2 are completed, invite each participant to distil their perspective into a Headline and a Storyline. 1.5.4 — Integration Summary: The Emerging Collective View
Convergence · Tension · Gaps · Bias
📋 FACILITATOR NOTE — Section 1.5.4

The integration synthesis: This is the most important facilitation moment in the entire lens. After all CXO contributions are captured in 1.5.2 and 1.5.3, lead the group through a structured analysis of the four dimensions below. Do not rush this — the most valuable strategic insight often emerges in the tension and missing dimensions, not in the convergence points.

Sequencing the synthesis: Start with Convergence (build collective confidence), then move to Tension (surface productive disagreement), then Missing Dimensions (name the blind spots), then Dominant Bias (invite honest self-assessment from the dominant function). The order matters — starting with tension creates defensiveness that prevents the insight from landing.

Closing the lens: End with the question: "We have now described the organisation we intend to build. The gap between what we described and what we currently have — is that gap strategic or operational? And who in this room is accountable for closing it?"

Suggested time: 15–20 minutes.
