Lens 6 — Session Overview

Use this tab to orient yourself before each section. It contains purpose, tone, key facilitation questions, and watch points for the full lens.

◆ Lens Intent

Lens 6 addresses the single most common reason strategy fails at the human level: expectations are communicated but never truly established. The distinction is subtle in language and profound in execution impact. The lens moves leaders from announcing targets to securing genuine ownership. The Alignment Dialogue (Score → Evaluate → Resolve) is the centrepiece tool — practical, immediate, and directly applicable.

This lens is highly experiential. The role-play in Application is not optional — it is where the conceptual work becomes a skill. Protect the time for it.

◆ Key Facilitation Questions
  • “If you distributed your team’s scorecard right now — without conversation, without context — how many of them would genuinely own those targets?”
  • “Think of the last time a delivery goal was missed. Was the expectation set — or was it established?”
  • “Who in your expectations chain could derail delivery — and have you had the alignment conversation with them?”
  • “When someone on your team gives you a 6 out of 10 on alignment — what does your instinct tell you to do? And what should you do?”
◆ Tone & Watch Points

Watch for: leaders who confuse clarity with alignment. A person can understand an expectation perfectly and still not own it. Probe: “Clarity tells you they heard it. Alignment tells you they’ll deliver it. How do you know the difference?”

Watch for: leaders who treat the Alignment Score as a report card. The score is a diagnostic, not a judgement. If someone gives a 6, the leader’s job is not to challenge it — it is to understand it and resolve it.

Application: Run the role-play with real expectations from participants’ current contexts where possible. The closer the scenario is to their actual work, the more transferable the skill.

Awareness · What

What is Establishing Performance Expectations?

Naming the distinction between setting and establishing expectations — and the two mechanisms that close the gap between a delivery goal and genuine ownership.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Distinguish between setting expectations (instruction) and establishing expectations (commitment)
  • Identify the three conditions — Clarity, Capability, Commitment — that must be present for a KPI to become a personal leadership promise
  • Map the Expectations Chain for a given delivery goal, identifying upline, downline, and external contributors
  • Describe the purpose and structure of the Alignment Dialogue
Setting vs Establishing Expectations
The defining distinction

The difference between setting expectations and establishing expectations is subtle in language and profound in execution impact. It determines whether a performance target becomes an instruction that people comply with or a commitment that people own.

Execution does not begin when delivery goals are written. Execution begins when the people responsible for those goals internalise the expectation and accept ownership of the result.

Dimension Setting Expectations Establishing Expectations

◆ Facilitator Note

Open with the script question: “If you distributed your team’s scorecard right now — without conversation, without context — how many of them would genuinely own those targets?” Let the silence sit. Most leaders will realise their answer is “fewer than I thought.” That discomfort is the entry point for the rest of the lens.

The Three Conditions of Established Expectations
Clarity · Capability · Commitment

When expectations are properly established, three conditions are simultaneously present. All three must be confirmed before execution begins. A KPI becomes a personal leadership promise only when all three are in place.

Condition 1
Clarity — The individual understands exactly what success looks like
▼

Clarity is not just knowing the number. It means understanding the full picture of the expected outcome: what must be delivered, by when, at what standard, and how success will be recognised. Most expectation conversations stop at the number. Establishing expectations requires confirming that both parties share the same picture of the result.

Facilitation test: “Ask someone on your team to describe what success looks like for their key result. If the description differs from yours, clarity has not yet been established.”
Condition 2
Capability — The system can realistically support delivery
▼

Capability in this context refers not only to the individual’s skill, but to the system surrounding them. Can the organisation actually support delivery of this expectation? Are the resources, decisions, cross-functional dependencies, and coordination mechanisms in place? A person may fully understand and deeply want to own a target that the current system cannot deliver. Establishing expectations surfaces this before execution begins — not after the deadline has passed.

Condition 3
Commitment — The person consciously accepts ownership of the outcome
▼

Commitment is the moment the expectation crosses from instruction to promise. The individual does not merely acknowledge the target — they accept personal responsibility for ensuring the outcome is delivered. This cannot be assumed from nodding agreement or email confirmation. It must be actively tested through dialogue. The Alignment Dialogue is the mechanism designed to confirm this transition has occurred.

The Alignment Dialogue asks: “On a scale of 1–10, how aligned are you with the expectation for you to deliver this outcome?” A score below 9 means Commitment is not yet established.
◆ Facilitator Note

Most leaders overestimate how much Clarity they have provided and underestimate how little Commitment they have secured. The conversation usually sounds complete to the leader and feels incomplete to the team member. Ask: “After your last expectations conversation, which of the three conditions did you explicitly verify? Clarity — probably. Capability — sometimes. Commitment — almost never.”

The Expectations Chain
Mapping who else influences the outcome

Very few delivery goals are achieved by a single person acting alone. Most results depend on a network of decisions, resources, and coordination across the organisation. The Expectations Chain identifies everyone whose work directly affects the delivery of a Key Result or KPI.

Mapping the Expectations Chain is a critical step before establishing expectations with any individual. It ensures that the KPI is not treated as an isolated personal obligation — and surfaces the dependencies that will determine whether delivery is possible.

◆ Facilitator Note

Ask leaders to pick one current key result and map their Expectations Chain on the spot — 3 minutes, three columns. Then ask: “Have you had an alignment dialogue with everyone in that chain? Or only with the person holding the KPI?” Most will realise they have had the conversation with one person and assumed the rest of the chain would follow. That assumption is where most execution gaps begin.

The Alignment Dialogue
Testing whether the expectation is owned

With the Expectations Chain mapped, the leader conducts an Alignment Dialogue with the individuals responsible for delivering the OKR/KPI. This is a structured conversation designed to test whether the expectation has moved from instruction to commitment.

The Alignment Dialogue uses a simple scale of 1–10. A score of 9 or 10 indicates genuine ownership of delivery. Anything lower demands further deliberation through the Score → Evaluate → Resolve sequence.

The power of the Alignment Dialogue is not the number — it is what the number reveals. A score of 6 is not a failure. It is data: there is something unresolved between the expectation and the person’s readiness to own it. The leader’s job is to find it and resolve it before execution begins.

◆ Facilitator Note

Introduce the Alignment Dialogue as a leadership safety system — not a performance assessment tool. “You are not asking for the score so you can judge. You are asking so you can help. A 10 means go. Anything below 10 means there is still work for you to do as the leader.”

Intelligence · Why

Why Establishing Expectations Determines Execution Outcomes

Understanding the behavioural mechanisms through which expectation alignment drives ownership, reduces ambiguity, surfaces constraints, and transforms accountability from formal obligation to personal commitment.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Explain why expectation alignment is the ignition point of execution, not a precursor to it
  • Distinguish between compliance-driven and commitment-driven execution dynamics
  • Identify the five behavioural mechanisms through which establishing expectations determines execution outcomes
  • Explain how engagement and accountability degrade when expectations are set but not established
Compliance vs Commitment — The Execution Dynamic
Ignition point

Strategy is ultimately delivered through human commitment, not through plans, scorecards, or systems alone. The moment when expectations are clarified between leader and contributor is the moment a performance target transitions from organisational intention to personal responsibility. If that transition does not occur, execution becomes fragile regardless of how well the system has been designed.

Dimension △ Compliance-Driven △ Commitment-Driven
How it looksExpectations communicated as instructions; acknowledged but not ownedExpectations established through dialogue; owned, not just acknowledged
Behaviour patternWait for direction; escalate problems upward; focus on reporting rather than accelerating outcomesAnticipate risks earlier; coordinate across functions actively; invest effort in solving obstacles
When obstacles ariseRemain cautious; protect reporting; defer resolution to leadershipTake initiative; surface issues early; co-design solutions with the leader
AccountabilityActivity ownership: responsible for completing tasks, not for the final resultOutcome ownership: personally accountable for the final result, not just the activities
EnergyReserved; discretionary effort withheldFully invested; discretionary effort freely given
◆ Facilitator Note

Invite leaders to locate their current team: “If you are honest — when your team hits an obstacle, what happens first? Do they solve it, or do they escalate it? The answer tells you whether you have compliance or commitment.” This is not a judgement of the team. It is a signal about whether expectations were established.

Five Mechanisms: Why Expectation Alignment Determines Outcomes
The behavioural logic
Mechanism 1
From Instruction to Ownership
▼

When expectations are merely communicated, people interpret them as instructions. The individual understands what must be achieved but does not yet feel personally accountable for ensuring the outcome is delivered. Establishing expectations through structured dialogue moves the individual from acknowledging the requirement to accepting ownership of the result. This is the fundamental shift that makes execution commitment-driven rather than compliance-driven.

Mechanism 2
Reducing Interpretation Ambiguity
▼

Even when strategic objectives are clearly articulated, individuals often interpret what success requires differently depending on their role, expertise, and operational context. Without structured expectation alignment, people may pursue the same goal while working from different assumptions. Establishing expectations creates a shared interpretation of: what must be delivered, why the result matters, and how success will be recognised. This reduces confusion and allows teams to move forward with greater coordination.

Mechanism 3
Surfacing Constraints Before Execution Begins
▼

Most organisational results require coordination across functions, access to resources, timely decision-making, and cooperation from multiple contributors. If these dependencies are not identified during the expectation conversation, they only become visible later — often when deadlines are already approaching. Establishing expectations allows leaders and contributors to identify these factors early, ensuring required resources are understood, cross-functional dependencies are visible, and potential risks are recognised before they become execution crises.

Mechanism 4
Strengthening Accountability
▼

Accountability is strongest when individuals perceive that the expectations placed on them are clear, achievable, and meaningful. When these conditions are present, people are far more likely to take ownership of outcomes and sustain effort when challenges arise. Establishing expectations strengthens accountability by ensuring both the leader and contributor share a common understanding of: the responsibility involved, what support will be available, what constraints must be addressed, and what standards will be used to evaluate success. This mutual clarity transforms accountability from a formal obligation into a shared commitment.

Mechanism 5
Building the Behavioural Foundation
▼

Strategy execution is often described as a systems challenge. It is fundamentally a behavioural challenge. Plans, metrics, and dashboards provide direction and visibility, but outcomes are ultimately produced through the daily decisions and actions of people. Establishing performance expectations ensures that individuals are not simply aware of what must be done but are psychologically prepared to take responsibility for doing it. When this foundation is in place: individuals understand the purpose behind the work, confidence in feasibility increases, collaboration improves, and accountability becomes proactive rather than reactive.

◆ Facilitator Note

Ask participants to identify which mechanism is most absent in their current execution environment. “Is your team unclear on what success looks like (Mechanism 2)? Are constraints surfacing too late (Mechanism 3)? Or is accountability diffuse — everyone feels responsible and no one is (Mechanism 4)?” The diagnosis shapes the intervention.

Engagement & Accountability — The Two Casualties
What breaks down when expectations are only set

When expectations are set without being established, two organisational conditions degrade in a predictable sequence.

First Casualty
Engagement Weakens — Observable but Subtle
▼

When leaders communicate expectations without alignment dialogue, individuals may understand what is required but do not feel personally involved in shaping how the outcome will be delivered. The expectation is acknowledged cognitively but not internalised behaviourally. Work continues and progress may still be visible, but the energy becomes uneven.

  • Discretionary effort is limited — people do what is assigned, not what is needed
  • Individuals wait for direction rather than proactively addressing emerging problems
  • Cross-functional coordination slows because people focus primarily on their own responsibilities
  • Engagement becomes transactional — people perform their roles but the deeper sense of collective ownership does not materialise
The signal: work is happening, but energy is not invested. People are present but not propelled.
Critical Long-Term Impact
Accountability Dilutes — The Deeper Organisational Cost
▼

The most significant long-term consequence is in the quality of accountability. When expectations are set without alignment, individuals may feel responsible for carrying out activities but not for the final organisational result. Activity ownership and outcome ownership become blurred.

  • Performance discussions revolve around explanations rather than ownership
  • Conversations focus on constraints — resources, dependencies, external conditions — rather than solutions
  • Cross-functional dependencies create delays because responsibility for resolving them is unclear
  • Accountability becomes dispersed: multiple teams feel partially responsible, none fully accountable
The paradox: teams appear to be working hard, meetings are frequent, updates are detailed — yet the organisation consistently struggles to achieve what it set out to deliver. The issue is not effort or capability. It is that ownership of the outcome was assigned without being secured.
◆ Facilitator Note

The paradox in the Accountability card is often a moment of recognition for leaders. Name it explicitly: “Have you ever been in a review where everyone has a reason, and no one has an answer? That is diluted accountability in action. It did not happen because of poor performance. It happened because ownership was never established.”

Integration Zone · Collective

Cross-Leadership Expectation Architecture

Designing a shared method for establishing expectations consistently across the organisation — connecting every expectation to the future, clarifying the chain, and securing ownership through the Alignment Dialogue.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Apply the four-step Expectation Architecture as a consistent leadership practice
  • Facilitate the Alignment Dialogue using the Score → Evaluate → Resolve coaching model
  • Interpret alignment scores and respond appropriately to each score range
  • Document the three leader reflections that convert the conversation into a visible commitment record
The Expectation Architecture — Four Steps
A shared leadership practice

The Expectation Architecture provides a simple, consistent method that every leader in the organisation can use — from executives to team leaders to individual contributors — to move expectations from communication into genuine ownership.

1. Start With the Future
2. Clarify the Delivery Expectation
3. Identify the Expectations Chain
4. Confirm Ownership

Begin by connecting the expectation to the organisation’s Success in Practice (SiP) — the future state the organisation is pursuing.

The objective is not simply to communicate the task, but to help the individual understand why the expectation matters. This anchors the expectation in organisational purpose rather than isolated activity.

Example leader framing:
“As part of our goal to become the most responsive and trusted partner in our market, we need to reduce complaint response times across the service teams. This directly enables the experience we have committed to delivering to our customers.”

When expectations begin with the Future, individuals understand the contribution their work makes to the broader direction — not just the task in front of them. This shifts the conversation from compliance to purpose.

Once the Future is clear, define the specific result expected — in outcome terms, not activity terms.

This step focuses the conversation on the result rather than the method. The leader defines what success looks like in concrete, measurable terms and checks that the individual understands and shares that picture.

Example leader framing:
“The outcome we need is a reduction in resolution time from 48 hours to 12 hours by the end of Q3 2026. That is the result we are holding ourselves to — not the activities that will get us there, but the outcome we are accountable for.”

Clarity is tested by asking the individual to describe what success looks like in their own words. If the description differs from the leader’s, the expectation requires further clarification before proceeding.

With the outcome clear, identify who else must contribute for the result to be achieved.

Most organisational outcomes depend on multiple contributors. By mapping the Expectations Chain early, the leader ensures that dependencies are visible before execution begins — not when deadlines are approaching.

The conversation explores:

  • Which teams or individuals will need to collaborate on this outcome?
  • Whose decisions or resources directly influence the delivery of the result?
  • Where might coordination be required across functions?
  • Which external actors influence whether the result can be achieved?

This step helps the individual understand the execution system surrounding the expectation — they are not delivering alone, and the leader is confirming that the full chain has been considered.

The final step confirms that the individual accepts responsibility for ensuring the outcome is delivered. This is the moment the expectation moves from instruction to commitment.

The leader asks the Alignment Question:

“On a scale of 1–10, how aligned are you with the expectation for you to deliver this outcome?”

A score of 9 or higher indicates that the expectation has moved into genuine ownership of delivery. The leader can proceed with confidence that the individual has consciously accepted responsibility for the result.

A score below 9 means there is unresolved concern between the expectation and the individual’s readiness to own it. The leader moves immediately into the Score → Evaluate → Resolve sequence to identify and address the source of misalignment before execution begins.

◆ Facilitator Note

The most common omission is Step 1. Leaders jump straight to the number (Step 2) and skip the Future entirely. Ask: “When you last communicated a delivery expectation, did you start with why it matters — or with what the number is?” The Future is not motivational padding. It is the foundation of ownership.

The Alignment Dialogue — Score → Evaluate → Resolve
Coaching model

When the alignment score is below 9, the leader does not challenge the score or move past it. The number is data — it signals that something is unresolved. The Score → Evaluate → Resolve sequence transforms that signal into a resolved commitment.

1. Score It
2. Evaluate It
3. Resolve It

The individual provides a numerical assessment of their level of alignment. The score acts as a signal of readiness for ownership — not a judgement of performance.

Score What it signals Leader action
9–10Clear ownership and readiness to deliverProceed; confirm the commitment and document
7–8Partial alignment — some uncertainty remainsMove to Evaluate → surface what is unresolved
5–6Significant concerns affecting confidence in deliveryMove to Evaluate → expect multiple concerns to surface
Below 5Expectation is not yet viable in its current formDo not proceed — the expectation must be redesigned

If the score is below 9, the leader explores the reasons behind the rating. The objective is to understand what is preventing full alignment — not to challenge or override the score.

The leader guides the conversation using diagnostic questions:

  • “What influenced the score you gave?”
  • “What feels unclear about the expectation?”
  • “What concerns do you have about delivering this outcome?”
  • “Where do you anticipate obstacles or constraints?”

This step surfaces issues that might otherwise only appear during execution — when they are far more costly to resolve. Common concerns include:

  • Unclear definition of the outcome or success criteria
  • Cross-functional dependencies not yet confirmed
  • Capacity or resource constraints affecting feasibility
  • Confidence in the delivery pathway or timeline

The purpose of Evaluate is to make hidden concerns visible before execution begins — not to create a case for lowering the expectation.

Once the sources of misalignment are understood, the leader works with the individual to resolve them.

The guiding coaching question becomes:

“What would help move your alignment from where it is now to a 9 or 10?”

This question shifts the conversation toward solutions rather than justification. Resolution may involve:

  • Clarifying the outcome definition or success criteria
  • Adjusting priorities or realistic timelines
  • Identifying additional support or resources required
  • Confirming collaboration across the Expectations Chain

Once concerns are resolved, the leader asks the alignment question again to confirm the expectation has moved into clear ownership. If it has not, the conversation continues — or the expectation itself is redesigned.

Leader’s Reflection Capture (after the dialogue):
1. Alignment Rating — Where does the commitment currently stand?
2. Primary Concern Identified — What surfaced during the discussion?
3. Action for Elevation — What will move alignment upward?

◆ Facilitator Note

The Resolve question — “What would move you to a 9 or 10?” — is the most important question in the whole architecture. It keeps the leader in the right posture: not defending the expectation, but solving for the person’s ability to own it. Have leaders practise asking it aloud before the Application section. It sounds simple and feels unfamiliar the first time.

◆ Facilitator Note — Role Play Setup

Format: Pairs or triads. One person plays the CXO leader, one plays the team member/HoD, and (in triads) one observes and gives feedback. Rotate roles so each person experiences the leader position.

Timing: 15 min per round (two rounds minimum). Debrief in plenary: what felt natural, what felt unfamiliar, where the Resolve question changed the dynamic.

Key watch point: Most leaders will rush Step 1 (the Future) and the alignment question. Observers should specifically note whether the leader genuinely connected to purpose before moving to the number — and whether the Alignment Question was asked with curiosity or with pressure.

Micro-Climb Summary — Establishing Expectations
Lens 6 synthesis
Awareness

Setting expectations communicates the required result. Establishing expectations ensures the individual understands it, can deliver it, and consciously accepts ownership of it. The Expectations Chain and Alignment Dialogue are the mechanisms that close this gap.

Intelligence

Expectation alignment determines execution outcomes through five mechanisms: shifting instruction to ownership, reducing ambiguity, surfacing constraints early, strengthening accountability, and building the behavioural foundation of execution.

Extrapolating

Every CXO function has a characteristic tendency to set rather than establish expectations. Without alignment dialogue, the organisation may appear aligned at the surface while engagement weakens and accountability dilutes beneath it.

Integration Zone

The Expectation Architecture (Start with the Future → Clarify → Identify Chain → Confirm Ownership) and the Alignment Dialogue (Score → Evaluate → Resolve) create a shared leadership discipline that transforms expectation-setting into a commitment practice.

“Strong performance cultures are not built by telling people what is expected. They are built by ensuring that expectations are mutually understood, realistically supported, and consciously accepted by the people responsible for delivering them.”

◆ Facilitator Closing Note

Close by asking each leader to name one expectation they have set but not yet established — with a specific person in their expectations chain. Ask them to commit to having that alignment dialogue within five working days and to capturing the score, the primary concern, and the action for elevation. The five-day window makes the commitment real rather than aspirational.
