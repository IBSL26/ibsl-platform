Section 1.1 · Awareness — What

What is KISS Framing & OKR Definition?

Before defining Objectives and Key Results, you must examine the organisation's current operating reality in relation to the Success in Practice narrative — this is the bridge between strategy and execution.

1.1.1 — From Success in Practice to Action
The Starting Question

Your leadership team has produced a Strategy Intent statement and a Success in Practice (SiP) narrative. These describe what, why, how, and when the organisation will deliver its value — and what success looks like in practice. The next step is translating this future description into a practical execution agenda.

The question now becomes: If the Success in Practice we described is the future organisation, what must change today to make that future possible?

Before jumping to Objectives and Key Results, the organisation must first examine its current operating reality honestly in relation to the SiP. The KISS framework provides the structured filter to do this.

1.1.2 — KISS: Keep · Improve · Start · Stop
4 Reflective Filters

KISS passes the SiP statement through four reflective filters, each examining the organisation against the future state described:

KEEP

Practices, capabilities, and behaviours already moving the organisation toward the SiP. Protect and reinforce these strategic assets.

IMPROVE

Areas already moving in the right direction where performance, coordination, or capability must strengthen to support the SiP more fully.

START

New practices, capabilities, or initiatives that do not yet exist but will be necessary for the organisation to operate as described in the SiP.

STOP

Activities, routines, or commitments that consume energy and resources but do not contribute to the future organisation described in the SiP.

Critical principle: Every KISS reflection must be anchored to the SiP statement — not a general audit. You are evaluating the organisation against the specific future your leadership team has collectively described.

1.1.4 — OKR Anatomy: Objectives & Key Results
Structure & Formula

OKRs translate the changes identified in the KISS reflection into measurable progress.

Objectives

Define strategic ambition. Must be: Qualitative · Ambitious · Action-Oriented · Inspirational · Memorable.

Key Results

Define measurable progress. Formula: Verb of Change + Metric + From X to Y + By Deadline.

Example Key Result: Increase digital transaction completion rate from 40% to 75% of total transactions by Q4 2026.

Critical Distinction: OKRs define outcomes — not activities. If a statement can be checked off a to-do list, it is a task. Key Results describe the impact those tasks must produce.

Section 1.2 · Intelligence — Why

Why the KISS-to-OKR Sequence Matters

Most OKR failures are reflection failures — not framework failures. The KISS-OKR bridge ensures your goals are built from operating reality, not from aspiration alone.

1.2.1 — The Danger of Moving Too Fast
3 Human Problems

Many organisations dive into OKRs with energy, but that enthusiasm often sours into frustration. Instead of clarity, teams end up with vague goals and Key Results that feel like yet another to-do list.

Most of the time, the framework is not the problem. The problem is that organisations try to measure progress before having an honest conversation about where they are actually standing. When leadership moves straight from vision to hard targets, three human problems emerge:

Disconnected Targets

People hit numbers without acknowledging the hurdles that make those numbers nearly impossible.

Fragmented Efforts

Functions row in different directions because they have not agreed on what success looks like in the trenches.

The Activity Trap

People default to measuring busyness because they are unsure how to prove they are achieving real outcomes.

1.2.2 — The S2R® Sequence: SiP → KISS → OKRs
3-Part Logic

The KISS reflection is where the team pauses to be honest. It surfaces the organisation's actual starting position relative to the SiP, bringing four critical realities to light: the strengths to lean into, the friction points draining energy, the gaps requiring investment, and the habits to finally release.

Success in Practice

The dream of where the organisation wants to be — a vivid future state across four observable dimensions.

KISS

The honest look at the changes required to get there — the bridge between the future and the present operating reality.

OKRs

The manageable, measurable steps that prove the organisation is moving — the execution compass pointing toward the SiP.

When this sequence is respected, OKRs stop being a goal-setting tool and become a way for the organisation to learn, align, and prove progress. Strategy transforms from a statement of intent into a living system.

Section 1.3 · Extrapolating — Where

Where do OKR Hot Zones Appear?

The Alignment Brigade · Every leadership role naturally emphasises certain future realities over others. Recognising your hot zone is the first step toward enterprise-level thinking.

1.3.1 — Natural Emphasis Across Leadership Functions
Why Functions Diverge

Every leadership role tends to emphasise some of the four future realities more strongly than others, because each role carries responsibility for specific organisational outcomes. This creates a natural imbalance in how OKRs are proposed and evaluated:

  • Commercial leaders emphasise Customer Experience and Enterprise Value Creation
  • Operational leaders emphasise Operational Capability and Enterprise Value Creation
  • People leaders emphasise People & Culture and Operational Capability
  • Financial leaders emphasise Enterprise Value and Operational Discipline

Each perspective is valid. But when unexamined, these perspectives produce functional OKRs rather than enterprise OKRs.

1.3.3 — The Alignment Brigade: From Functional to Enterprise Thinking
The Shift Required

When the leadership team names its natural biases explicitly, the conversation shifts from functional interpretation of success to collective definition of success.

If these perspectives remain unexamined, OKRs gradually drift into functional scorecards — producing competing definitions of success, fragmented measurement systems, and functional optimisation rather than enterprise progress.

Recognising these patterns allows the leadership team to integrate perspectives into a balanced execution system that reflects the full enterprise — not just the sum of its functions.

Priority Matrix

Review your KISS reflection and identify the 2–3 big shifts in your area that will contribute to moving the whole organisation forward. Ask: "If the organisation is to reach the future it has described, what is the one thing my area can change that would make the biggest difference for everyone?"

Translate each theme into a bold, qualitative statement that expresses strategic ambition. An Objective should look like a transformation — not a budget line. Make it ambitious, keep it qualitative, and keep it memorable. If it cannot survive a hallway conversation, it is too complex.

For every Objective, define 2–3 ways to prove achievement using the four-part rhythm: Verb + Metric + From X to Y + Deadline.

Quick check: If the team can simply "check it off" a list, it is a task — not a Key Result. Key Results describe the actual change in the world that proves the Objective is being achieved.

Before sharing your OKR with the group, test it against four questions:

  • Does this truly get the organisation closer to the shared SiP vision?
  • Would fellow leaders see this as a win for the whole organisation — or just for one function?
  • Does it visibly improve culture, operations, or value creation?
  • Is there clear accountability for delivering this outcome?

Once initial OKRs are drafted, the leadership team determines which Objectives deserve enterprise focus now. Strategy execution fails when too many priorities are pursued simultaneously.

The Less is More Rule: No more than 4 Enterprise Objectives. No more than 3 Key Results per Objective. This constraint forces explicit trade-offs and concentrates execution energy on what matters most.

The Prioritisation Matrix evaluates each initiative against two dimensions: Impact — the strategic value delivered — and Effort — the time, resources, coordination, and change required. This structured approach helps distinguish initiatives that accelerate strategic movement from those that consume resources without meaningful progress.
