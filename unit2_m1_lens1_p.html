Section 1.1 · Awareness — What

What is Strategy?

Interrogative Anchor · What is strategy? What is strategic intent? What is Success in Practice?

1.1.1 — Strategy Master Definition
7 Concepts

Strategy is frequently used but rarely precisely defined. In the S2R® framework it has a specific applied definition — every word carries strategic weight.

"Strategy is the output of iterative thinking, logic and process to determine what, why, how and when an organisation creates, delivers and sustains value to its stakeholders while navigating the forces within its operating environment."

4 Dimensions

1.1.3 — Success in Practice (SiP): From Intent to Future Reality
2 Forms · 4 Dimensions

The shift: from planning to experiencing. From "What are we going to do?" to "If this works, what will it look like in reality?" The team is positioned inside the future, not outside it.

The 3W1H must show up in lived form across four observable domains:

WHAT →

How the value is experienced by customers

WHY →

Why stakeholders trust, choose, and advocate for you

HOW →

How the organisation actually operates (rhythm, coordination, capability)

WHEN →

The pace, sequencing, and responsiveness visible in execution

"If our strategic intent is sound and our strategy works — what will the organisation actually look like, feel like, and perform like in practice?"

Headline — Strategic Vision Anchor

One sentence capturing the strategic destination. Leaders repeat it in meetings, town halls, and board discussions. Signals the destination without describing the route.

Storyline — Strategic Narrative

A narrative of how the organisation operates when success has been achieved, across four observable future-reality dimensions. Leaders describe an operating reality they intend to build.

Section 1.2 · Intelligence — Why

Why Strategic Intent Must Precede Success Definition

Ignition Point · Why must intent be clear before success can be described? Why must both be co-created?

1.2.1 — Why Intent and SiP Must Be Co-Created
3 Irreplaceable Results

The Strategic Intent Statement and SiP are not written by one executive and distributed. They are co-created by the leadership team. This is not a procedural preference — it is a strategic requirement.

  • Leaders agree on the destination — not through compliance, but through genuine shared construction.
  • Trade-offs become visible early — the tensions between functions surface in the room, not in execution.
  • Collective ownership is built — the only ownership that sustains during execution pressure.

Awareness · What

Performance Gravity — and the KISS Navigation Disciplines

Understanding the force that pulls organisations back toward average — and the four precise decisions that counter it.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Understand Performance Gravity as the consequence of unnavigated force movements — not an internal leadership failure
  • Distinguish the three mechanisms of Performance Gravity and their force origins
  • Articulate the four KISS disciplines as the navigation response
  • Apply KISS to your own function with honesty and specificity
The Navigation Premise

The forces in your operating environment never stop moving. They moved before your strategy was designed. They are moving now. And they will continue moving regardless of what your performance indicators show, what your strategy says, or what your leadership team has decided.

Sustained performance is not the reward for strong results. It is the outcome of continuous navigation — the deliberate, disciplined practice of reading the forces that shape the environment and adjusting your position in response to their movement.

Performance Gravity is what happens when forces move and the organisation does not move with them. It is not a failure of effort. It is a failure of navigation.

✎ Reflection
The Three Mechanisms of Performance Gravity
Each produced by a specific force

Performance Gravity operates through three distinct mechanisms. Each is the organisational consequence of a specific category of external force going unnavigated. Select each mechanism to explore.

Mechanism 1
Momentum Illusion
The enterprise competes with a playbook the market has already moved past
Force: Competitive + Disruptive
▼

When competitive and disruptive forces shift and the organisation does not track them, it continues running the approach that once produced results — unaware that the landscape has moved. Results remain acceptable in the short term because momentum carries forward. The gap between the playbook and the market widens invisibly until it becomes undeniable — at which point the cost of correction is significantly higher than earlier navigation would have required.

Mechanism 2
Complexity Creep
Structural responses to old conditions accumulate permanently as organisational weight
Force: Macro-Environmental
▼

When macro-environmental forces shift — regulatory frameworks, economic conditions, political dynamics, technological infrastructure — organisations build structural responses to each change. Collectively, across multiple shifts, they create an organisation that is heavier, slower, and more internally focused than its current environment requires. The processes, governance layers, and approval structures built for yesterday remain permanent fixtures even after the conditions that justified them have changed.

Mechanism 3
Execution Regression
The organisation reverts to its historical operating pattern across all mechanisms simultaneously
Force: Internal
▼

Every organisation has a cultural gravity — norms, habits, assumptions, and enterprise behaviour patterns reinforced over years of operating history. This internal gravity continuously pulls the organisation’s behaviour back toward its historical pattern. When internal forces are not actively navigated, Execution Regression sets in: the enterprise reverts to the operating pattern that predates its current strategic direction. Standards quietly return to historical baselines. The gap between what the strategy says and what daily behaviour reflects widens until the two are no longer recognisably connected.

The fourth force category — Social and Environmental forces — produces a distinct fourth consequence when unnavigated: Legitimacy Erosion. Stakeholder expectations shift while enterprise behaviour remains anchored to what once earned approval. Intelligence examines all five forces in depth.

KISS — The Four Navigation Disciplines
Select each to expand

KISS provides the four precise navigation decisions every leadership team must make at the close of every performance cycle. They apply in every industry, in every quarterly review, permanently. Select each discipline to explore and reflect.

K
KEEP
What is working and must be actively protected
▼

The discipline of Keep is the conscious decision to identify, name, and protect the practices, routines, enterprise behaviours, and systems that are producing reliable results. Effective practices are vulnerable — they depend on specific leadership attention, team behaviour, and structural support. When that attention is redirected, the practice degrades without announcement. Keep is the direct antidote to Momentum Illusion.

✎ Reflection
I
IMPROVE
What has shifted and requires recalibration
▼

The discipline of Improve is the honest recognition that something which was working well is beginning to work less well — and that the gap is growing slowly enough to ignore but fast enough to be dangerous. Improve is the discipline of early correction: addressing drift when it is still a signal rather than waiting until it becomes a crisis. Every organisation has practices that were excellent six months ago and are merely adequate today.

✎ Reflection
S
STOP
What is harming performance and must be eliminated
▼

The discipline of Stop is the most difficult and most neglected of the four. Stop requires leaders to identify and eliminate practices, meetings, processes, reporting requirements, and approval layers consuming organisational energy without producing commensurate value. Stop is the direct antidote to Complexity Creep — the accumulation of structural responses to conditions that no longer exist.

✎ Reflection
S
START
What must be introduced to sustain the next level of performance
▼

The discipline of Start is the forward-facing investment that sustained performance requires. Start asks: what is the organisation not yet capable of that the changing force environment will require? It demands that leaders examine emerging strategic requirements and internal capability gaps before the environment makes them urgent. Start is the investment in future fitness that current performance makes possible.

Why the Five Forces Produce Performance Gravity When Unnavigated

The causal relationship between force movement and performance decline — across industries, in daily execution, permanently.

◆ Learning Objectives
  • Understand each of the five force categories as a permanent feature of every organisation’s operating environment
  • Trace the precise causal link between each force category and the Performance Gravity mechanism it produces
  • Recognise Success Inertia as the total navigation failure — all five forces moving simultaneously without response
  • Identify which force category is most active in your organisation right now
The Five Forces — Causal Analysis
Select each force to explore

Each of the five force categories is a permanent feature of the environment in which every organisation operates. None can be managed away. Each produces a specific and predictable consequence when organisations stop navigating it.

Competitive + Disruptive Forces → Momentum Illusion

In daily execution, these forces move in the background of every commercial decision. A competitor repositions. A new entrant captures a segment considered low-value. A technology application begins solving the same customer problem differently. None of these movements appear as crises on the day they begin. They appear as gradual shifts: win rates that move slightly, customer acquisition costs that increase incrementally, renewal conversations that feel subtly different.

Daily Signal

Gradual shifts in win rates. Customers referencing alternatives that did not exist in last year’s review. Competitors responding faster to market changes than your organisation can match.

When unnavigated produces: Momentum Illusion. The organisation is running a playbook that once produced results in a competitive landscape that has since moved. The results still appear acceptable. The playbook is already obsolete.

Macro-Environmental Forces → Complexity Creep

Macro-environmental forces — economic cycles, regulatory frameworks, political dynamics, technological infrastructure — are experienced as background conditions that constrain or enable every operational decision. Their characteristic danger is accumulation. A regulation tightened three years ago, another two years ago, a third last year — none individually disruptive, together a fundamentally different operating constraint. Organisations adapt to each change incrementally, building structural responses each time, without examining the cumulative weight of all responses together.

Daily Signal

Processes and governance layers that no one can explain the current purpose of. Approval requirements that pre-date three regulatory cycles. Budget structures built for economic conditions that no longer exist.

When unnavigated produces: Complexity Creep. Old structural responses to old conditions accumulate permanently as organisational weight. The macro environment has changed. The structures have not.

Social + Environmental Forces → Legitimacy Erosion

Social and environmental forces operate simultaneously across every stakeholder relationship the organisation maintains. Customer expectations about how organisations behave evolve continuously. Employee expectations about purpose and fairness shift generationally. Investor expectations about governance and environmental impact have moved from peripheral to central in most sectors. The critical feature: these forces operate through perception and legitimacy rather than operational constraint. An organisation can be commercially strong while its legitimacy is eroding — because stakeholder expectations have moved and enterprise behaviour has not.

Daily Signal

Recruitment conversations where candidates ask questions about values and practices not asked three years ago. Customers raising expectations in account reviews that feel different from previous cycles. Investor questions about governance that are more pointed than historical norms.

When unnavigated produces: Legitimacy Erosion. Enterprise behaviour remains anchored to what once earned stakeholder approval while expectations have moved. The organisation is not behaving badly by its own historical standards. It is simply no longer meeting the current standards of its stakeholder universe.

Internal Forces → Execution Regression

Every organisation has a cultural gravity — norms, habits, assumptions, and enterprise behaviour patterns reinforced over years of operating history. This internal gravity does not pause when a new strategy is set or when strong results are achieved. It continuously pulls the organisation’s behaviour back toward its historical pattern. Internal forces must be read in the daily observable behaviour of the enterprise — in how decisions are actually made versus how the governance structure says they should be made, in which conversations happen in the room and which happen afterwards.

Daily Signal

The gap between what the strategy says and what daily decisions and enterprise behaviours actually reflect. Review conversations that are operationally busy but strategically vague. Accountability standards applied unevenly across the organisation.

When unnavigated produces: Execution Regression — and uniquely amplifies all three other mechanisms simultaneously. An organisation whose internal forces are reasserting has no capacity to navigate external forces.

All Five Unnavigated → Success Inertia

Success Inertia is the total navigation failure. It is the state in which all five force categories have moved and the organisation is still positioned for the environment that existed when its results were strong.

Success Inertia operates through a deeply human mechanism. When an approach produces strong results, the people who executed it develop a natural conviction in its continued validity. This conviction is not irrational. But it is dangerous, because the environment that rewarded the approach has already changed.

Every organisation that has achieved something significant and then lost it followed this pattern. Not through a single catastrophic failure — but through the quiet accumulation of five unnavigated force movements, each individually dismissible, collectively devastating.

Part 2 — ABCV as the Navigation Diagnostic
HOW TO READ what force movement means

When the Alignment Brigade detects force movement in your domain, ABCV reveals what that movement means for your strategic position. Introduced earlier in this programme as the OKR integrity stress-test, ABCV reaches its full strategic purpose here: four questions applied to any force movement, each with a Must-Be-True condition that identifies precisely when a navigation response is required.

For each force movement detected: run the four ABCV questions. When an MBT condition can no longer be confirmed as true — that is the navigation signal. That is when KISS is activated.

A — Arena
Has this force shifted the customer end-game we are competing for?
▼
Strategic Logic
Strategy starts with a clear definition of the arena — the specific customer need the organisation is competing to serve. Force movements shift the arena itself: the customer’s problem evolves, the job they need done changes.
Must-Be-True Condition

What must remain true about the relevance of the customer problem we are solving for our current approach to remain the right approach?

B — Boundaries
Has this force created new constraints on our ability to deliver?
▼
Strategic Logic
Boundaries are the internal and external constraints within which the organisation must operate. Macro-environmental forces move these boundaries continuously. Internal forces also create boundaries through enterprise behaviour patterns that constrain what people actually do.
Must-Be-True Condition

What must remain true about our operational and regulatory environment for our current delivery model to continue functioning within tolerance?

C — Competition
Has this force changed who we are actually competing against?
▼
Strategic Logic
Competition is defined by the customer’s choice set, not by the organisation’s view of its industry peers. When Competitive and Disruptive forces move, the set of alternatives available to the customer changes.
Must-Be-True Condition

What must remain true about our specific advantage relative to every alternative the customer could now choose?

V — Value Proposition
Has this force eroded our distinctive advantage?
▼
Strategic Logic
The Value Proposition is the distinctive edge that defines the organisation’s position. Social and Environmental forces move this constantly — what was distinctive practice becomes expected standard.
Must-Be-True Condition

What must remain true about the distinctiveness of our advantage for our current positioning to remain credible to the stakeholders whose choice sustains our performance?

Scenario 1 · Internal · Execution Regression
The COO’s Execution Rhythm
Domain: COO — Execution Cadence

A manufacturing organisation delivered three consecutive strong quarters. The COO’s execution rhythm was rigorous: weekly cross-functional reviews, disciplined escalation protocols, structured performance dashboards. Four months later the results are still acceptable — but the rhythm has quietly changed. Weekly reviews have moved to fortnightly. Dashboard discipline has softened. Issues that would previously have been escalated within 24 hours now sit with teams for a week. No one has formally decided to relax the standards. The internal force — the organisation’s historical habit of less rigorous review cadences — has quietly reasserted. Enterprise behaviour has begun reverting to the pattern that predated the performance improvement.

ABCV Diagnostic — MBT Condition Under Threat
B — Boundaries: Internal enterprise behaviour patterns are constraining execution quality. The operational standard the strategy requires can no longer be confirmed as the standard the organisation is actually maintaining.
Must-Be-True Condition
What must remain true about our internal enterprise behaviour and execution standards for our current performance level to continue?

Scenario 2 · Macro-Environmental · Complexity Creep
The CFO’s Legacy Budget
Domain: CFO — Capital Discipline

A financial services organisation completed a rigorous zero-based budgeting exercise eighteen months ago. Since then, two macro-environmental shifts have occurred: a significant regulatory change that created new compliance requirements, and an economic cycle shift that changed the cost of capital. Neither has been formally integrated into the budget structure. The regulatory compliance costs have been absorbed additively. The investment assumptions have not been revisited. The budget still reflects the macro environment of eighteen months ago. Strategic initiatives are underfunded. Legacy cost structures have quietly re-established their original funding levels.

ABCV Diagnostic — MBT Condition Under Threat
B — Boundaries: Macro-environmental forces have created new operational and regulatory constraints that the budget structure was not built for. Old structural responses to old conditions are accumulating as weight.
Must-Be-True Condition
What must remain true about the macro-environmental conditions for our current capital allocation to continue supporting our strategic priorities?

Scenario 3 · Competitive + Disruptive · Momentum Illusion
The CMO’s Comfortable Intelligence
Domain: CMO — Market Intelligence

A retail organisation’s marketing function produced exceptional results during a strong strategic period. The customer intelligence underpinning this performance was built twenty months ago. Since then, two new entrants have positioned in segments previously considered low-priority, and a technology-enabled alternative has emerged that solves a significant portion of the customer’s problem at a lower price point. The marketing team is executing the existing strategy with discipline. But the foundational customer intelligence — who the customer is, what they are trying to achieve, and who else is competing for that achievement — has not been meaningfully updated.

ABCV Diagnostic — MBT Condition Under Threat
A — Arena: The customer end-game may have shifted. C — Competition: The alternatives the customer now has include entrants and technology solutions that were not present when the strategy was built.
Must-Be-True Condition
What must remain true about the customer problem we are solving and the alternatives available for our current marketing approach to continue producing the results it achieved when built?

Scenario 4 · Social + Environmental · Legitimacy Erosion
The CRO’s Legitimacy Gap
Domain: CRO — Risk Intelligence

A professional services organisation has maintained what is widely regarded as exemplary risk governance. The risk register is comprehensive, current, and consistently well-reviewed. However, social and environmental forces have moved significantly. Institutional investors — who represent a material portion of the client base — have shifted their expectations about risk governance from compliance demonstration to strategic integration. They now expect risk intelligence to be visibly embedded in strategic decision-making. The CRO’s approach was built for a stakeholder environment in which compliance demonstration earned approval. That environment has moved. The value proposition of the risk function is eroding not because the function has declined but because what stakeholders now value has advanced.

ABCV Diagnostic — MBT Condition Under Threat
V — Value Proposition: Social and environmental force movements have shifted what stakeholders perceive as valuable risk governance. The current approach no longer earns the approval it once earned from the stakeholder universe that now matters most.
Must-Be-True Condition
What must remain true about what our stakeholders consider credible and valuable risk governance for our current approach to continue earning their confidence?
